## 1. Which agency regulates the transport of hazardous materials in the United States?

A) EPA  
B) DOT  
C) OSHA  
D) NRC  

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** B) DOT  
**Explanation:**  
The Department of Transportation (DOT) regulates the transportation of hazardous materials (49 CFR). EPA handles waste, OSHA covers worker safety, NRC covers radioactive material, but only DOT regulates all hazard transport.
</details>

## 2. According to RCRA, a “hazardous waste” is…

A) Any material that can burn  
B) Any listed or characteristic waste discarded that can pose a threat  
C) Any chemical with a Safety Data Sheet  
D) Any radioactive substance

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** B) Any listed or characteristic waste discarded that can pose a threat  
**Explanation:**  
EPA’s RCRA program applies both to “listed” wastes and to “characteristic” (IRTC) wastes. The waste must also be “discarded” to count.
</details>

## 3. A chemical has a vapor density of 2.5. If there’s a leak in a basement, what’s the biggest danger?

A) Vapors will rise and escape outdoors  
B) Vapors will accumulate near the ceiling  
C) Vapors will sink and accumulate in low spots  
D) No risk—vapor density is irrelevant

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** C) Vapors will sink and accumulate in low spots  
**Explanation:**  
Vapor density >1 means heavier than air; such gases/vapors pool in low areas creating serious confined space hazards.
</details>

## 5. OSHA’s GHS label “Signal Word” is either:

A) “Danger” or “Warning”  
B) “Poison” or “Warning”  
C) “Fatal” or “Hazard”  
D) “Danger” or “Safe”

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** A) “Danger” or “Warning”  
**Explanation:**  
GHS containers use only “Danger” (greater risk) and “Warning” (lesser) as signal words. Poison is not used as a general signal word in the GHS.
</details>

## 6. A material has a pH = 13.1. How would RCRA code it?

A) D001 — Ignitability  
B) D002 — Corrosivity  
C) D003 — Reactivity  
D) D004 — Toxicity

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** B) D002 — Corrosivity  
**Explanation:**  
Corrosive wastes under RCRA are aqueous pH ≤2 or ≥12.5, or aqueous liquids that corrode steel fast (>6.35mm/yr). So pH 13.1 is D002.
</details>

## 8. What is the “Lower Explosive Limit” (LEL)?

A) The maximum % vapor for ignition  
B) The minimum % vapor/air mixture that can ignite  
C) The minimum temperature for ignition  
D) The lowest pressure a chemical can exert

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** B) The minimum % vapor/air mixture that can ignite  
**Explanation:**

## 10. Which HAZMAT property is defined as “spontaneous ignition below 130°F (54°C), no spark required”?

A) Flammable  
B) Pyrophoric  
C) Water-reactive  
D) Explosive

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** B) Pyrophoric  
**Explanation:**  
Pyrophoric substances ignite spontaneously in air below ~130°F without a source of ignition (e.g., white phosphorus).
</details>

## 12. What would be the PRIMARY regulatory concern with storing strong acids and strong bases together?

A) Toxic vapor release  
B) Water pollution  
C) Violent reaction and heat release  
D) Increased volatility

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** C) Violent reaction and heat release  
**Explanation:**  
Mixing acids and bases leads to neutralization, which releases a large amount of heat and can cause splattering (exothermic reaction hazard).
</details>

## 13. Which of the following is a “reportable quantity” spill for CERCLA/EPA?

A) Any amount of diesel  
B) Release reaching or exceeding the “RQ” for that substance  
C) Release above the PEL  
D) Any chemical release inside a shed

<details>
<summary>Show Answer</summary>

**Correct answer:** B) Release reaching or exceeding the “RQ” for that substance  
**Explanation:**  
The EPA requires any release at or above the set Reportable Quantity (RQ) for that chemical be reported to the NRC within 24h.
</details>
