# 🏗️ PART 1 — EPCRA: THE "YOUR NEIGHBORS HAVE A RIGHT TO KNOW" LAW

---

## 🌍 Why EPCRA Exists — The Bhopal Disaster

> 🕛 **December 3, 1984. 12:30 AM. Bhopal, India.**

While 500,000 people slept in the city surrounding a pesticide plant, a storage tank began to overheat. Inside: 40 tons of **methyl isocyanate (MIC)** — a chemical so reactive it was manufactured on-site and used immediately during normal operations. Tonight, something went catastrophically wrong.

Within minutes, a massive white cloud of MIC gas rolled through the streets. The community had **no warning system**, **no evacuation plan**, **no shelters**. Hospitals didn't know what the chemical was. Emergency responders had no protective equipment rated for it. People woke up choking, eyes burning, unable to breathe.

**Estimated deaths: 3,800–16,000. Over 500,000 people exposed.**

The people of Bhopal had no idea this chemical existed next door — in quantities large enough to kill a city.

**The United States watched Bhopal and made a law.**

In 1986, Congress passed the **Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA)** as Title III of SARA. The core idea is simple: communities have the **RIGHT to know** what hazardous chemicals sit near them, and **local governments must plan** for worst-case scenarios.

> 💡 **Real-World Connection:**
> Any industrial facility that stores significant quantities of hazardous chemicals — solvents, acids, oxidizers, cryogenic gases, reactive materials — must report these inventories annually, maintain emergency coordinators, and coordinate with local fire departments and LEPCs. When emergency responders arrive at a facility at 3 AM because something went wrong, they should NOT be walking in blind. EPCRA ensures they don't.

---

## 📋 EPCRA — The Four Sections You Must Know Cold

EPCRA contains Sections 301–330. Four sections dominate the CHMM exam and real-world compliance:

| Section Range | Name | One-Line Summary |
|---|---|---|
| **301–303** | Emergency Planning | Build the local planning structure + notify if you have EHS chemicals |
| **304** | Emergency Release Notification | Something just released — call NOW |
| **311–312** | Chemical Inventory Reporting | Annual audit of what you store |
| **313** | Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) | Annual public accountability for routine releases |

## 🚨 SECTIONS 301–303 — Emergency Planning: Building the Safety Net

### The Three-Layer Planning System

EPCRA created a national-to-local planning pyramid:

```
FEDERAL (EPA)
    ↓  Guidance and oversight
SERC — State Emergency Response Commission
    ↓  Coordinates and oversees
LEPC — Local Emergency Planning Committee
    ↓  Actual local emergency planning
LOCAL FACILITY (YOU)
    ↓  Provides information and coordinates
```

---

### 🏛️ SERC — State Emergency Response Commission

Every state must establish a **SERC** under EPCRA Section 301.

**SERC responsibilities:**
- Designate Local Emergency Planning Districts
- Appoint and supervise LEPCs
- Review local emergency plans
- Receive Tier II reports from facilities
- Establish procedures for public information requests

> 💡 **Real-World Example:**
> In Texas, the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) fulfills the SERC function. In California, the California Governor's Office of Emergency Services (Cal OES) handles SERC duties. The name changes by state; the role is identical.

---

### 🏘️ LEPC — Local Emergency Planning Committee

The LEPC is where emergency planning **actually happens** — at the community level, where real people live near real facilities.

**Required LEPC membership (all nine must be included):**

| # | Member Type | Why They're There |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Elected state and local officials | Authority and accountability |
| 2 | Law enforcement | Scene security and traffic control |
| 3 | Civil defense / emergency management | Overall coordination |
| 4 | **Firefighting** | First responders for chemical incidents |
| 5 | First aid / EMS | Medical treatment of victims |
| 6 | Health officials | Public health guidance |
| 7 | Local environmental and transportation officials | Spill response, road closures |
| 8 | **Broadcast and print media** | Mass public notification ← *always tested* |
| 9 | **Facility owners/operators** | Source of chemical information |

> 💡 **Why media is included — The 2 AM Problem:**
> Imagine a chlorine gas cloud moving toward a suburb at 2:47 AM. You need to reach 80,000 sleeping people in minutes. There's no app, no text system, nothing faster than local radio and TV cutting in with an emergency broadcast. EPCRA's drafters understood this — broadcast and print media are **required** LEPC members, not optional guests. In a real chlorine release, the LEPC's media member calls the station to push shelter-in-place instructions to every radio in a 10-mile radius. Low-tech, but life-saving.

**LEPC responsibilities:**
- Develop and maintain the local emergency response plan
- Review and update the plan **at least annually**
- Receive and process facility emergency notifications
- Receive Tier II chemical inventory reports
- Make chemical inventory information available to the public on request
- Conduct exercises and drills

> ⚠️ **Exam Fact:** LEPC plans must include: identification of EHS facilities, emergency response procedures, designation of community and facility emergency coordinators, emergency equipment inventory, evacuation plans, and a training program.

---

### 🏭 Section 302 — EHS Notification: "You Have Something Dangerous. They Need to Know."

**Section 302 is triggered when a facility stores an Extremely Hazardous Substance (EHS) at or above its Threshold Planning Quantity (TPQ).**

EPA publishes a list of approximately **356 EHS chemicals** — each with a TPQ (the storage amount that triggers planning notification) and an RQ (the release amount that triggers emergency notification).

**TPQ examples — memorize these:**

| Chemical | TPQ | Why So Low? |
|---|---|---|
| Chlorine (Cl₂) | 100 lbs | Dense toxic gas, community-wide impact |
| Ammonia (anhydrous) | 500 lbs | Toxic, explosive range, refrigeration risk |
| Sulfur dioxide | 500 lbs | Toxic gas, industrial acid rain precursor |
| Methyl isocyanate (MIC) | 500 lbs | The Bhopal chemical — need we say more? |
| Hydrogen fluoride (HF) | 100 lbs | Penetrates skin, attacks bones |
| **Phosgene (COCl₂)** | **10 lbs** | WWI chemical weapon — deceptively pleasant smell |

> 💡 **The Phosgene Story:**
> World War I. French and British troops in the trenches notice a faint smell — "freshly cut hay," some say. It's pleasant. They don't flee. Within 24–48 hours, their lungs fill with fluid. Phosgene was responsible for an estimated **85% of chemical warfare deaths** in WWI — not mustard gas, which people remember. The danger is that phosgene smells *nice*. By the time you think "what's that pleasant smell?" your lungs are already damaged. Industrial uses today include polymer and pharmaceutical manufacturing. The 10-lb TPQ means even a small cylinder triggers full LEPC notification. The CHMM exam loves testing extreme TPQ values like this.

**Facility obligation under Section 302:**
When you first have an EHS at or above its TPQ:
1. Notify the SERC and LEPC **in writing** within 60 days
2. Designate a **Facility Emergency Coordinator**
3. Cooperate with the LEPC in developing the local emergency plan

> 📖 **Scenario — The Chlorine Tanker:**
> A metal finishing plant receives its first delivery of liquid chlorine for an electroplating bath. The delivery: one rail car holding 90 tons = **180,000 lbs**. Chlorine's TPQ = 100 lbs. The plant is now storing **1,800× the TPQ**.
>
> Obligations triggered: Notify SERC and LEPC within 60 days. Designate a facility emergency coordinator. The LEPC now knows chlorine is on-site. The fire department receives information on chlorine's properties: vapor density 2.47 (heavier than air — it hugs the ground and flows into basements), the evacuation radius for a worst-case release, and the fact that you should **never use water fog in a confined area with chlorine** (it creates hydrochloric acid mist). A year from now, if a valve fails at 3 AM, firefighters will not arrive uninformed.

## 🆘 SECTION 304 — Emergency Release Notification: "Call Now. Explain Later."

**Section 304 activates the moment a reportable quantity of an EHS or CERCLA hazardous substance is released to the environment.**

This is the emergency notification provision. There is no time for memos, emails, or internal approvals. When something escapes, the phone calls start immediately.

### Who Gets Called and When:

| Notification | Who | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| LEPC of the affected area | Community emergency coordinator | EPCRA Section 304 |
| SERC of the affected state | State emergency planning | EPCRA Section 304 |
| **National Response Center: 1-800-424-8802** | Federal government | CERCLA Section 103 |

> ⚠️ **Exam Critical:** The NRC call is technically a CERCLA Section 103 requirement, not EPCRA Section 304. But all three notifications happen simultaneously in a real emergency. The exam may ask which notification goes where. **NRC = federal (CERCLA). LEPC/SERC = community (EPCRA 304).**

### The 9 Things You Must Communicate Immediately:

1. Chemical name or identity
2. Whether it is an EHS
3. Estimated quantity released
4. Time and duration of the release
5. Medium released into (air, water, soil)
6. Known or anticipated health risks
7. Medical advice if needed
8. Proper precautions (evacuate? shelter-in-place?)
9. Name and phone number of contact person

> 📖 **Scenario — The 3 AM Valve Failure:**
> A valve fails at a chemical storage facility at 3:12 AM. Chlorine gas begins releasing from a 90-ton railcar. The on-call supervisor grabs the emergency contact list:
>
> - **3:13 AM:** Calls the LEPC emergency coordinator: *"Chlorine release in progress. Estimating 50–100 lbs released so far. Wind out of the northwest at 8 mph. Recommend shelter-in-place for residents within 1 mile northeast."*
> - **3:14 AM:** Calls the SERC hotline.
> - **3:14 AM:** Calls NRC at 1-800-424-8802.
>
> Notice the calls go out **before knowing the exact quantity released**. Section 304 requires notification "as soon as the person in charge has knowledge of the release." **"We don't know how much yet"** is not a defense for waiting. Call first. Quantify later. The written follow-up report can fill in the exact numbers.

### Written Follow-Up Report:

After the initial oral notification, EPCRA Section 304 requires a **written follow-up** to the LEPC and SERC as soon as practicable, including:
- Actions taken to respond and contain the release
- Known or anticipated health risks
- Medical advice
- Whether compensation may be available to affected persons

> 💡 **The Transportation Exception:**
> A tanker truck carrying anhydrous ammonia crashes on a highway and releases 600 lbs (above the 500-lb RQ). Who calls whom?
>
> Answer: The **carrier notifies the NRC** directly. There is no "facility LEPC" to call because there's no facility involved — it happened on a public road. The CHMM exam loves this scenario. In a transportation release, the carrier bears the notification responsibility to NRC. Local first responders handle the rest through 911.

### Exemptions from Section 304 Notification:

- **Federally permitted releases** (authorized by NPDES, Clean Air Act permit, RCRA permit)
- **No exposure outside the facility boundary** — *narrow exception; if vapors reach a public road at the fence line, this does NOT apply*
- **Pesticides applied per FIFRA label** (agricultural operations)
- **Normal agricultural operations**

> ⚠️ **Exam Trap — The "No Exposure" Exception is Narrow:** If your facility has a public road running along the fence line, even a small vapor cloud reaching that road potentially exposes the public. The exception requires truly zero exposure outside the boundary — not just "probably no one was hurt."

## 📊 SECTIONS 311–312 — Chemical Inventory Reporting: The Annual Audit

These sections require facilities to tell the government **what chemicals they have** and **in what quantities** — before an emergency happens, so responders can plan.

### Section 311 — SDS Submission: "Arm the Fire Department"

**Who must comply:** Facilities with OSHA HazCom chemicals above threshold (10,000 lbs general, or any EHS above its TPQ)

**What to submit:** A copy of each chemical's SDS (or a chemical list) to: LEPC, SERC, and the **local fire department**

**When:** Within 3 months of first having the chemical above threshold; again when new chemicals arrive above threshold

> 📖 **Scenario — The Warehouse Fire:**
> Engine 12 gets a call at 2 AM: a warehouse is on fire. On the way, the captain pulls up the facility's Section 311 SDS file on the department's laptop. He sees: 15,000 lbs of methanol (flammable, water-soluble), 5,000 lbs of glacial acetic acid (corrosive, flammable), and 800 lbs of sodium hypochlorite solution (oxidizer — do NOT mix with the acetic acid). He radios the crew: *"Watch for two separate storage areas — do not let the acid and hypochlorite mix. Water application to the methanol fire is acceptable."*
>
> That briefing happened because of Section 311. Without the SDS file, the captain walks his crew into a potentially reactive chemical situation with zero information.

---

### Section 312 — Tier I and Tier II Reports: The Annual Chemical Census

**Due every year by March 1** for the prior calendar year.

**Two tiers — but one dominates practice:**

| Feature | Tier I | Tier II |
|---|---|---|
| Detail level | Aggregate by hazard category | Chemical-by-chemical detail |
| What's reported | Total quantities by category | Each chemical: name, CAS#, max/avg amounts, storage locations, container types |
| Who receives it | LEPC, SERC, local fire department | LEPC, SERC, local fire department |
| Public access | Limited | **Full public access on request** |
| Real-world use | Essentially obsolete | **Standard — virtually all facilities file Tier II** |

> ⚠️ **In Practice:** Tier I is a relic. If you ever see "Tier I" on the CHMM exam, know what it is conceptually. But in the field, everyone files **Tier II**.

**Tier II thresholds:**
- EHS chemicals: Above the TPQ **or 500 lbs** — whichever is **less**
- All other OSHA HazCom chemicals: Above **10,000 lbs**

**Each chemical on Tier II requires:**
- Chemical name and CAS number
- Maximum amount stored at any point during the year (in coded ranges)
- Average amount stored
- Number of days on-site
- Storage locations (building, area, lot)
- Storage container types (aboveground tank, underground tank, cylinders, drums, etc.)
- Whether trade secret is being claimed

> 📖 **Scenario — The February 28th Scramble:**
> It's February 28. The environmental manager at an industrial plant is finalizing Tier II for the prior year. She reviews the spreadsheet:
>
> - Sulfuric acid: 12,000 lbs peak (EHS, TPQ = 1,000 lbs) ✅ **must report**
> - Waste solvent (flammable): 50,000 lbs peak (non-EHS, but above 10,000 lbs) ✅ **must report**
> - Sodium cyanide: 200 lbs peak (EHS, TPQ = 100 lbs) ← She almost skips this one
>
> Stop. **200 lbs > 100-lb TPQ = must be reported.** Missing a single EHS chemical because "it's only a small amount" is precisely the kind of compliance gap that triggers EPA enforcement actions. The rule isn't about how dangerous you think it is — it's about the threshold.

> ⚠️ **Trade Secret Claims:** Facilities can withhold the specific chemical identity as trade secret — but must still report: that they are withholding a chemical identity, the hazard category it belongs to, and the approximate quantities. **Trade secret is not a reporting exemption. It is a name-withholding provision only.**

## 📈 SECTION 313 — TOXICS RELEASE INVENTORY (TRI): The Public Accountability Report

**Section 313 is fundamentally different from the rest of EPCRA.**

Sections 301–312 are about emergency planning and response. Section 313 is about **public accountability for routine pollution** — whether an emergency happened or not.

### What TRI Is:

TRI requires covered facilities to **annually report the total quantities of listed toxic chemicals released to the environment** — to air, water, land, or through underground injection — AND the quantities transferred off-site.

**This includes legally permitted routine releases.** Every pound of a listed chemical released — intentionally or accidentally, legally or otherwise — must be counted.

> 💡 **The "Pollution Scorecard" That Changed Corporate Behavior:**
> When TRI data became public in 1988, something unexpected happened. Companies that were 100% legally compliant suddenly saw their names at the top of "worst polluters" lists in local newspapers. The data was accurate — they were releasing chemicals legally, under permit — but the public didn't care about the permits. They cared about the pounds.
>
> Within a few years, many major manufacturers voluntarily reduced TRI releases by 30–50% — not because of new regulations, but because of **public embarrassment**. TRI created accountability that permits alone never could. This is the law working exactly as intended.

### Who Must File — All Three Criteria Must Apply:

| Criterion | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Industry** | Covered SIC/NAICS code (manufacturing, mining, electric utilities, chemicals, federal facilities, etc.) |
| **Employees** | 10 or more full-time equivalent employees |
| **Chemical Threshold** | Manufactures/processes ≥ 25,000 lbs/year **OR** otherwise uses ≥ 10,000 lbs/year |

> ⚠️ **Critical Distinction — Three Activity Types:**
>
> | Activity | Threshold | Example |
> |---|---|---|
> | **Manufactures** | ≥ 25,000 lbs | Produces the chemical, including as a byproduct |
> | **Processes** | ≥ 25,000 lbs | Incorporates the chemical into a product |
> | **Otherwise Uses** | ≥ 10,000 lbs | Uses it in production but it doesn't end up in the product (e.g., solvent for cleaning) |
>
> The "otherwise uses" threshold is LOWER. A company that uses trichloroethylene (TCE) as a degreaser — not in their product, just to clean parts — hits the reporting threshold at 10,000 lbs, not 25,000 lbs. The exam tests this distinction constantly.

### Special Low Thresholds (PBT Chemicals):

Persistent, Bioaccumulative, and Toxic (PBT) chemicals have much lower reporting thresholds:
- **Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds:** 0.1 grams (not pounds — *grams*)
- **Lead and lead compounds:** 100 lbs
- **Mercury and mercury compounds:** 10 lbs
- **PCBs:** 10 lbs

> 💡 **Why Dioxins Get a 0.1 Gram Threshold:**
> Dioxins don't degrade. They accumulate in fat tissue. They travel up the food chain — from contaminated sediment to insects to fish to humans. The dose that causes reproductive harm in animals is measured in parts per trillion. A 0.1-gram reporting threshold isn't bureaucratic overreach — it reflects genuine toxicological severity. Any facility generating even trace dioxin as a combustion byproduct must file Form R.

### The TRI Chemical List:

Over **800 individual chemicals and categories**, including:
Lead, mercury, benzene, trichloroethylene (TCE), PCBs, dioxins, arsenic, formaldehyde, methylene chloride, and hundreds more.

### Form R — The Annual TRI Report:

**Due: July 1 each year** (for the prior calendar year) — filed electronically via EPA's TRI-MEweb system

Form R captures:
- Chemical name and CAS number
- Maximum amount on-site during the year
- Releases to each environmental medium: air (stack/fugitive), water (surface/POTW), land, underground injection
- Quantities transferred off-site (to recyclers, treatment facilities, disposal facilities, POTW)
- Pollution prevention and waste management activities
- Source reduction initiatives

> ⚠️ **"Release" vs. "Off-Site Transfer" — A Key Distinction:**
> Sending hazardous waste to a licensed Treatment, Storage, and Disposal Facility (TSDF) for treatment is an **off-site transfer**, not an environmental release. It does NOT count as releasing the chemical to the environment on your Form R. However — the TSDF receiving the waste has **its own TRI reporting obligations** for what it processes. TRI tracks the chemical through the entire treatment chain.

### Form A — The Simplified Alternative:

Available if the facility:
- Manufactures, processes, or uses the chemical at or below **1 million lbs/year**
- Has annual reportable releases below **500 lbs**
- The chemical is **not a PBT chemical**

Form A is a simple certification — "our releases are minimal" — with far less detail than Form R.

## What is SARA?

**SARA = Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act of 1986**

SARA amended CERCLA (the original 1980 Superfund law) and simultaneously **created EPCRA** (as Title III of SARA). It dramatically strengthened the hazardous waste cleanup program, injected billions into the Superfund trust fund, and established community right-to-know.

### SARA's Four Titles — Know Every One:

| Title | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **Title I** | Amendments to CERCLA — strengthened cleanup authority, added enforcement tools, increased funding |
| **Title II** | Created the national asbestos in schools program (AHERA — *Day 3 topic*) |
| **Title III** | **EPCRA** — Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know (everything in Part 1 above) |
| **Title IV** | Radon gas and indoor air quality |

> ⚠️ **Exam Note — SARA III = EPCRA:** When the exam says "SARA Title III," it means EPCRA. "SARA Title III Section 313" = "EPCRA Section 313." These terms are **completely interchangeable**. Don't let the dual naming confuse you.

# ☠️ PART 3 — CERCLA: THE SUPERFUND LAW
## "Who Pays to Clean Up the Mess? EVERYONE. Forever."

---

## The Origin Story — Love Canal

> 🏘️ **Niagara Falls, New York. 1978.**

In the 1940s and 1950s, the Hooker Chemical Company dumped approximately **21,000 tons** of chemical waste into an old canal near Niagara Falls, New York. They lined it, capped it, and then sold the land to the Niagara Falls School Board for **$1** — with a liability disclaimer in the deed that essentially said "we're not responsible for what's buried here."

A school was built on top. Homes were developed around it.

By the mid-1970s, residents were noticing:
- Basement floors seeping black chemical sludge
- Children with chemical burns from playing in their own backyards
- Gardens where nothing would grow
- Unusually high rates of cancer, miscarriages, and birth defects

In 1978, President Carter declared a **federal health emergency** and evacuated 800 families. The cleanup cost hundreds of millions of dollars over decades.

**Congress responded with CERCLA in 1980.**

> 💡 **The Lesson That Created the Law:**
> Hooker Chemical believed that selling the land and including a liability disclaimer in the deed meant their legal exposure was finished. Courts disagreed, and Congress made it permanent with CERCLA. Love Canal became the defining example of why CERCLA's liability rules are so aggressive: because "we sold it, it's not our problem anymore" thinking had literally poisoned children in their own homes.

---

## What CERCLA Does — Five Core Functions:

1. **Established the Superfund Trust Fund** — money to pay for emergency cleanup of abandoned sites when no responsible party can be found or forced to pay immediately
2. **Created the National Priorities List (NPL)** — the ranked list of the nation's worst contaminated sites, eligible for Superfund cleanup funding
3. **Established CERCLA liability** — the legal framework that determines who pays, how much, and with no escape
4. **Required reporting of hazardous substance releases** via the Reportable Quantity (RQ) system
5. **Created the National Contingency Plan (NCP)** — the federal blueprint for how cleanups must be conducted

---

## 💰 CERCLA Liability — The Legal Framework That's Nearly Impossible to Escape

CERCLA liability has three characteristics that make it uniquely powerful — and feared:

### ① STRICT LIABILITY — "You Don't Have to Be Guilty to Be Liable"

You are liable even if you did **nothing wrong**. No negligence required. No violation needed. No bad intent required.

If you are a Potentially Responsible Party (PRP) and your waste ended up at a contaminated site, you are liable for cleanup costs. Period.

> 📖 **Scenario — The Innocent Generator:**
> A pharmaceutical company properly characterized its hazardous waste, hired a licensed and permitted transporter, and shipped it to a licensed RCRA Treatment, Storage, and Disposal Facility (TSDF) — following every rule perfectly.
>
> Ten years later, the TSDF is discovered to have been secretly disposing of waste illegally in an unlined pit behind the facility. The site is contaminated. The TSDF is bankrupt and its owners are in federal prison.
>
> CERCLA makes the **pharmaceutical company liable** for their share of cleanup costs — even though they followed every single rule. This is strict liability. It's why generator due diligence in selecting disposal contractors is not optional — it's essential self-protection.

---

### ② JOINT AND SEVERAL LIABILITY — "You Might Pay for Everyone Else Too"

Each PRP can be held liable for the **entire cleanup cost** — not just their proportional share.

If you sent 1% of the waste to a contaminated site but you're the only financially solvent party remaining, EPA can require **you to pay 100%** of the cleanup.

> 📖 **Scenario — The Deepest Pockets Problem:**
> Three companies sent waste to a contaminated industrial lagoon:
> - **MegaCorp A:** Sent 50% of the waste volume → *filed bankruptcy last year*
> - **MegaCorp B:** Sent 49% of the waste volume → *dissolved after acquisition*
> - **TinyCo C:** Sent 1% of the waste volume → *still operating, has $15 million in assets*
>
> EPA sends a demand letter to TinyCo C for **the full $12 million cleanup cost**.
>
> TinyCo C's lawyer says: "But we sent 1% of the waste!" EPA's lawyer says: "CERCLA Section 107(a). Joint and several. You are liable."
>
> TinyCo C can pay, then try to **sue the estates of Companies A and B** to recover their proportional share. Good luck collecting from bankruptcies.
>
> **The lesson:** Your CERCLA exposure is not determined by how much waste you sent. It's determined by who's still standing with money. Keep meticulous waste manifests — if you can prove you sent only 1%, you can negotiate a **de minimis settlement** with EPA. Without records, you can't prove a thing.

---

### ③ RETROACTIVE LIABILITY — "The Past Is Never Dead"

CERCLA applies **backwards in time**. The law passed in 1980. But waste disposed of **before 1980** — even legally under the laws of that era — still creates CERCLA liability today.

> 📖 **Scenario — The 1963 Defense That Doesn't Exist:**
> A company president says: *"We disposed of that waste in 1963 — it was completely legal at the time. There were no RCRA regulations, no CERCLA, nothing. We did everything right by the standards of the day."*
>
> CERCLA's answer: Doesn't matter. The contamination is in the ground now. The community is at risk now. You contributed to it. You are a PRP now.
>
> Retroactive liability is why old industrial properties are so difficult to sell, and why environmental due diligence (Phase I/II ESAs) exists for every commercial real estate transaction.

---

## 👥 Potentially Responsible Parties (PRPs) — The Four Categories

**CERCLA Section 107(a)** identifies four categories of PRPs — all of whom face strict, joint, and several liability:

### Category 1 — Current Owners and Operators

The current owner or operator of a contaminated facility, **even if they didn't cause the contamination** and even if they had no idea it was there.

> ⚠️ **The Innocent Purchaser Defense (All Appropriate Inquiries):**
> If a buyer performs **"All Appropriate Inquiries" (AAI)** before purchase — specifically a **Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)** and, if Phase I identifies concerns, a **Phase II ESA** — and does not discover contamination, they may qualify as a **Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser** with significantly limited CERCLA liability.
>
> This is why banks require Phase I ESAs before lending on commercial property. It's not bureaucracy. It's CERCLA liability protection.
> - **Phase I ESA (ASTM E1527-21):** Desktop review + site inspection. Identifies Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs). *No sampling.*
> - **Phase II ESA (ASTM E1903):** Soil and groundwater sampling. Confirms and quantifies contamination.

### Category 2 — Past Owners or Operators at the Time of Disposal

Whoever **owned the property** when hazardous waste was disposed of there — even if they sold it 30 years ago.

### Category 3 — Generators (Arrangers)

Companies that **"arranged for"** the disposal or treatment of hazardous substances — meaning the companies that generated the waste and contracted for its disposal, even if they never touched the disposal site directly.

### Category 4 — Transporters

Companies that **transported** hazardous substances to the contaminated site — **if they selected the disposal site**. If the generator told the transporter where to deliver the waste, more liability shifts to the generator.

---

## 🗺️ National Priorities List (NPL) — The Superfund "Worst Of" Roster

The **NPL** is EPA's list of contaminated sites serious enough to qualify for long-term Superfund cleanup funding. Being listed on the NPL is effectively a designation of: *"This site is bad enough that it requires a federally funded, multi-year cleanup."*

### Getting onto the NPL — The HRS Score:

Sites are scored using the **Hazard Ranking System (HRS)** — a numerical model evaluating risk based on:
- Migration pathways: groundwater, surface water, soil, air
- Toxicity and quantity of hazardous substances present
- Human and ecological targets at risk

> ⚠️ **The Magic Number: HRS score ≥ 28.50** → Site is eligible for NPL listing

### NPL by the Numbers:
- Approximately **1,300 sites** currently on the NPL
- Cleanup of a single NPL site can take **10–30+ years**
- Cleanup costs range from millions to hundreds of millions of dollars
- Sites can be **deleted from the NPL** when cleanup goals are achieved

> 📖 **Scenario — From Field to NPL:**
> A former metal plating operation in an industrial district is abandoned. Neighbors report orange-stained water seeping into a creek. EPA conducts a preliminary assessment. The HRS model runs the numbers — groundwater is contaminated with hexavalent chromium and nickel extending 600 feet from the site, reaching a residential well head. HRS score: 41.
>
> The site gets proposed for NPL listing. A 60-day public comment period opens. Community members attend a public meeting. EPA finalizes the NPL listing. Fourteen PRPs receive special notice letters: the original factory owner (now retired but still alive), three subsequent property owners, six companies that shipped plating waste to the site, and four waste transporters. All fourteen are now jointly and severally liable.

## 🧹 CERCLA Cleanup Process — How Superfund Actually Works

The cleanup follows the **National Contingency Plan (NCP)** — a methodical, legally required sequence:

```
Step 1: PA/SI — Preliminary Assessment / Site Inspection
         ↓  (Is this worth more investigation?)
Step 2: HRS Scoring
         ↓  (Score ≥ 28.50 = NPL eligible)
Step 3: NPL Listing (60-day public comment)
         ↓
Step 4: RI/FS — Remedial Investigation & Feasibility Study
         ↓  (What's contaminated? What are the options?)
Step 5: ROD — Record of Decision
         ↓  (EPA picks the remedy, documents why)
Step 6: RD/RA — Remedial Design & Remedial Action
         ↓  (Design the cleanup. Execute it.)
Step 7: O&M — Operation & Maintenance
         ↓  (Long-term monitoring — years to decades)
Step 8: Five-Year Reviews
         ↓  (Is the remedy still working?)
Step 9: NPL Deletion (cleanup goals achieved)
```

> 📖 **Why Cleanup Takes 25 Years — A Realistic Example:**
> A dry cleaning shop operated for 35 years, using tetrachloroethylene (PCE/PERC) as a solvent. Over decades, spills and improper disposal contaminated the soil and groundwater below the building. When the shop closes, the new owner's Phase I/Phase II ESA reveals contamination in 8 monitoring wells extending 400 feet in a plume toward a residential neighborhood.
>
> The RI finds PCE at 200 times drinking water standards in the source zone. The FS evaluates options: excavation (too deep, too disruptive), soil vapor extraction (works for the shallow soil), and pump-and-treat (for the groundwater plume). EPA issues a ROD selecting dual remediation.
>
> The pump-and-treat system runs for 18 years. Concentrations slowly decline. A five-year review at year 15 confirms progress but notes a "rebound" — contamination slightly increasing after the pumps were briefly shut down for maintenance. The system runs another 5 years. Total timeline from discovery to NPL deletion: **27 years**. This is not unusual for dense non-aqueous phase liquid (DNAPL) contamination.

### The ROD — Record of Decision:

The ROD is the **pivotal document** in any Superfund cleanup. It:
- Documents the nature and extent of contamination (from the RI)
- Evaluates all cleanup alternatives (from the FS)
- Selects the cleanup remedy
- Explains the rationale for the selected remedy
- Sets cleanup goals (the target concentrations or conditions)
- Is published and **available to the public**

Remedy selection criteria (from the NCP) include: overall protection of human health and environment, compliance with standards, long-term effectiveness, short-term effectiveness, implementability, cost, and state/community acceptance.

## ☎️ THE REPORTABLE QUANTITY (RQ) SYSTEM — The Numbers That Start the Phone Calls

### What is a Reportable Quantity?

An **RQ** is the minimum amount of a CERCLA hazardous substance that, if released to the environment within a 24-hour period, requires **immediate notification to the National Response Center (NRC).**

**NRC Number: 1-800-424-8802** — memorize this. It is on the CHMM exam and it belongs on every emergency contact sheet in every facility you ever work at.

### RQs Are Assigned by Hazard Level:

| RQ | Hazard Level | Representative Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **1 lb** | Most toxic — default for unlisted substances | Dioxin, certain radionuclides |
| **10 lbs** | Extremely toxic | Chlorine, hydrogen fluoride, hydrogen sulfide |
| **100 lbs** | Highly toxic | Ammonia (anhydrous), arsenic trioxide |
| **1,000 lbs** | Moderately hazardous | Benzene, toluene, xylene |
| **5,000 lbs** | Least hazardous of listed substances | Acetone, ethylbenzene |

> ⚠️ **The 1-Pound Default:** If a substance is on the CERCLA hazardous substance list but has no assigned RQ, the default is **1 pound**. Any detectable release of such a substance triggers NRC notification. This is an extremely strict standard.

---

> 📖 **Scenario — Accidental Valve Release:**
> A worker at a chemical blending facility opens the wrong valve. For 6 minutes, liquid benzene flows from a storage tank onto the concrete floor before the valve is shut and the area is contained. The spill is vacuum-recovered. Estimated quantity released to environment (through floor drain): approximately **45 gallons ≈ 310 lbs**.
>
> Benzene's RQ = **1,000 lbs**. Wait — 310 lbs is LESS than 1,000 lbs. Do you need to report?
>
> **No federal NRC notification required** for this specific release. However — check your state. Many states have their own reporting thresholds that may be lower than federal RQs. And if this is also an EPCRA EHS chemical with its own RQ, LEPC/SERC notification may be triggered separately.
>
> Now change the scenario: the valve fails entirely and 1,500 lbs of benzene spills before the emergency shutoff activates. 1,500 lbs > 1,000-lb RQ. **Call the NRC immediately.**

---

### ⚠️ CERCLA RQ vs. EPCRA EHS RQ — The Exam's Favorite Trick:

This trips up more exam-takers than almost any other concept on Day 2:

| Notification Type | What Triggers It | Who Gets Called |
|---|---|---|
| **CERCLA Section 103** | Release ≥ CERCLA RQ of any hazardous substance | **NRC: 1-800-424-8802** |
| **EPCRA Section 304** | Release ≥ RQ of an **EHS chemical** | **LEPC and SERC** |

- A single release can trigger **both** (if the chemical is both a CERCLA hazardous substance AND an EPCRA EHS)
- Some releases trigger **only NRC** (CERCLA substance but not an EPCRA EHS)
- Some releases trigger **only LEPC/SERC** (EHS with a lower RQ than its CERCLA counterpart)

> 📖 **Scenario — Chlorine Pipe Rupture:**
> 50 lbs of chlorine gas releases from a ruptured pipe. Chlorine is both a CERCLA hazardous substance (RQ = 10 lbs) AND an EPCRA EHS (RQ = 10 lbs). 50 lbs > 10 lbs.
>
> **Both notifications triggered simultaneously:**
> - Call NRC (CERCLA Section 103)
> - Call LEPC (EPCRA Section 304)
> - Call SERC (EPCRA Section 304)

### The 24-Hour Rule:

The RQ is evaluated against the **total quantity released within any 24-hour period**. Split releases on separate days are evaluated day by day — not cumulatively across days.

- Day 1: release 8 lbs of chlorine (RQ = 10 lbs) → No notification required
- Day 2: release 3 lbs of chlorine → No notification required
- If both happened on the same day: 11 lbs total > 10 lb RQ → **Notification required**

### Exemptions from RQ Notification:
- **Federally permitted releases** (authorized by NPDES, CAA, RCRA permits)
- **Application of pesticides per FIFRA label**
- **Releases of nuclear materials** (governed by Atomic Energy Act / NRC)

## 🧪 National Contingency Plan (NCP) — The Federal Playbook

The **NCP** is the federal government's master blueprint for responding to hazardous substance releases and oil spills. Originally created under the Clean Water Act for oil spills, it was expanded under CERCLA to cover all hazardous substance releases.

**What the NCP establishes:**
- **National Response System structure** — how federal, state, and local agencies coordinate
- **On-Scene Coordinator (OSC)** system — who is the federal incident commander
- **Remedial process** for Superfund cleanups (the 9-step process above)
- **Natural Resource Damage (NRD)** assessment procedures
- **Community relations** requirements during cleanups

### Incident Command System (ICS) and the NCP:

The NCP incorporates **ICS** — the standardized incident management structure used by fire, police, hazmat, and federal agencies. ICS creates a unified chain of command regardless of which agencies are present.

> 📖 **Scenario — Multi-Agency Spill Response:**
> A barge carrying fuel oil ruptures in a navigable river. Within an hour: Coast Guard sets up Unified Command as the On-Scene Coordinator (OSC), state environmental agency joins as deputy, county emergency management provides community liaison, and contracted hazmat responders arrive as a resource under Logistics.
>
> Each contracted responder integrates under the **Operations Section Chief** in the ICS structure. They don't answer to their own company chain of command on-scene — they answer to the ICS Incident Commander. If the contracted hazmat team goes off-script and starts making independent decisions that conflict with the OSC, they're not just being difficult — they're potentially creating liability.
>
> ICS is not bureaucracy. It's the system that prevents 12 agencies from all doing different things at a scene simultaneously.

# 🌿 PART 4 — CERCLA HAZARDOUS SUBSTANCES vs. RCRA HAZARDOUS WASTE: Not the Same Thing

This distinction trips up more CHMM candidates than almost anything else on Day 2.

| Feature | RCRA Hazardous Waste | CERCLA Hazardous Substance |
|---|---|---|
| **What it covers** | Materials being actively MANAGED as waste | Materials RELEASED to the environment |
| **Lists used** | F, K, P, U lists + IRTC characteristics | ~800 substances (includes RCRA + CWA + CAA + TSCA chemicals) |
| **Regulatory trigger** | Generating, storing, transporting, treating, disposing | RELEASING to the environment |
| **Purpose** | Manage hazardous waste safely during its lifecycle | Clean up when management has already failed |
| **Key regulation** | 40 CFR Parts 260–270 | 40 CFR Part 302 |
| **Legal mechanism** | Permitting and operating standards | Liability and cleanup |

> 💡 **The Core Insight — RCRA Prevents. CERCLA Cleans Up.**
>
> Think of it this way: RCRA is the **preventive medicine** — proper management, permitting, and standards that keep hazardous waste from becoming an environmental problem. CERCLA is the **surgery** — it kicks in when the preventive medicine failed and contamination already exists in the ground or water.
>
> Every Superfund site is, in some sense, evidence that RCRA either didn't exist yet (pre-1976) or wasn't followed. RCRA prevents future CERCLA sites. They are complementary, not redundant.

> ⚠️ **CERCLA's List Is Bigger:** CERCLA hazardous substances include ALL RCRA hazardous wastes, PLUS Clean Water Act priority pollutants, PLUS Clean Air Act hazardous air pollutants, PLUS TSCA chemicals, PLUS "imminently hazardous chemical substances or mixtures" under TSCA Section 7. If you're thinking about the size of the CERCLA list vs. the RCRA list — CERCLA wins by a wide margin.

## The Manifest System — RCRA's Cradle-to-Grave Paper Trail (and Your CERCLA Defense)

The **Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest** (EPA Form 8700-22) is the foundational document of the RCRA waste management system. Every shipment of hazardous waste from generator to TSDF must be accompanied by a manifest.

**What the manifest captures:**

| Field | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Generator name, address, EPA ID | Who created the waste |
| Transporter name, address, EPA ID | Who moved it |
| Designated TSDF name, address, EPA ID | Where it's going |
| DOT description (PSN, hazard class, UN/NA #, packing group) | What hazard it poses in transit |
| Quantity of each waste | How much |
| Emergency response information | What to do if something goes wrong |
| Generator certification | Legal attestation of accuracy |
| Signatures from all parties | Chain of custody |

**The manifest exception report:**
If a generator does NOT receive a signed copy of the manifest back from the TSDF within **45 days** of the waste leaving the facility, the generator must investigate and file an **exception report** with EPA within **60 days** of the original shipment date.

This prevents waste from "disappearing" between generator and disposal facility.

> ⚠️ **A Manifest Is a Federal Legal Document:**
> A manifest that says "waste oil" for a drum actually containing chlorinated solvents is not a paperwork error. It is a **false statement on a federal document under 18 USC 1001** — a federal crime. In the field, if you receive a waste drum whose contents don't match the manifest, you don't sign and accept it. You quarantine it, notify the generator, document everything, and if the discrepancy suggests undisclosed hazards, you notify the regulatory agency.

> 💡 **The CERCLA Defense Function of Manifests:**
> In a CERCLA case, a waste generator's best argument for a de minimis settlement (reduced liability based on small waste volume contribution) requires proving exactly how much waste they sent and where. The proof is the manifest. Waste manifests should be kept for a minimum of **3 years** (RCRA requirement) but ideally **much longer** — decades — because CERCLA liability is retroactive and there is no statute of limitations for cleanup costs at NPL sites.

## The Facility Emergency Coordinator Role

Every facility subject to EPCRA Section 302 (EHS at or above TPQ) must designate a **Facility Emergency Coordinator** responsible for:
- Interfacing with the LEPC
- Serving as the point of contact for emergency notifications
- Coordinating with emergency responders during an incident
- Updating the LEPC when chemical inventories change significantly

> 📖 **Scenario — What Emergency Coordinators Actually Do:**
> It's 11:47 PM on a Tuesday. An alarm sounds in a chemical storage warehouse. The facility Emergency Coordinator — in this case, the EHS Manager — gets a call from the night supervisor: *"Valve on the ammonia refrigeration line is stuck open and we can smell ammonia outside the building."*
>
> What happens in the next 90 seconds:
> 1. The Emergency Coordinator calls the LEPC emergency line: *"We have a potential ammonia release at [address]. Anhydrous ammonia, approximately 2,000 lbs on-site, release in progress, quantity unknown. Wind from the south, employees are evacuating upwind."*
> 2. The Emergency Coordinator calls the SERC hotline.
> 3. If the release exceeds ammonia's RQ of 100 lbs: calls NRC at 1-800-424-8802.
> 4. Coordinates with the arriving fire department — provides the emergency coordinator contact for the LEPC plan, shows responders the chemical inventory, points to the ammonia storage area and isolation valves.
>
> The Emergency Coordinator doesn't fight fires. They are the **information bridge** between facility knowledge and emergency response. That role only works if the person knows the facility's chemicals, quantities, and emergency plan cold — **no looking things up during an actual emergency.**

## The Biennial Report — LQG Annual Accounting

Every **Large Quantity Generator (LQG)** must submit a **Biennial Report** to EPA:
- **When:** Even-numbered years only (every 2 years) — not annually
- **Due:** March 1
- **Covers:** Prior calendar year's hazardous waste activities

Report content:
- Quantities of each hazardous waste generated
- Quantities shipped off-site and to which TSDFs
- Waste minimization efforts
- Methods of on-site treatment if any

> ⚠️ **Memory Hook:** BIENNIAL = BI (two) + ENNIAL (years) = every TWO years. Only in EVEN years. Only LQGs. SQGs and VSQGs do NOT file biennial reports.

## 📌 TIER 3 — FIELD APPLICATION SCENARIOS

### Reading a Chemical Release Correctly

When a release happens, ask three questions immediately:

**1. What was released?**
→ Is it an EHS? A CERCLA hazardous substance? Both? Neither?

**2. How much was released (in 24 hours)?**
→ Compare to the RQ. If ≥ RQ: federal notification required.
→ Compare to any applicable EHS RQ: LEPC/SERC notification required.

**3. Where did it go?**
→ Did it reach beyond the facility boundary? Any exemptions apply?

### Manifest Red Flags in the Field

Situations that require you to stop, hold, and document:
- Waste contents don't match manifest description
- No manifest accompanies the shipment
- Generator EPA ID doesn't match records
- Drum condition inconsistent with manifest characterization
- Chemical odor or appearance inconsistent with described waste

A discrepancy between manifest and actual waste content is not a paperwork issue — it is a potential federal violation requiring regulatory notification.

### CERCLA Self-Protection Best Practices

For anyone working in hazardous waste generation, transportation, or disposal:

1. **Keep manifests forever** — there is no "safe" time after which CERCLA liability expires at contaminated sites
2. **Know your disposal contractors** — due diligence before selecting a TSDF is your best protection against strict liability surprises
3. **Document everything** — the only thing better than records is more records
4. **Phase I before any commercial property purchase** — it's not optional, it's the difference between inheriting someone else's CERCLA liability and having a defensible purchaser position

# 🏁 DAY 2 — THE BIG IDEAS THAT STICK

**1. EPCRA = The community's right to know what sits near them and what gets released.**

**8. Your manifest is a legal document, a chain-of-custody record, and your best CERCLA defense.** Every signature on a manifest is a legal attestation of fact. Sign carefully. Keep it forever.
