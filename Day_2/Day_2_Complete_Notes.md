# 🏗️ PART 1 — EPCRA: THE "YOUR NEIGHBORS HAVE A RIGHT TO KNOW" LAW

---

## 🌍 Why EPCRA Exists — The Bhopal Story

On December 3, 1984, a chemical plant in Bhopal, India operated by Union Carbide leaked approximately 40 tons of methyl isocyanate (MIC) — an extremely toxic gas used in pesticide production — into the surrounding community while residents slept.

The result: somewhere between 3,800 and 16,000 deaths (estimates vary), with over 500,000 people exposed. The local community had NO idea this chemical was stored in massive quantities next door. There were no emergency plans. Local hospitals didn't know what the chemical was. Responders didn't know how to treat it.

**The United States watched Bhopal and made a law.**

In 1986, Congress passed the **Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA)** as Title III of the Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act (SARA). The core idea: communities have the **RIGHT to know** what hazardous chemicals are stored near them, and **local governments must plan** for the worst case.

> 💡 **The Clean Harbors Connection:**
> Clean Harbors facilities store massive quantities of hazardous chemicals — solvents, acids, oxidizers, treated waste streams. Under EPCRA, they must report these inventories annually, maintain emergency coordinators, and coordinate with local fire departments and LEPCs. When emergency responders arrive at a Clean Harbors facility at 3 AM because something went wrong, they should NOT be walking in blind. EPCRA ensures they don't.

EPCRA contains Sections 301 through 330. For the CHMM exam and real-world practice, there are four sections that matter enormously:

```
SECTION 301-303 = Emergency Planning

SECTION 311-312 = Chemical Inventory Reporting (Tier I/Tier II)
SECTION 313     = Toxics Release Inventory (TRI/Form R)
```

## 🚨 SECTIONS 301-303: Emergency Planning — Building the Safety Net

### The Three-Layer System

EPCRA created a three-tier emergency planning structure from national → state → local:

```
FEDERAL (EPA)
    ↓ Guidance and oversight
SERC — State Emergency Response Commission
    ↓ Coordinates and oversees
LEPC — Local Emergency Planning Committee
    ↓ Actual local emergency planning
LOCAL FACILITY (YOU)
    ↓ Provides information and coordinates
```

---

### 🏛️ SERC — State Emergency Response Commission

Every state must establish a SERC under EPCRA Section 301.

**SERC responsibilities:**
- Designate Local Emergency Planning Districts
- Appoint and supervise LEPCs
- Review local emergency plans
- Receive Tier II reports from facilities
- Establish procedures for receiving and processing public requests for information

> 💡 **Real World:** In Texas (where Clean Harbors has operations), the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) serves the SERC function. They oversee the local emergency planning structure statewide.

---

### 🏘️ LEPC — Local Emergency Planning Committee

The LEPC is where emergency planning actually HAPPENS — at the community level.

**Required LEPC membership (must include all of these):**
- Elected state and local officials
- **Law enforcement** (police)
- **Civil defense** (emergency management)
- **Firefighting** (fire department)
- **First aid** (EMS/paramedics)
- **Health officials**
- **Local environmental and transportation officials**
- **Broadcast and print media** (so they can warn the public)
- **Community groups** (neighborhood representatives)
- **Owners and operators of facilities** subject to EPCRA ← **This means YOU if you work at a regulated facility**

> 💡 **Why media is included — The warning system:**
> In an emergency, the fastest way to reach 50,000 people at 2 AM is local radio and TV. EPCRA's founders understood this — hence broadcast and print media are required LEPC members. When a chlorine release happens at an industrial facility, the LEPC activates the media members to broadcast shelter-in-place or evacuation orders. This is low-tech but life-saving.

**LEPC responsibilities:**
- Develop and maintain local emergency response plans
- Review and update plans at least annually
- Receive and process facility emergency notifications
- Receive Tier II chemical inventory reports
- Make chemical inventory information available to the public
- Conduct exercises and drills

> ⚠️ **Exam Fact:** LEPC plans must include: identification of EHS facilities, emergency response procedures, designation of community and facility emergency coordinators, emergency equipment inventory, evacuation plans, and a training program.

---

### 🏭 Section 302 — Extremely Hazardous Substance (EHS) Notification

**Section 302 kicks in when a facility has an EHS at or above its Threshold Planning Quantity (TPQ).**

The EPA has a list of **Extremely Hazardous Substances (EHS)** — approximately 356 chemicals — each with a:
- **TPQ (Threshold Planning Quantity):** The amount that, if stored at a facility, triggers emergency planning notification requirements
- **RQ (Reportable Quantity):** The release amount that triggers immediate emergency notification (covered in Section 304)

**TPQ examples:**
| Chemical | TPQ |
|----------|-----|
| Chlorine | 100 lbs |
| Ammonia (anhydrous) | 500 lbs |
| Sulfur dioxide | 500 lbs |
| Methyl isocyanate (the Bhopal chemical) | 500 lbs |
| Hydrogen fluoride | 100 lbs |
| Phosgene | 10 lbs |

> 💡 **The Phosgene Reality Check:**
> Phosgene's TPQ is just 10 pounds. Why so low? Phosgene was used as a chemical weapon in World War I, killing an estimated 85,000 soldiers. It has an odor similar to freshly cut grass or hay — deceptively pleasant. By the time you smell it and think "what's that nice smell?" you're already being injured. A small industrial release can affect a wide area. 10-pound TPQ means even tiny storage quantities trigger full emergency planning. The CHMM exam loves extreme TPQ scenarios like this.

**Facility obligation under Section 302:**
If you have an EHS at or above the TPQ, you must:
1. Notify the SERC and LEPC in writing
2. Designate a **facility emergency coordinator**
3. Cooperate with the LEPC in developing the local emergency plan

> 🏭 **Clean Harbors Scenario:**
> A Clean Harbors facility receives a tanker of liquid chlorine (Cl₂) for treatment. Chlorine's TPQ is 100 lbs. A typical tanker holds 90 tons = 180,000 lbs. That's 1,800 times the TPQ. The LEPC was notified when the facility first began storing chlorine. The local fire department has a copy of the emergency plan. They know chlorine hugs the ground (vapor density 2.47 — heavier than air), they know the evacuation radius, and they know NOT to use water fog in a confined area with chlorine (it creates hydrochloric acid mist). EPCRA made that knowledge transfer happen.

## 🆘 SECTION 304: Emergency Release Notification — "Call Now, Explain Later"

**Section 304 is activated the moment a reportable quantity of an EHS or CERCLA hazardous substance is released.**

This is the EMERGENCY notification provision. When something goes wrong and a regulated quantity escapes into the environment, there is no time to draft a memo.

### Who Gets Called and When:

**Immediate notification required to:**
1. **LEPC** of the area where the release occurred
2. **SERC** of the state where the release occurred

**Also notify** (under CERCLA, not EPCRA technically, but happens simultaneously):
3. **National Response Center (NRC): 1-800-424-8802**

> ⚠️ **EXAM CRITICAL:** The NRC notification comes from CERCLA Section 103 (not EPCRA Section 304 directly), but both notifications happen simultaneously in a real emergency. The exam may test which notification goes where. NRC = federal (CERCLA). LEPC/SERC = community (EPCRA 304).

### What Must Be Communicated in the Emergency Notification:

Section 304 requires the initial notification to include:
- Chemical name or identity
- Whether it's an EHS
- Estimated quantity released
- Time and duration of release
- Medium released into (air, water, soil)
- Known or anticipated acute or chronic health risks
- Medical advice if needed
- Proper precautions (evacuate? shelter-in-place?)
- Name and phone of contact person

> 💡 **Scenario — The 3 AM Chlorine Leak:**
> A valve fails at a Clean Harbors facility at 3:12 AM. Chlorine gas begins releasing. The on-call supervisor immediately calls the LEPC emergency coordinator, the SERC hotline, and the National Response Center — all within minutes, before full containment, before knowing the exact quantity. Why before knowing the exact quantity? Because Section 304 requires notification as soon as the person in charge has knowledge of the release. "We don't know how much yet" is not a legal defense for waiting to call. Call first. Quantify later.

### Written Follow-Up Report:

After the initial emergency oral notification, EPCRA Section 304 requires a **written follow-up notice** to the LEPC and SERC as soon as practicable. The written notice must include:
- Actions taken to respond and contain the release
- Known or anticipated health risks
- Medical advice if appropriate
- Whether compensation may be available

> 💡 **The "Transportable Release" Exception:**
> Releases during transport (from a truck accident, for example) don't require facility emergency coordinator notification — the carrier notifies the NRC instead. The CHMM exam loves this wrinkle. A truck crashes and spills chlorine: the driver (or carrier) calls the NRC. They don't call a facility LEPC because there's no facility involved.

---

### Exemptions from Section 304 Notification:

Not every release triggers a call. You're exempt if:
- The release is a **Federally Permitted Release** (permitted air emission, NPDES permit discharge, etc.)
- The release results in **no exposure to persons outside the facility**
- The released substance is a **pesticide applied in accordance with its label** (FIFRA exemption)
- The release is from a **normal/routine agricultural operation**

> ⚠️ **Exam Trap:** "No exposure to persons outside the facility" is a very narrow exception. If vapors drift to the facility fence line where a road runs, there IS potential exposure outside the facility. This exception is narrower than it sounds.

## 📊 SECTIONS 311-312: Chemical Inventory Reporting — The Annual Audit

These two sections require facilities to tell the government WHAT chemicals they have and in WHAT quantities. This feeds the emergency planning system with the data it needs.

### Section 311 — SDS Submission

**Who must comply:** Facilities that have OSHA HazCom chemicals present in quantities at or above OSHA's threshold (either above the 10,000-lb general threshold, OR any EHS above its TPQ)

**What to submit:** A copy of the SDS (or a list of chemicals in lieu of SDS) for each chemical that meets the threshold

**Submit to:** LEPC, SERC, and local fire department

**When:** Within 3 months of first having the chemical above threshold, and when a new chemical is brought in

> 💡 **Why This Exists:**
> Fire departments need to know what's in a building BEFORE they walk in. When a warehouse is on fire, firefighters need to know if there are acids, oxidizers, or reactive materials inside. The Section 311 SDS submission ensures the local fire department has that information on file — ideally in a database they can query from the truck on the way to the scene.

---

### Section 312 — Tier I and Tier II Reports — The Annual Inventory

This is the annual chemical inventory report. **Due every year by March 1** for the prior calendar year.

**Two tiers:**

| Feature | Tier I | Tier II |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Type | Aggregate information by category | Specific chemical-by-chemical detail |
| What's reported | Total quantities by hazard category | Each chemical name, CAS number, max/average quantities, storage locations |
| Who gets it | LEPC, SERC, local fire department | LEPC, SERC, local fire department |
| Public access | Limited | Full public access on request |
| Common in practice | Rarely used (Tier II is standard) | Standard practice — almost all facilities file Tier II |

**In practice, virtually all facilities file Tier II.** Tier I is essentially obsolete in real-world compliance.

**Tier II requires reporting for each chemical:**
- Chemical name and CAS number
- Maximum amount stored at any time during the year (in coded ranges)
- Average amount stored
- Number of days on-site
- Storage locations (building, lot, etc.)
- Whether the chemical is stored in above-ground tank, underground tank, cylinders, etc.
- Whether information is being withheld as trade secret

**Thresholds for Tier II reporting:**
- EHS chemicals: Above the TPQ or 500 lbs (whichever is less)
- All other OSHA HazCom chemicals: Above 10,000 lbs

> 💡 **Scenario — Annual March 1 Deadline:**
> It's February 28. The environmental compliance manager at Clean Harbors is reviewing the chemical inventory data for the prior year. The facility stored 12,000 lbs of sulfuric acid at peak (EHS, TPQ = 1,000 lbs), 50,000 lbs of waste solvent (flammable OSHA hazard chemical, above 10,000 lb threshold), and trace quantities (200 lbs) of sodium cyanide (EHS, but below its 100-lb TPQ). Wait — 200 lbs of sodium cyanide above its 100-lb TPQ → must be reported. Missing this one because "it's only 200 lbs of one chemical" is exactly the type of compliance failure that results in EPA enforcement actions.

> ⚠️ **Trade Secret Claims:** Facilities can claim the specific chemical identity as trade secret on Tier II, but they MUST still disclose: that they are withholding a chemical identity, the hazard categories it belongs to, and the approximate quantities. "Trade secret" is not a blank exemption from reporting — it's just a way to protect the specific chemical name.

---

## 📈 SECTION 313 — TOXICS RELEASE INVENTORY (TRI): The Public Accountability Report

**Section 313 is different from everything else in EPCRA.** Sections 301-312 are about planning and emergency response. Section 313 is about public accountability for routine chemical releases to the environment.

### What TRI Is:

TRI requires covered facilities to **annually report the total quantities of toxic chemicals they release to the environment** — to air, water, land, or via underground injection — AND the quantities transferred off-site.

**This is not just emergency releases — it includes routine permitted releases.** Every pound of listed chemical released legally or illegally, intentionally or accidentally, must be accounted for.

> 💡 **The "Pollution Scorecard" Concept:**
> TRI data is public. Environmental organizations, journalists, community groups, and government officials use TRI data to identify the biggest polluters in a given area. A company might be 100% in legal compliance with every permit — and still appear at the top of the TRI list for their county. TRI created public accountability that permits alone never could. Many companies voluntarily reduced emissions just to improve their TRI rankings.

### Who Must Report (All Three Criteria Must Be Met):

1. **Industry:** Facility is in a covered SIC code or NAICS code (manufacturing, mining, electric utilities, chemicals, and others)
2. **Employees:** 10 or more full-time equivalent employees
3. **Chemical threshold:** Manufactures, processes, or otherwise uses a listed TRI chemical above threshold quantities:
   - **Manufactures or Processes:** ≥ 25,000 lbs/year threshold
   - **Otherwise Uses:** ≥ 10,000 lbs/year threshold
   - **Special lower thresholds** for certain chemicals (dioxins: 0.1 grams; PBTs: 100 lbs)

> ⚠️ **Manufactures vs. Processes vs. Otherwise Uses:**
> - **Manufactures** = produces the chemical (including as a byproduct)
> - **Processes** = incorporates the chemical into a product
> - **Otherwise uses** = uses the chemical but it's not incorporated into the product (e.g., as a solvent for cleaning)
> The thresholds are different! 25,000 lbs for manufacture/process, 10,000 lbs for otherwise use. The exam tests this distinction.

### TRI Listed Chemicals:

Over **800 individual chemicals** and chemical categories are on the TRI list, including:
- Lead and lead compounds
- Mercury and mercury compounds  
- Benzene
- Trichloroethylene (TCE)
- Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs)
- Dioxin and dioxin-like compounds
- Arsenic and arsenic compounds
- Formaldehyde
- Methylene chloride

### Form R — The TRI Report:

**Due: July 1 each year** for the prior calendar year (filed electronically through EPA's TRI-MEweb system)

Form R requires reporting of:
- Chemical name and CAS number
- Maximum amount on-site
- Releases to each environmental medium (air stack, fugitive air, water, land, underground injection)
- Quantities transferred off-site (to POTW, recyclers, disposal facilities)
- Pollution prevention and waste management activities
- Source reduction activities

> 💡 **"Otherwise Disposed" vs. "Released":**
> This confuses people. On Form R, a "release" means a discharge to the environment. Sending waste to a permitted hazardous waste facility (like Clean Harbors) for treatment/disposal is an "off-site transfer" — it's NOT counted as an environmental release. The receiving facility (Clean Harbors) then has ITS OWN TRI obligations for what it does with the waste. This is why TRI data shows materials flowing through the treatment chain.

### Form A — The Simplified Alternative:

Some facilities can use **Form A** (the simplified certification form) instead of Form R if:
- They claim the chemical is manufactured, processed, or used below 1 million lbs/year
- They claim the annual reportable releases are below 500 lbs
- The chemical is not a persistent, bioaccumulative, and toxic (PBT) chemical

Form A is much simpler — it's basically a certification that releases are minimal.

> 🏭 **Clean Harbors TRI Obligation:**
> Clean Harbors treatment facilities receive hazardous waste containing TRI chemicals and treat/dispose of them. The facility must track and report TRI chemical quantities in incoming waste, releases from the treatment process (stack emissions from the incinerator, for example), and off-site transfers. Incinerators that destroy TRI chemicals count the INPUT quantity (what went into the incinerator) on Form R — even if the output is much less. This is important: destruction = still counts as a "treatment" activity on the form, not a release.

# 🏚️ PART 2 — SARA: THE 1986 AMENDMENTS THAT CHANGED EVERYTHING

## What is SARA?

**SARA = Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act of 1986**

SARA amended CERCLA (the original 1980 Superfund law) and also created EPCRA (as Title III of SARA). It significantly strengthened the hazardous waste cleanup program, added billions of dollars to the Superfund trust fund, and established community right-to-know.

### SARA's Four Titles:

| Title | What It Is |
|-------|------------|
| **Title I** | Amendments to CERCLA — strengthened cleanup authority, added enforcement tools |
| **Title II** | Establishment of a new asbestos program |
| **Title III** | **EPCRA** — Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know (the whole Part 1 above) |
| **Title IV** | Radon gas and indoor air quality |

> ⚠️ **Exam Note:** When the exam says "SARA Title III," it means EPCRA. These terms are used interchangeably. "SARA Title III Section 313" = "EPCRA Section 313." Same thing.

# ☠️ PART 3 — CERCLA: THE SUPERFUND LAW

## "Who Pays to Clean Up the Mess? EVERYONE. Forever."

---

## The Origin Story — Love Canal

In the 1940s-1950s, Hooker Chemical Company dumped approximately 21,000 tons of chemical waste in a lined canal in Niagara Falls, New York. They sold the property to the Niagara Falls School Board for $1 with a deed containing a liability disclaimer. A school was built. Homes were built. By the 1970s, residents were reporting:

- Basement floors seeping chemical sludge
- Children with chemical burns from playing in their backyards
- Abnormally high rates of cancer, miscarriages, and birth defects
- Black oily liquid rising through the ground

The federal government declared a health emergency in 1978. President Carter evacuated 800 families. The cleanup cost hundreds of millions of dollars.

**Congress responded with CERCLA in 1980.**

> 💡 **The Lesson:**
> Hooker Chemical thought selling the land and including a liability disclaimer meant their legal exposure ended. It didn't. Love Canal became Exhibit A for why CERCLA's liability structure is so aggressive — because corporate "we sold it, it's not our problem anymore" thinking had literally poisoned an entire neighborhood.

---

## What CERCLA Does:

1. **Established the Superfund** — a trust fund to pay for emergency cleanup of abandoned contaminated sites when no responsible party can be found or forced to pay immediately
2. **Created the National Priorities List (NPL)** — the list of the worst contaminated sites in the US that qualify for Superfund cleanup funding
3. **Established CERCLA liability** — the legal framework that determines who pays for cleanup
4. **Required reporting of hazardous substance releases** via the Reportable Quantity system
5. **Created the National Contingency Plan (NCP)** framework for how cleanups must be conducted

---

## 💰 CERCLA Liability — The Most Important Legal Concept in Environmental Law

CERCLA's liability is famous for being almost impossible to escape. It has three key characteristics:

### 1. STRICT LIABILITY

You are liable even if you did NOTHING WRONG. You don't have to have been negligent. You don't have to have violated any rule. If you are a "potentially responsible party" (PRP) and your waste ended up at a contaminated site, you are liable for cleanup. Period. No "but I followed all the rules" defense.

> 💡 **The "Innocent Generator" Paradox:**
> A company properly characterized its hazardous waste, properly hired a licensed transporter, properly sent it to a licensed RCRA facility — and did everything right. 10 years later, the RCRA facility goes bankrupt and is discovered to have been illegally disposing of waste. The site is contaminated. CERCLA makes the GENERATOR liable for their share of cleanup costs even though they followed every rule. Strict liability. This is why generator due diligence in selecting disposal contractors is so critical.

### 2. JOINT AND SEVERAL LIABILITY

Each PRP can be held liable for the ENTIRE cleanup cost, not just their proportional share. If you sent 1% of the waste to a site but you're the only solvent party left, EPA can make YOU pay 100% of the cleanup.

> 💡 **The Deep Pockets Problem:**
> Company A sent 50% of the waste. Company B sent 49%. Company C sent 1%. Companies A and B are bankrupt. EPA comes to Company C for payment. Company C says "but we only sent 1% of the waste!" CERCLA says: doesn't matter. You are jointly and severally liable. Pay for the whole thing and then try to sue Companies A and B to recover — good luck with bankrupt defendants.

> 💡 **Why This Matters for Generator Record-Keeping:**
> If you can PROVE you sent only 1% of the waste, you can negotiate with EPA for a de minimis settlement (a small payment that releases you from further liability). But you need RECORDS — manifests, bills of lading, waste characterization documents. Without records, you can't prove your small share. Waste manifests are your legal defense document. Keep them. Keep them for at least 3 years (RCRA requirement), keep them much longer for CERCLA protection.

### 3. RETROACTIVE LIABILITY

CERCLA applies BACKWARDS in time. The law passed in 1980. But if your company disposed of waste BEFORE 1980 — even LEGALLY under the rules of the time — you are still liable under CERCLA. The law is retroactive to pre-1980 disposal activities.

> 💡 **The "We Did It Legally" Defense That Doesn't Work:**
> "We disposed of that waste legally in 1965" is not a defense under CERCLA. Contamination doesn't care what year the rules said was acceptable. If there's contamination and you contributed to it, you're a PRP.

---

## 👥 Potentially Responsible Parties (PRPs) — The Four Categories

CERCLA Section 107(a) identifies four categories of PRPs — all of whom can be held liable:

### Category 1: Current Owners and Operators
The current owner or operator of a contaminated facility, even if they didn't cause the contamination. If you buy contaminated property, you may be buying a CERCLA liability.

> ⚠️ **The Due Diligence Defense (Innocent Purchaser):** If a buyer conducts "All Appropriate Inquiries" (AAI) before purchasing property — specifically an **Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)** and if warranted a **Phase II ESA** — and discovers no contamination, they may qualify as a bona fide prospective purchaser with limited CERCLA liability. This is why Phase I/Phase II ESAs exist. Banks require them before lending on commercial property.

### Category 2: Past Owners/Operators at Time of Disposal
Whoever OWNED the property when the hazardous waste was disposed of there. Even if they've since sold the property.

### Category 3: Generators (Arrangers)
Anyone who "arranged for" the disposal or treatment of hazardous substances — including the companies that GENERATED the waste and hired a disposal company.

### Category 4: Transporters
Companies that transported hazardous substances to the contaminated site, if they selected the disposal site. (If the generator told them where to go, the generator bears more liability.)

---

## 🗺️ The National Priorities List (NPL) — The Superfund "Worst Of" List

EPA maintains the **National Priorities List (NPL)** — the list of contaminated sites that are so serious they qualify for long-term Superfund cleanup funding.

**Getting on the NPL:**
Sites are scored using the **Hazard Ranking System (HRS)** — a numerical scoring system that evaluates the site's risk to human health and the environment based on:
- Migration of contamination through groundwater, surface water, soil, air
- Toxicity and quantity of hazardous substances
- Targets (people and sensitive environments) at risk

**HRS score ≥ 28.50** → eligible for NPL listing

**NPL by the numbers:**
- Approximately 1,300 sites currently on the NPL
- Thousands more have been proposed, studied, or cleaned up and deleted
- Cleanup of an NPL site can take 10-30+ years and cost tens to hundreds of millions of dollars

> 💡 **NPL Site Scenario:**
> A former industrial site in Houston has a HRS score of 45 and gets listed on the NPL. EPA identifies 15 PRPs including the original factory owner, 3 subsequent property owners, 7 companies that sent waste to the site, and 4 transporters. All 15 PRPs receive "special notice letters" giving them the opportunity to negotiate with EPA. If they can agree on a settlement (a "consent decree"), they conduct the cleanup themselves under EPA oversight. If they refuse or can't agree, EPA does the cleanup and sues them all later. Refusing to negotiate is almost always more expensive than settling early.

---

## 🧹 CERCLA Cleanup Process — How Superfund Actually Works

The cleanup process under CERCLA is methodical and follows the National Contingency Plan (NCP). Here are the key steps:

### Step 1: Preliminary Assessment/Site Inspection (PA/SI)
EPA or a contractor conducts a preliminary review — often desktop research and a site visit. Determines if the site warrants further investigation.

### Step 2: Hazard Ranking System (HRS) Scoring
EPA scores the site. If ≥ 28.50, it can be proposed for the NPL.

### Step 3: NPL Listing
Site is proposed for the NPL, public comment period (60 days), then finalized.

### Step 4: Remedial Investigation / Feasibility Study (RI/FS)
- **RI (Remedial Investigation):** Characterizes the nature and extent of contamination — what's there, how much, where it's going, who's at risk
- **FS (Feasibility Study):** Develops and evaluates cleanup alternatives — excavation, pump and treat, in situ treatment, containment, etc.

### Step 5: Record of Decision (ROD)
EPA selects the cleanup remedy and publishes the **Record of Decision (ROD)**. The ROD is public. It documents why EPA selected the specific remedy.

### Step 6: Remedial Design / Remedial Action (RD/RA)
The selected remedy is designed and then implemented (actual cleanup).

### Step 7: Operation and Maintenance (O&M)
After initial cleanup, many sites require long-term monitoring and maintenance (pump and treat systems can run for decades).

### Step 8: Five-Year Reviews
For sites with contamination that remains above levels safe for unrestricted use, EPA conducts **five-year reviews** to assess whether the remedy is still working.

### Step 9: NPL Deletion
When cleanup goals are achieved and the site no longer poses an unacceptable risk, it can be deleted from the NPL.

> 💡 **Why This Takes So Long:**
> A former dry cleaning shop contaminated soil and groundwater with PCE (tetrachloroethylene) over 30 years. The RI finds contamination in 5 monitoring wells extending 400 feet from the source. The FS evaluates: excavation (too expensive — contamination is 50 feet deep), air sparging, pump and treat (groundwater extraction), and monitored natural attenuation. EPA selects a pump and treat system. The system runs for 20 years, achieving cleanup goals. Then a five-year review confirms continued progress. Total timeline: 25+ years from listing to deletion. This is typical for complex groundwater contamination.

## ☎️ THE REPORTABLE QUANTITY (RQ) SYSTEM — The Numbers That Start the Phone Calls

### What is a Reportable Quantity?

A **Reportable Quantity (RQ)** is the minimum amount of a CERCLA hazardous substance that, if released into the environment, REQUIRES immediate notification to the National Response Center (NRC).

**Key rule:** If you release ≥ the RQ in any 24-hour period, you must notify the NRC as soon as possible.

**RQs are assigned based on toxicity, carcinogenicity, and reactivity:**
| RQ | Basis | Examples |
|----|-------|----------|
| 1 lb | Most toxic substances | Dioxin, radionuclides |
| 10 lbs | Very toxic | Chlorine, hydrofluoric acid |
| 100 lbs | Highly toxic | Ammonia, arsenic acid |
| 1,000 lbs | Moderately hazardous | Benzene, toluene |
| 5,000 lbs | Least hazardous of CERCLA list | Acetone, ethylbenzene |

> ⚠️ **Default RQ = 1 pound:** If a CERCLA hazardous substance doesn't have a specified RQ, the default is **1 pound**. This is a strict standard — essentially any detectable release of an unspecified hazardous substance requires notification.

> 💡 **Scenario — The Accidental Valve Opening:**
> A worker accidentally opens the wrong valve at a facility. For 8 minutes, liquid benzene flows through a pipe and onto the ground before the valve is shut. Estimated release: 45 gallons = approximately 310 lbs. Benzene's RQ = 10 lbs. Since 310 lbs >> 10 lbs, this is a reportable release. Call the NRC at 1-800-424-8802 IMMEDIATELY. Also notify the LEPC and SERC per EPCRA Section 304 (benzene is an EHS? Actually benzene is a CERCLA hazardous substance but NOT on the EPCRA EHS list — the CERCLA RQ notification goes to NRC; EPCRA 304 notification to LEPC/SERC is for EHS releases).

> ⚠️ **CERCLA RQ vs. EPCRA EHS RQ — The Exam Loves This:**
> - CERCLA RQ release → notify **National Response Center** (NRC)
> - EPCRA Section 304 EHS release → notify **LEPC and SERC**
> - In practice, a single release can trigger BOTH (many EHS chemicals are ALSO CERCLA hazardous substances)
> - Some releases trigger NRC notification but NOT LEPC/SERC notification (if the chemical has a CERCLA RQ but is not an EPCRA EHS)

### The 24-Hour Rule:

The RQ applies to the **total quantity released to the environment within a 24-hour period.** If you release 8 lbs of chlorine (RQ = 10 lbs) one day and 3 lbs the next day, each day is evaluated separately — neither triggers the RQ alone. But if you release 12 lbs in one day, you must report.

### Exemptions from RQ Notification:

- **Federally permitted releases** (releases authorized by NPDES, Clean Air Act permit, RCRA permit, etc.)
- **Application of pesticides per FIFRA label**
- **Nuclear material releases** (handled under Atomic Energy Act / NRC)

## 🧪 NCP — National Contingency Plan

The **National Contingency Plan (NCP)** is the federal government's blueprint for responding to oil spills and hazardous substance releases. It was originally created under the Clean Water Act for oil spills and was amended by CERCLA to cover hazardous substance releases.

**NCP key elements:**
- National Response System structure (federal, regional, and area response teams)
- On-Scene Coordinator (OSC) system — who is in charge during emergency response
- Remedial process for long-term Superfund cleanups
- Natural Resource Damage (NRD) assessment procedures
- Community relations requirements during cleanups

**Incident Command and the NCP:**
The NCP incorporates the **Incident Command System (ICS)** for emergency response coordination. ICS is the standardized management structure used by emergency responders — fire, police, hazmat, and federal agencies — to manage complex emergency incidents with a unified chain of command.

> 💡 **Why ICS Matters for Clean Harbors:**
> When Clean Harbors responds to a spill as a contractor, they integrate into the ICS structure at the scene. The **On-Scene Coordinator (OSC)** — usually a Coast Guard officer for coastal spills or an EPA representative for inland releases — is the federal authority in charge. Clean Harbors provides specialized equipment and expertise UNDER the OSC's direction. Understanding ICS isn't just theory — it's how you avoid stepping on each other's toes during a complex multi-agency emergency response.

# 🌿 PART 4 — CERCLA HAZARDOUS SUBSTANCES vs. RCRA HAZARDOUS WASTE — Why These Are NOT the Same Thing

This distinction trips up more CHMM candidates than almost anything else.

| Feature | RCRA Hazardous Waste | CERCLA Hazardous Substance |
|---------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| Scope | Materials being MANAGED as waste | Materials released to ENVIRONMENT |
| Lists | F, K, P, U lists + IRTC characteristics | ~800 substances (includes RCRA wastes, CWA pollutants, CAA pollutants, TSCA chemicals, and more) |
| Regulatory trigger | Generating, storing, transporting, treating, disposing | RELEASING to environment |
| Who enforces | EPA's Office of Solid Waste | EPA's Office of Emergency Response and Remediation |
| Key regulation | 40 CFR Parts 260-270 | 40 CFR Part 302 |
| Legal mechanism | Permitting and operating standards | Liability and cleanup |

> 💡 **The Key Insight:**
> RCRA is about MANAGING hazardous waste properly during its lifecycle. CERCLA kicks in when the management FAILED — when contamination has already occurred and needs to be cleaned up. RCRA prevents future CERCLA sites.

> ⚠️ **CERCLA Hazardous Substances Include:**
> All RCRA hazardous wastes, PLUS Clean Water Act priority pollutants, PLUS Clean Air Act hazardous air pollutants, PLUS TSCA chemicals, PLUS "imminently hazardous chemical substances or mixtures" under TSCA Section 7. The CERCLA list is BIGGER than the RCRA list.

## The Manifest System — RCRA's Cradle-to-Grave Paper Trail

The **Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest** (EPA Form 8700-22) is the key document in the RCRA transportation system. Every shipment of hazardous waste from generator to TSDF must be accompanied by a manifest. 

This is also the primary CERCLA liability protection document for generators.

**The manifest must include:**
- Generator name, address, EPA ID number
- Transporter name, address, EPA ID number
- Designated TSDF name, address, EPA ID number
- DOT description of each waste (proper shipping name, hazard class, UN/NA number, packing group)
- Quantity of each waste
- Emergency response information
- Generator certification
- Signatures from all parties as waste changes hands

**The manifest "exception report":** If a generator does not receive a signed copy of the manifest back from the TSDF within 45 days of the waste leaving the generator's facility, the generator must investigate and submit an exception report to EPA within 60 days. This system ensures that waste doesn't "disappear" in transit.

> 🏭 **Clean Harbors Connection:**
> When a drum of hazardous waste arrives at a Clean Harbors facility, one of the first things verified is the manifest. The manifest is signed by the receiving facility, a copy is returned to the generator, and the original is retained by Clean Harbors per RCRA requirements. Clean Harbors' EPA ID numbers, the types of waste they're permitted to receive, and the accuracy of the manifest against the actual waste contents are all critical compliance checkpoints. A manifest that says "waste oil" for a drum containing chlorinated solvents is not just a paperwork error — it's a federal crime (false statement on a federal document under 18 USC 1001).

---

## The Biennial Report — LQG Annual Accounting

Every **Large Quantity Generator (LQG)** must submit a **Biennial Report** to EPA (even-numbered years, due March 1) covering the prior year's hazardous waste activities:
- Quantities of each hazardous waste generated
- Quantities shipped off-site and to which facilities
- Waste minimization efforts
- Methods of on-site treatment

> 💡 **Remember:** Biennial means every two years (even years). It is NOT an annual report. SQGs do not file biennial reports; only LQGs do.

---

## Emergency Coordinator Role Under EPCRA

Every facility subject to Section 302 (EHS above TPQ) must designate a **Facility Emergency Coordinator** whose job is to:
- Interface with the LEPC
- Be the point of contact for emergency notification
- Coordinate with emergency responders during an incident
- Update the LEPC when chemical inventories change significantly

> 🏭 **At Clean Harbors:**
> The facility environmental health and safety manager typically serves as the Emergency Coordinator. During an incident, this person is the bridge between facility operations and external emergency responders. They need to know the facility's chemical inventory, the emergency plan, evacuation routes, and the specific chemical hazards — cold, without looking anything up. Under the pressure of an actual emergency, there's no time to search through binders.

### CERCLA Liability — Three Characteristics
```
1. STRICT = Liable even without negligence
2. JOINT AND SEVERAL = Any PRP can be held for 100% of cost
3. RETROACTIVE = Applies to pre-1980 disposal activities
```

### Four PRP Categories (CERCLA Section 107a)
```
1. Current owners/operators of contaminated facility
2. Past owners/operators AT TIME OF DISPOSAL
3. Generators (arrangers) who sent waste to the site
4. Transporters who selected the disposal site
```

```
NPL = National Priorities List (worst sites, qualify for Superfund $)
HRS = Hazard Ranking System (score ≥ 28.50 → NPL eligible)
ROD = Record of Decision (documents EPA's chosen remedy)
RI/FS = Remedial Investigation / Feasibility Study
NCP = National Contingency Plan (the playbook for Superfund cleanups)
OSC = On-Scene Coordinator (federal incident commander)
PRP = Potentially Responsible Party (any of the 4 categories above)
```

```
1 lb = Most toxic (dioxin, default for unlisted substances)
10 lbs = Very toxic (chlorine, HF, hydrogen sulfide)
100 lbs = Highly toxic (ammonia, arsenic compounds)
1,000 lbs = Moderate (benzene, toluene)
5,000 lbs = Least hazardous of listed substances

Release ≥ RQ within 24 hours → Call NRC: 1-800-424-8802
```

4. Firefighting

6. Health officials

### TRI Form R Details
```
Due: July 1 annually
Filed to: EPA (TRI-MEweb electronic system)
Three activity thresholds:
  - Manufacture/Process: ≥ 25,000 lbs → must report
  - Otherwise Use: ≥ 10,000 lbs → must report
  - PBT chemicals: much lower thresholds (dioxins: 0.1 grams)
Form A (simplified): available if releases < 500 lbs AND total < 1 million lbs
```

### CERCLA Cleanup Steps (in order)
```
1. PA/SI → Preliminary Assessment / Site Inspection
2. HRS Scoring → ≥ 28.50 = NPL eligible
3. NPL Listing → 60-day public comment period
4. RI/FS → Remedial Investigation & Feasibility Study
5. ROD → Record of Decision (EPA selects remedy)
6. RD/RA → Remedial Design & Remedial Action (actual cleanup)
7. O&M → Long-term Operation and Maintenance
8. Five-Year Reviews → Required if contamination remains
9. NPL Deletion → When cleanup goals achieved
```

### Manifest System
```
Form: EPA Form 8700-22 (Uniform Hazardous Waste Manifest)
Required for: All RCRA hazardous waste shipments
Exception Report: If generator doesn't get signed manifest back in 45 days
Exception Report filed to EPA: Within 60 days of shipment date
Manifest retention: Minimum 3 years (RCRA), longer for CERCLA protection
```

### Generator Reporting
```
Biennial Report: LQGs only, even-numbered years, due March 1
Who files: LQGs (≥ 1,000 kg/month hazardous waste)
SQGs and CESQGs: Do NOT file biennial reports
```

### Phase I vs Phase II ESA
```
Phase I = Desktop review + site inspection = identifies recognized
          environmental conditions (RECs) — NO sampling
Phase II = Actual soil/groundwater sampling = confirms/quantifies
           contamination
Purpose: Establish "Innocent Purchaser" defense under CERCLA
Standard: ASTM E1527-21 (Phase I), ASTM E1903 (Phase II)
```

# 🏁 DAY 2 SUMMARY — THE BIG IDEAS

1. **EPCRA = Community's right to know what's stored near them AND what's been released**

2. **Four critical EPCRA sections:** 302 (planning), 304 (emergency notification), 312 (annual inventory Tier II), 313 (TRI Form R)

3. **Two deadlines you'll use forever:** March 1 = Tier II reports. July 1 = TRI/Form R.

4. **CERCLA is retroactive, strict, and joint-and-several** — meaning it is essentially impossible to escape if you're a PRP

5. **Four PRP categories:** current owner/operator, past owner at time of disposal, generators (arrangers), transporters who chose the site

6. **RQ releases trigger NRC notification (CERCLA)** and LEPC/SERC notification (EPCRA 304) — these are two separate notification requirements that often happen simultaneously

7. **RCRA manages waste during its lifecycle. CERCLA cleans up when RCRA failed.** They are complementary laws, not duplicates.

8. **Your manifest is your legal defense document.** Every manifest your facility signs is a representation of fact. Make it accurate. Keep it forever.
