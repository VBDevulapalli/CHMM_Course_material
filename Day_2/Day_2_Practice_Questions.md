**Q1.** On December 3, 1984, residents of a city in India woke up choking as a cloud of methyl isocyanate (MIC) rolled through their neighborhoods. Somewhere between 3,800 and 16,000 people died. No warning was given. The community had no idea the chemical was stored nearby. Which U.S. law was passed DIRECTLY in response to this disaster?

- A) RCRA — to regulate hazardous waste generation
- B) TSCA — to restrict dangerous new chemicals in commerce
- C) **EPCRA — to require emergency planning and community right-to-know**
- D) FIFRA — to regulate pesticide application

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

The Bhopal disaster (Union Carbide plant, India, 1984) was the direct catalyst for **EPCRA — the Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act**, passed in 1986 as Title III of SARA. The three failures Bhopal exposed — no community notification, no emergency plan, no responder information — are exactly what EPCRA's three-layer planning system (SERC → LEPC → Facility) was designed to fix.

> 🧠 **Lock it in:** Bhopal → EPCRA. Every time you see that connection on the exam, answer EPCRA.

</details>

**Q2.** A compliance officer says: *"We need to file our SARA Title III reports by March 1."* What law are they actually referring to?

- A) CERCLA
- B) RCRA
- C) **EPCRA (Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act)**
- D) TSCA

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

"SARA Title III" and "EPCRA" are **completely interchangeable terms**. EPCRA was enacted AS Title III of the Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act (SARA) of 1986. When the exam says "SARA Title III Section 313," it means the same thing as "EPCRA Section 313." Do not let the dual naming trip you up.

> 🧠 **SARA Titles:** I = CERCLA amendments | II = Asbestos (AHERA) | **III = EPCRA** | IV = Radon

</details>

**Q4.** When a chemical emergency happens at an industrial facility, which is the CORRECT order of the emergency planning structure EPCRA created — from federal level down to local?

- A) EPA → LEPC → SERC
- B) NRC → SERC → LEPC
- C) **EPA → SERC → LEPC**
- D) SERC → EPA → LEPC

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

The EPCRA planning pyramid flows: **EPA** (federal guidance and oversight) → **SERC** (State Emergency Response Commission — designates local districts, appoints and supervises LEPCs) → **LEPC** (Local Emergency Planning Committee — where actual community-level planning happens). Facilities feed information into the LEPC level.

> 🧠 **Memory trick:** Federal → State → Local. Three layers, top to bottom.

</details>

**Q5.** A metal finishing plant just received its first delivery of liquid chlorine for an electroplating process. The delivery: one railcar containing 90 tons. Chlorine's Threshold Planning Quantity (TPQ) is 100 lbs. The railcar holds approximately 180,000 lbs. Which EPCRA section is triggered?

- A) Section 304 — there must have been a release
- B) Section 313 — TRI reporting for chlorine
- C) **Section 302 — EHS planning notification because chlorine storage exceeds the TPQ**
- D) Section 311 — SDS submission only; no other obligation

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

180,000 lbs of chlorine is **1,800× the 100-lb TPQ**. **Section 302** is triggered whenever an EHS is stored at or above its TPQ. The facility must: notify the SERC and LEPC **in writing within 60 days** and designate a **facility emergency coordinator**.

Section 304 only activates for an **actual release** — not mere storage. The plant hasn't spilled anything yet.

> ⚠️ Storage above TPQ = Section 302. Release above RQ = Section 304. These are different triggers.

</details>

**Q6.** A plant manager asks: *"We have anhydrous ammonia above the TPQ. Who is the 'facility emergency coordinator' supposed to be, and what do they actually do?"* Which answer is correct?

- A) The local fire chief designates the coordinator — it's not the facility's choice
- B) Any manager, but their only role is to sign the Section 302 notification
- C) **A facility-designated person who interfaces with the LEPC, serves as the emergency contact, coordinates with responders during incidents, and must know the chemical inventory cold — no time to look things up during an emergency**
- D) A third-party emergency management consultant required to live on-site

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

Section 302 requires the facility to designate its own **Facility Emergency Coordinator** — typically the EHS manager or plant manager. This person must: interface with the LEPC, be the point of contact for emergency notification, coordinate with responding agencies during an actual incident, and proactively update the LEPC when chemical inventories change significantly. The role matters most at 2 AM during an actual release when there's no time to search binders.

</details>

**Q9.** An EHS chemical is released above its Section 304 reportable quantity, but it is NOT on the CERCLA hazardous substances list. Who must be notified?

- A) National Response Center (NRC) only — federal takes priority
- B) **LEPC and SERC only — EPCRA Section 304 applies; NRC notification is a CERCLA obligation that doesn't apply here**
- C) LEPC, SERC, and NRC — all three always required for any EHS release
- D) No notification — only CERCLA hazardous substances trigger notification

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

The NRC call is a **CERCLA Section 103** obligation — it applies when a CERCLA hazardous substance is released above its RQ. If a chemical is an **EPCRA EHS but NOT a CERCLA hazardous substance**, only the **EPCRA Section 304 notifications** (LEPC and SERC) are required. The NRC is not called.

> 🧠 **The two notification systems:** EPCRA 304 → LEPC + SERC. CERCLA 103 → NRC. A single release can trigger both, one, or neither depending on the chemical's classification.

</details>

**Q10.** At 3:15 AM, a supervisor calls the LEPC emergency coordinator to report an ammonia release. The release is contained by 4:30 AM. The LEPC coordinator asks: *"Will we get a written report?"* What does EPCRA Section 304 require?

- A) Nothing further — the oral call is the entire obligation
- B) A written report filed with EPA within 30 days
- C) **A written follow-up notice to the LEPC and SERC as soon as practicable after the release, covering actions taken, health risks, and whether compensation may be available**
- D) A formal OSHA incident report within 8 hours

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

Section 304 has **two steps**: (1) Immediate oral notification to LEPC and SERC (and NRC if CERCLA substance), and (2) **written follow-up notice** to the LEPC and SERC as soon as practicable. The written notice must include: response and containment actions taken, known or anticipated health risks, any medical advice, and whether affected persons may be eligible for compensation. There is no fixed hour deadline for the written follow-up, but "as soon as practicable" means quickly.

</details>

**Q12.** A chemical plant has an NPDES permit that authorizes discharge of treated process water containing low levels of a regulated substance. One evening, the routine permitted discharge occurs. The substance happens to be an EHS above its RQ in the discharge. Is Section 304 notification required?

- A) Yes — any EHS release above the RQ requires immediate notification, permit or not
- B) **No — federally permitted releases are specifically exempt from EPCRA Section 304 notification**
- C) Yes — NPDES permits only cover water; Section 304 still applies
- D) Only if the discharge goes to a navigable waterway

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

**Federally permitted releases** — discharges authorized by NPDES permits, Clean Air Act operating permits, RCRA operating permits, and certain other federal authorizations — are **exempt from Section 304 notification**. The theory: EPA already evaluated and approved these releases through the permit process. Unpermitted, accidental, or unauthorized releases must be reported regardless.

> ⚠️ **The exemption requires a permit that specifically authorizes the release** — not just "we have some permits."

**Q13.** A neighborhood resident hears there's a large chemical plant near her home and wants to know what hazardous chemicals are stored there. Under EPCRA, what is she entitled to do?

- A) Nothing — facility chemical inventories are confidential for security reasons
- B) Request access from the LEPC or SERC; Tier II inventory information is public upon request for any member of the public

This is the core purpose of EPCRA — communities have the right to know what sits near them.

**Q14.** A homeowner is concerned about elevated radon levels in his basement. Which SARA title addressed radon and indoor air quality?

- A) Title I
- B) Title II
- C) Title III
- D) **Title IV**

<details>

> - **July 1** = TRI / Form R (toxic releases)

**Q17.** A distribution warehouse stores 8,500 lbs of isopropyl alcohol (an OSHA HazCom chemical, not an EHS) year-round. Does this trigger a Tier II filing requirement?

- A) Yes — any OSHA hazard chemical requires Tier II filing
- B) **No — for non-EHS OSHA HazCom chemicals, the Tier II threshold is 10,000 lbs. At 8,500 lbs, they are below the threshold**
- C) No — only EHS chemicals are covered by Tier II
- D) Yes — flammable liquids automatically require Tier II regardless of quantity

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

Non-EHS OSHA HazCom chemicals require Tier II filing only if the **maximum amount on-site at any point during the year was ≥ 10,000 lbs**. At 8,500 lbs, this warehouse is below the threshold. If a single large delivery pushed inventory to 10,500 lbs even for one day, that would trigger the requirement — because Tier II reports the **maximum amount** during the year.

</details>

**Q18.** A compliance trainer explains Tier I vs. Tier II to a new hire: *"One of them is essentially a relic that no one uses anymore."* Which is the relic, and why?

- A) Tier II — because Tier I provides more useful aggregate data
- B) **Tier I — because Tier II provides specific chemical-by-chemical detail (name, CAS#, quantities, locations, storage types) and virtually all facilities choose to file Tier II**
- C) Neither — both are equally used in practice
- D) Tier I — because it was replaced by Form R in 1990

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

**Tier I** provides only aggregate quantities by hazard category — total lbs of flammables, total lbs of corrosives, etc. — without identifying which specific chemicals. **Tier II** provides full chemical-by-chemical detail that emergency planners and responders actually need. In practice, virtually all facilities file Tier II, making Tier I obsolete. Know Tier I conceptually for the exam, but Tier II is reality.

</details>

**Q20.** A chemical company claims trade secret protection for a proprietary solvent blend on their Tier II report. Under EPCRA, which of the following is the company STILL required to disclose?

- A) Nothing — trade secret claim allows complete omission from Tier II
- B) The chemical name to emergency responders only, not the public
- C) **The hazard categories the chemical belongs to, approximate quantities, and storage locations — just not the specific chemical identity**
- D) The CAS number but not the chemical name

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

Trade secret protection under EPCRA is **name-withholding only** — it is NOT a reporting exemption. The facility must still disclose: that a chemical identity is being withheld, which hazard categories it belongs to (flammable, toxic, reactive, etc.), and the approximate quantities and storage locations. During an actual emergency, first responders can access the withheld chemical identity.

> ⚠️ "Trade secret" ≠ "doesn't have to be reported." The hazard information and quantities must still appear on Tier II.

</details>

**Q23.** A startup chemical company is trying to determine if they must file Form R. Their attorney says all three TRI criteria must be met simultaneously. Which set of criteria is correct?

- A) Any hazardous chemical present; more than 50 employees; any federal permit
- B) **Covered industry/NAICS code; 10 or more full-time equivalent employees; manufactures/processes ≥ 25,000 lbs OR otherwise uses ≥ 10,000 lbs of a listed TRI chemical**
- C) RCRA-regulated waste generator; OSHA-regulated workplace; any CERCLA-listed chemical on-site
- D) Registered with EPA; has an air permit; generates hazardous waste

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

All three TRI criteria must be satisfied simultaneously:
1. **Covered industry** (manufacturing, mining, electric utilities, chemical wholesale, federal facilities, etc.)
2. **10+ full-time equivalent employees**
3. **Activity threshold exceeded**: manufacture or process ≥ 25,000 lbs/year, OR otherwise use ≥ 10,000 lbs/year

Miss any one of the three and TRI reporting is not required for that chemical.

**Q24.** A paint manufacturer uses trichloroethylene (TCE) as a solvent to clean equipment between production runs. The TCE never ends up in the paint product — it's used and then evaporates or is captured as waste. Last year they used 18,000 lbs of TCE. What is the applicable TRI threshold?

- A) 25,000 lbs — same as manufacturing threshold
- B) 5,000 lbs — lower threshold for solvents
- C) **10,000 lbs — TCE is "otherwise used" (not manufactured or incorporated into a product), so the lower threshold applies**
- D) 50,000 lbs — threshold is higher for cleaning applications

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

"**Otherwise uses**" means the chemical is used in the production process but does NOT end up in the final product (cleaning solvents, lubricants, process aids). The threshold for "otherwise use" is **10,000 lbs/year** — lower than the 25,000-lb threshold for manufacturing/processing. At 18,000 lbs, this paint company exceeds the 10,000-lb "otherwise use" threshold and must file Form R for TCE.

> ⚠️ The lower threshold for "otherwise use" exists because these chemicals are MORE likely to be released — they're not captured in products.

</details>

**Q25.** An incinerator generates small amounts of dioxin as a combustion byproduct — it wasn't intentionally produced and nobody wanted it. Under EPCRA Section 313, is this "manufacturing" of a TRI chemical?

- A) No — unintentional byproduct formation is not "manufacturing"; no reporting required
- B) **Yes — "manufacturing" includes producing a TRI chemical as a byproduct, even unintentionally**

Under TRI rules, **"manufacturing" includes unintentional byproduct formation**. Incinerators, smelters, and other combustion processes often generate dioxins, furans, and other toxic byproducts. This is especially critical for combustion facilities because dioxins have an extraordinarily low reporting threshold (0.1 grams).

**Q26.** An auditor asks a facility manager: *"Why does dioxin have a TRI reporting threshold of 0.1 grams while benzene's threshold is 25,000 lbs? That's a 100-million-fold difference."* What is the correct explanation?

- A) Dioxin is more common in industrial processes so EPA set a lower threshold to capture more reporters
- B) The 0.1-gram threshold was set arbitrarily and is under revision
- C) **Dioxins are PBT chemicals — Persistent, Bioaccumulative, and Toxic — that don't degrade, accumulate up the food chain, and cause harm at parts-per-trillion concentrations; their extreme toxicity and persistence justify the ultra-low threshold**
- D) Dioxin is more expensive to produce so facilities release less of it

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

Dioxins are the definition of **PBT — Persistent, Bioaccumulative, and Toxic**. They: (1) don't break down in the environment, (2) accumulate in fat tissue and travel up the food chain from sediment → insects → fish → birds → humans, and (3) cause reproductive harm, immune suppression, and cancer at concentrations measured in **parts per trillion**. A 0.1-gram annual threshold reflects genuine toxicological severity — any detectable dioxin release matters.

</details>

**Q27.** A chemical plant ships 60,000 lbs of benzene-contaminated waste to a licensed hazardous waste treatment facility for incineration. On the plant's Form R, how is this 60,000 lbs treated?

- A) As a release to land — because waste was removed from the property
- B) As a release to air — because it will eventually be incinerated
- C) **As an "off-site transfer" to a licensed treatment facility — not an environmental release**
- D) Not reported at all — the plant's TRI obligation ends when the waste leaves the gate

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

Sending waste to a permitted treatment, storage, and disposal facility (TSDF) is an **off-site transfer**, reported separately on Form R — it is NOT counted as an environmental release. The licensed TSDF that receives and processes the waste then has its OWN TRI obligations for that material. TRI tracks the chemical through the entire treatment chain, preventing underreporting while also avoiding double-counting as environmental releases.

</details>

**Q30.** What is the key difference between what TRI Section 313 reports and what EPCRA Section 304 emergency notification covers?

- A) TRI covers water releases; Section 304 covers air releases
- B) **TRI is an annual accounting of ALL releases (routine and accidental) of listed chemicals; Section 304 is the immediate emergency notification for acute releases of EHS chemicals above their RQ**
- C) TRI covers small facilities; Section 304 covers large facilities
- D) There is no difference — they cover the same events

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

These serve completely different purposes: **TRI (Section 313)** is an **annual retrospective** — how much of listed chemicals was released to the environment over the entire year, including legally permitted routine releases. **Section 304** is an **immediate emergency notification** — something bad just happened and the community needs to know right now. One is calendar-year accounting; the other is a phone call at 3 AM.

</details>

**Q31.** A craft brewery has 8 full-time employees and uses 30,000 lbs per year of a TRI-listed chemical (a cleaning agent) in their production process. Must they file Form R?

- A) Yes — they exceed the 25,000-lb "otherwise use" threshold... wait, the "otherwise use" threshold is 10,000 lbs, so yes
- B) **No — they have only 8 employees, which fails the 10+ full-time employee criterion; all three TRI criteria must be met**
- C) Yes — any use of a TRI chemical above 10,000 lbs requires Form R regardless of facility size
- D) No — breweries are exempt from TRI as food/beverage facilities

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

The **employee threshold** acts as a firm cutoff for small facilities. Even though this brewery uses 30,000 lbs (well above the 10,000-lb "otherwise use" threshold), **8 employees < 10 employees required**. **Q37.** A company discovers their waste was illegally dumped at a site in 1968 — long before CERCLA was passed. They argue: *"Whatever happened before 1980 can't count — CERCLA didn't exist yet."* Is their argument correct?

- A) Yes — CERCLA only applies to disposal activities after 1980
- B) Yes — the statute of limitations protects actions more than 10 years old

**✅ Answer: C**

**Retroactive liability** is one of CERCLA's three defining characteristics. The law applies backward in time with no cutoff date. "We disposed of it legally in 1963 under the rules of that era" is not a defense. The contamination exists now. The public health risk exists now. CERCLA holds responsible parties accountable regardless of when the disposal occurred or whether it was legal at the time.

**Q38.** A pharmaceutical company followed every RCRA rule perfectly: proper waste characterization, licensed transporter, permitted TSDF. - B) No — CERCLA only pursues companies that violated regulations

**✅ Answer: C**

**Strict liability** under CERCLA means **no negligence required, no rule violation required, no bad intent required**. If you arranged for disposal of hazardous waste that ended up at a contaminated site, you are a PRP — period. This is exactly why generator due diligence in selecting disposal contractors goes far beyond checking a license: periodic audits of the TSDF, checking their compliance history, confirming they're actually treating/disposing properly.

- B) **Removal Action — short-term emergency response to address an immediate threat; Remedial Actions are long-term cleanups following the full RI/FS/ROD process**

**Q89.** The National Contingency Plan (NCP) expresses a "preference for treatment." What does this mean for Superfund remedy selection?

- A) EPA must always select the cheapest treatment option
- B) All Superfund cleanups must include active chemical treatment of some kind
- C) **CERCLA Section 121 establishes a statutory preference for remedies that permanently and significantly reduce toxicity, mobility, or volume through treatment — pure containment without treatment is disfavored when treatment is practicable**
- D) Treatment technologies must be operated by licensed chemical contractors only

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

CERCLA Section 121's **preference for treatment** means EPA favors cleanup approaches that actually destroy or transform contaminants (excavation with thermal treatment, in situ chemical oxidation, bioremediation) over approaches that merely contain or immobilize them (engineered caps, slurry walls, institutional controls alone). Containment may be selected for practical or cost reasons, but EPA must explain why treatment was rejected. Many real-world remedies combine treatment of the most contaminated areas with containment of lower-concentration zones.

</details>

**Q90.** A company wants to reduce its Form R releases of a TRI chemical to improve its community image. Following the pollution prevention (P2) hierarchy, what is the MOST preferred approach?

- A) Install better stack scrubbers to capture more emissions before release
- B) Treat the waste more thoroughly before disposal
- C) **Substitute a non-TRI chemical (or eliminate use of the chemical entirely) — source reduction at the top of the P2 hierarchy is always preferable to treatment or disposal**
- D) Transfer more waste to licensed TSDFs so it appears as "off-site transfer" rather than environmental release

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

The **pollution prevention hierarchy** ranks approaches from most to least preferred:
1. **Source reduction** (eliminate or reduce the chemical at the source) ← Most preferred
2. Reuse/recycle
3. Treatment (destroy or neutralize the pollutant)
4. Disposal/release ← Least preferred

Installing better scrubbers (A) is treatment — lower on the hierarchy. Sending more waste to TSDFs (D) just moves the TRI obligation elsewhere. **Switching to a non-TRI chemical eliminates the reporting obligation AND the actual release** — the best outcome.

</details>

**Q91.** Among four parties involved with a Superfund site, which one has the STRONGEST potential argument to limit CERCLA liability?

- A) A generator who sent waste following all RCRA rules but whose waste ended up at the site
- B) A transporter who independently selected the contaminated disposal site
- C) **A current property buyer who conducted a thorough Phase I ESA before purchase (found no RECs), has not contributed to contamination, and has cooperated with EPA**
- D) The original property owner who sold it in 1978 before CERCLA was passed

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

The **Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser (BFPP)** defense is CERCLA's strongest available protection for current property owners. The original property owner (D) is still a PRP as past owner at time of disposal — retroactive liability. The generator (A) faces strict liability regardless of RCRA compliance. The transporter who selected the site (B) is a clear PRP Category 4. The innocent purchaser with proper due diligence (C) has the most legally defensible position.

</details>

**Q92.** An LEPC discovers that a new industrial facility in their district has been storing EHS chemicals above the TPQ for 8 months without submitting Section 302 notification or Tier II reports. What authority does the LEPC have to compel compliance?

- A) The LEPC can immediately revoke the facility's operating license
- B) The LEPC can impose fines of up to $10,000 per day
- C) **LEPCs have no direct enforcement authority — they should notify the SERC and escalate to state enforcement authorities and/or EPA, which have EPCRA civil penalty authority**
- D) The LEPC can order the facility to cease operations until compliant

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

**LEPCs are planning and coordination bodies, not enforcement agencies.** They have no authority to impose fines, revoke permits, or compel compliance. When non-compliance is found, the LEPC's role is to escalate: notify the SERC, which can refer to state enforcement or EPA. EPA has civil penalty authority under EPCRA — penalties up to $25,000 per day per violation for Section 302, 311, and 312 violations. In practice, proactively contacting the non-compliant facility to explain their obligations often resolves ignorance-based non-compliance without enforcement action.

</details>

**Q93.** Hooker Chemical sold contaminated Love Canal property for $1 with a liability disclaimer deed. Under CERCLA, does that deed disclaimer protect Hooker from liability?

- A) Yes — contractual limitations on liability are enforceable under CERCLA
- B) **No — CERCLA liability provisions override private contractual disclaimers; Hooker as the entity that disposed of 21,000 tons of chemical waste is a classic PRP regardless of the deed language**
- C) Yes — but only for cleanup costs after the sale date, not before
- D) Partially — the disclaimer limits liability to 50% of cleanup costs

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

CERCLA's liability provisions **override private contracts and deed disclaimers**. You cannot contractually disclaim CERCLA liability to the government through a property deed. Hooker Chemical as the waste depositor is an arranger/generator PRP — one of the four statutory categories. This is exactly the lesson Congress drew from Love Canal: "We sold it, we disclaimed it, it's not our problem anymore" thinking was precisely what CERCLA was designed to prevent. The Love Canal deed disclaimer became Exhibit A for why CERCLA needed to be written the way it was.

</details>

**Q94.** A CHMM candidate is asked to put the CERCLA cleanup sequence in correct order. Which sequence is right?

- A) HRS → ROD → RI/FS → NPL → RD/RA → O&M → Deletion
- B) NPL → PA/SI → RI/FS → ROD → HRS → O&M → Deletion
- C) **PA/SI → HRS Scoring → NPL Listing → RI/FS → ROD → RD/RA → O&M → Five-Year Reviews → NPL Deletion**
- D) RI/FS → HRS → PA/SI → NPL → ROD → O&M → Deletion

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

The 9-step CERCLA cleanup sequence — memorize this cold:

```
1. PA/SI    → Preliminary Assessment / Site Inspection
2. HRS      → Hazard Ranking System scoring (≥28.50 = NPL eligible)
3. NPL      → Listing (60-day public comment period)
4. RI/FS    → Remedial Investigation / Feasibility Study
5. ROD      → Record of Decision (EPA selects remedy)
6. RD/RA    → Remedial Design / Remedial Action (cleanup)
7. O&M      → Operation and Maintenance (long-term)
8. 5-Year   → Reviews (if contamination remains above unrestricted-use levels)
9. Deletion → From NPL when cleanup goals achieved
```

</details>

**Q95.** A Form R for a manufacturing facility shows 40,000 lbs of toluene released during the year. Half came from a permitted smokestack and half from equipment leaks throughout the plant. On Form R, these must be reported separately as:

- A) Both as "releases to air" — no distinction between air release types is required
- B) **Stack emissions as "point source (stack) air releases" and equipment leaks as "fugitive air emissions" — TRI distinguishes between these two air release categories**
- C) Stack emissions as permitted releases (not required to be reported) and fugitive as unpermitted releases
- D) Both combined in a single "total air release" field

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

Form R distinguishes between two types of air releases:
- **Point source (stack) releases:** From identifiable discharge points — stacks, vents, pipes, exhausts
- **Fugitive air releases:** Diffuse, non-point emissions — equipment leaks, evaporative losses, open-process emissions, loading/unloading losses

Both must be reported separately. Fugitive emissions are often harder to quantify and require engineering estimation. Many facilities are surprised to find their fugitive emissions are larger than their stack emissions.

</details>

**Q96.** A local activist group submits a written request to the LEPC asking for Tier II chemical inventory reports for three industrial facilities in their district. The LEPC chair says it's confidential. Is this correct?

- A) Yes — Tier II data is for government use only
- B) Yes — only LEPC members can access Tier II data
- C) **No — EPCRA's community right-to-know provisions make Tier II information available to any member of the public upon request; the LEPC must provide the information within a reasonable time**
- D) Partially correct — they can access aggregate data but not facility-specific chemical names

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

**Any member of the public** can request Tier II information from the LEPC or SERC under EPCRA's community right-to-know provisions — no explanation, no standing, no proximity requirement necessary. The LEPC must make the information available. The only withholding allowed is for **specific chemical identities claimed as trade secrets** — but even then, hazard categories and quantities must still be disclosed.

**Q97.** A small dry cleaning shop has 6 employees, uses 8,000 lbs per year of perchloroethylene (PCE — a TRI-listed chemical), and is in a covered NAICS code. Must they file Form R?

- A) Yes — PCE is a high-priority TRI chemical requiring filing at any quantity
- B) **No — the business fails two TRI criteria simultaneously: fewer than 10 employees AND below the 10,000-lb "otherwise use" threshold for PCE**
- C) Yes — any dry cleaner using PCE must file Form R
- D) No — small businesses are categorically exempt from TRI

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: B**

This business fails **two** of the three TRI criteria:
- **Employees:** 6 < 10 required ❌
- **Chemical threshold:** 8,000 lbs PCE < 10,000-lb "otherwise use" threshold ❌
- Industry: covered NAICS code ✓

With two criteria unmet, no TRI obligation exists. Important context: despite no TRI obligation, dry cleaner PCE contamination of soil and groundwater is one of the most common Superfund problems in the US — because small dry cleaners operated for decades with poor solvent disposal practices. TRI reporting and CERCLA cleanup liability are separate questions.

</details>

**Q98.** An LEPC member asks at a meeting: *"What's the point of the emergency plan if it just sits in a binder? Nobody reads it."* What is the correct answer about why the LEPC plan matters?

- A) It satisfies a federal filing requirement — the practical value is secondary
- B) It serves as legal protection for the LEPC in case of lawsuits
- C) **The plan's value is the PREPARATION it forces: identifying who has what chemicals where, establishing notification procedures, training responders, conducting exercises — so that when an emergency occurs at 2 AM, everyone already knows their role**
- D) The plan is primarily a public relations document for community reassurance

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

A LEPC emergency plan is only as valuable as the **preparation it drives** and the **exercises that test it**. The plan identifies EHS facilities, maps evacuation routes, establishes who calls whom, inventories emergency equipment, and trains responders. An LEPC that meets once a year to "adopt the plan" without conducting exercises, updating chemical inventories, or coordinating with facility emergency coordinators has a plan that will fail under real conditions. Annual review and periodic exercises (tabletop and full-scale) are required by EPCRA for a reason.

</details>

**Q99.** A company wants to qualify for a de minimis settlement at a Superfund site, claiming they sent only 0.3% of the total waste volume. EPA asks for documentation. Which is the strongest form of evidence?

- A) A signed affidavit from the company's CEO attesting to the small quantity
- B) Testimony from an employee who worked in the shipping department at the time
- C) **Original waste manifests, waste characterization records, and transporter shipping documents contemporaneously created at the time of disposal — showing the specific quantity, waste description, and destination facility**
- D) The company's accounting records showing disposal costs paid

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: C**

**Contemporaneous documentary records** — manifests, waste analysis records, transporter bills of lading, generator logs — are the strongest evidence for de minimis claims because they were created at the time of disposal with no litigation motive. Affidavits and testimony can be challenged as self-serving. Accounting records show costs but not quantities or composition. The manifest is the gold standard because it was signed by multiple parties, required by federal law, and created in real time — making it the most credible record of what was sent and where.

</details>

**Q100.** Which statement BEST captures the relationship between RCRA and CERCLA in the overall U.S. hazardous waste management framework?

- A) They are redundant laws covering the exact same situations — one should be repealed
- B) RCRA covers liquid wastes; CERCLA covers solid wastes — they divide jurisdiction by physical form
- C) RCRA applies to facilities; CERCLA applies only to contaminated land
- D) **RCRA manages hazardous waste prospectively during its active lifecycle (cradle-to-grave); CERCLA provides the legal and financial mechanism to remediate contamination retrospectively when RCRA management failed, didn't exist, or wasn't followed — they are complementary partners in the complete hazardous waste legal framework**

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**✅ Answer: D**

RCRA and CERCLA are the **two pillars of U.S. hazardous waste law**, designed to work together:

- **RCRA** (1976, amended 1984 HSWA): *Prospective* — manages ongoing waste generation, transportation, treatment, storage, and disposal to **prevent** future contamination
- **CERCLA** (1980, amended 1986 SARA): *Retrospective* — cleans up contamination that **already occurred** from past disposal practices

Every CERCLA Superfund site is evidence that RCRA either didn't exist yet, wasn't followed, or wasn't adequate at the time. Strong RCRA compliance today prevents future CERCLA sites. They are partners, not competitors.

</details>
