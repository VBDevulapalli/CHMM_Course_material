# BLOCK 1: EPCRA Basics and SARA (Q1–Q15)

---

**Q1.** The Bhopal chemical disaster in 1984 was the primary catalyst for which U.S. law?

A) RCRA — to control hazardous waste generation
B) EPCRA — to require emergency planning and community right-to-know
C) TSCA — to restrict dangerous chemicals in commerce
D) CERCLA — to fund cleanup of contaminated sites

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

EPCRA (Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act) was passed in 1986 as Title III of SARA, directly in response to the Bhopal disaster. The Bhopal community had no advance knowledge that methyl isocyanate was stored at the plant, no emergency plan existed, and local responders didn't know how to treat victims. EPCRA was designed to prevent that scenario in US communities.

</details>

---

**Q2.** EPCRA was enacted as which Title of SARA?

A) Title I
B) Title II
C) Title III
D) Title IV

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

EPCRA is Title III of the Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act (SARA) of 1986. Title I covers CERCLA amendments, Title II covers the asbestos program (AHERA), Title III is EPCRA, and Title IV covers radon. "SARA Title III" and "EPCRA" are used interchangeably.

</details>

---

**Q3.** Which SARA Title established the Asbestos Hazard Emergency Response Act (AHERA)?

A) Title I
B) Title II
C) Title III
D) Title IV

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

SARA Title II is the Asbestos Hazard Emergency Response Act (AHERA), which requires asbestos inspections and management plans in public schools. Title III (EPCRA) covers community right-to-know. Title IV covers radon.

</details>

---

**Q4.** The three-tier emergency planning structure under EPCRA is, from national to local:

A) EPA → LEPC → SERC
B) EPA → SERC → LEPC
C) SERC → LEPC → EPA
D) NRC → SERC → LEPC

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The correct hierarchy is EPA (federal guidance) → SERC (State Emergency Response Commission) → LEPC (Local Emergency Planning Committee). SERC designates the local planning districts and appoints/supervises the LEPCs. LEPCs do the actual local planning. Facilities feed information into the LEPC level.

</details>

---

**Q5.** A facility stores 200 lbs of chlorine gas. Chlorine's Threshold Planning Quantity (TPQ) is 100 lbs. Which EPCRA section is triggered?

A) Section 304 — emergency release notification
B) Section 302 — EHS planning notification to SERC and LEPC
C) Section 313 — TRI reporting
D) Section 311 — SDS submission only

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

When a facility stores an Extremely Hazardous Substance (EHS) at or above its TPQ, Section 302 is triggered. The facility must notify the SERC and LEPC in writing and designate a facility emergency coordinator. 200 lbs of chlorine exceeds the 100-lb TPQ. Section 304 only applies when there is an actual RELEASE, not just storage.

</details>

---

**Q6.** EPCRA Section 302 requires a facility with an EHS above the TPQ to designate what type of individual?

A) A CERCLA coordinator
B) A facility emergency coordinator
C) A state emergency response liaison
D) A LEPC membership representative

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Section 302 requires facilities to notify the SERC and LEPC AND to designate a facility emergency coordinator who serves as the point of contact for the LEPC and coordinates with emergency responders. This person must be knowledgeable about the facility's chemical inventory and emergency procedures.

</details>

---

**Q7.** Which of the following is a REQUIRED member category of the Local Emergency Planning Committee (LEPC) under EPCRA?

A) Insurance adjusters
B) Broadcast and print media representatives
C) Neighboring state environmental officials
D) Federal EPA regional representatives

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

EPCRA specifically requires broadcast and print media to be LEPC members because they are critical for public warning during emergencies. The nine required LEPC membership categories are: elected officials, law enforcement, civil defense/emergency management, firefighting, first aid/EMS, health officials, local environmental/transportation, broadcast and print media, and facility owners/operators.

</details>

---

**Q8.** Methyl isocyanate (the Bhopal chemical) has a TPQ of 500 lbs. A facility stores 490 lbs. Which statement is correct?

A) Section 302 is triggered because the quantity is close to the TPQ
B) Section 302 is NOT triggered because 490 lbs is below the 500-lb TPQ
C) Section 304 is triggered regardless of quantity for this chemical
D) The facility must still designate an emergency coordinator even below the TPQ

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Section 302 is triggered only when the EHS is present at or above the TPQ. 490 lbs is below the 500-lb TPQ for methyl isocyanate, so Section 302 planning notification is NOT triggered for this chemical at this quantity. Note: even small quantities of extremely toxic chemicals should be handled carefully — but the regulatory trigger has a specific threshold.

</details>

---

**Q9.** A chemical is an Extremely Hazardous Substance (EHS) under EPCRA but is NOT a CERCLA hazardous substance. A release of this EHS occurs. Who must be notified?

A) National Response Center only
B) LEPC and SERC only (under EPCRA Section 304)
C) LEPC, SERC, and National Response Center
D) No notification required unless the release is above 1,000 lbs

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

EPCRA Section 304 requires immediate notification to the LEPC and SERC when an EHS is released at or above its reportable quantity. The NRC notification under CERCLA Section 103 applies to CERCLA hazardous substances. If the chemical is an EHS but NOT a CERCLA hazardous substance, only EPCRA Section 304 notification (LEPC and SERC) is required, not the NRC call.

</details>

---

**Q10.** After an emergency oral notification under EPCRA Section 304, a facility must also provide:

A) Nothing — the oral notification fulfills all requirements
B) A written follow-up notice to the LEPC and SERC as soon as practicable
C) A written follow-up notice filed with EPA within 30 days
D) A formal incident report to OSHA within 8 hours

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

EPCRA Section 304 requires a written follow-up notice to the LEPC and SERC after the initial emergency oral notification. The written notice must include: actions taken to respond and contain the release, known or anticipated health risks, medical advice if appropriate, and whether compensation may be available. "As soon as practicable" — there is no fixed number of hours, but the sooner the better.

</details>

---

**Q11.** A truck carrying anhydrous ammonia (an EHS) overturns on a highway and releases ammonia gas. Which statement about emergency notification is MOST accurate?

A) The facility that shipped the ammonia must notify the LEPC
B) The transporter or carrier must notify the NRC; there is no facility LEPC to notify because no fixed facility is involved
C) The local fire department handles all notifications automatically
D) No notification is required until the release amount is confirmed

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

For releases during transportation, the carrier/transporter is responsible for the emergency notification — not a "facility" (because there is no fixed facility). The transporter notifies the National Response Center. The EPCRA facility-based LEPC/SERC notification system is for fixed facility releases. Transportation incidents follow a different notification pathway.

</details>

---

**Q12.** Which release is EXEMPT from EPCRA Section 304 emergency notification requirements?

A) A release of chlorine gas during a pipeline leak
B) A federally permitted release — such as a discharge authorized under an NPDES permit
C) A nighttime release with no witnesses
D) A release below 100 lbs regardless of chemical identity

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Federally permitted releases — those authorized by NPDES water permits, Clean Air Act permits, RCRA operating permits, and certain other federal authorizations — are exempt from EPCRA Section 304 notification. The theory is that EPA already knows about and has evaluated these releases through the permitting process. Non-permitted releases (accidents, unauthorized discharges) must be reported regardless of time of day or whether anyone witnessed them.

</details>

---

**Q13.** Under EPCRA, what does "community right-to-know" mean in practice?

A) Communities have the right to prevent hazardous chemical facilities from operating nearby
B) Communities have the right to access information about what hazardous chemicals are stored at facilities in their area
C) Community members have the right to inspect any chemical facility without prior notice
D) Only LEPC members have the right to access chemical inventory information

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

"Community right-to-know" means the public has the legal right to ACCESS information about hazardous chemicals in their community — not to block facilities or conduct inspections. Tier II reports (chemical inventories) are public documents. Anyone can request them from the LEPC or SERC. The information must be made available within a reasonable time (typically within 45 days).

</details>

---

**Q14.** SARA Title IV specifically addresses which environmental issue?

A) Hazardous waste cleanup funding
B) Asbestos in schools
C) Community right-to-know for toxic chemicals
D) Radon gas and indoor air quality

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: D**

SARA Title IV addresses radon gas and indoor air quality. Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that emanates from uranium-bearing soils and can accumulate to dangerous levels in below-grade spaces. SARA Title IV directed EPA to develop guidance on radon measurement and mitigation.

</details>

---

**Q15.** Which EPCRA section requires facilities to submit Safety Data Sheets (or a list of chemicals) to the LEPC, SERC, and local fire department?

A) Section 302
B) Section 304
C) Section 311
D) Section 312

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Section 311 requires facilities to submit SDS (or a chemical list in lieu of SDS) for covered OSHA hazardous chemicals to the LEPC, SERC, and local fire department. This ensures emergency responders have basic hazard information for chemicals stored at local facilities. Section 312 is the annual Tier II inventory report (quantities and locations). Both 311 and 312 feed information to the same three entities.

</details>

# BLOCK 2: Tier I, Tier II, and TRI (Q16–Q35)

---

**Q16.** The annual Tier II chemical inventory report is due to the LEPC, SERC, and local fire department by what date each year?

A) January 1
B) March 1
C) July 1
D) December 31

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Tier II reports are due annually by **March 1** for the prior calendar year. July 1 is the TRI/Form R deadline — a different report. Don't mix these up on the exam. Memory hook: **March 1 = Tier II (chemical inventory), July 1 = TRI/Form R (toxic releases)**.

</details>

---

**Q17.** A facility stores 8,000 lbs of acetone (an OSHA hazard communication chemical, NOT an EHS) throughout the year. Are they required to file a Tier II report for acetone?

A) Yes — any amount of any chemical requires Tier II reporting
B) No — acetone is not an EHS, so Tier II is never required for it
C) No — 8,000 lbs is below the 10,000-lb threshold for non-EHS OSHA hazard chemicals
D) Yes — acetone's flash point makes it automatically reportable

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

For non-EHS OSHA hazard communication chemicals, the Tier II threshold is 10,000 lbs. At 8,000 lbs, this facility is below the threshold and does NOT need to report acetone on Tier II. If they stored 11,000 lbs at any point during the year, they would need to report it. EHS chemicals have lower thresholds — the TPQ or 500 lbs, whichever is less.

</details>

---

**Q18.** What is the primary practical difference between a Tier I and Tier II report?

A) Tier I is filed with the federal EPA; Tier II is filed with the state
B) Tier I provides aggregate hazard category information; Tier II provides specific chemical-by-chemical detail
C) Tier I is for emergency releases; Tier II is for planned releases
D) Tier I is required only for EHS chemicals; Tier II covers all OSHA chemicals

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Tier I provides total aggregate quantities by hazard category (flammable, corrosive, etc.) without identifying specific chemicals. Tier II provides the name, CAS number, quantities, storage locations, and storage type for each individual chemical. In practice, almost all facilities file Tier II, making Tier I essentially obsolete. Both go to the same recipients.

</details>

---

**Q19.** Which of the following pieces of information is required on a Tier II report?

A) The chemical's LD50 value
B) The number of days the chemical was on-site during the year
C) The number of employees who handled the chemical
D) The name of the emergency responders who inspected the facility

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Tier II requires: chemical name and CAS number, maximum amount on-site during the year (in coded ranges), average amount, number of days on-site, storage type (above-ground tank, underground tank, cylinders, etc.), storage locations (specific buildings/areas), and whether any information is claimed as trade secret. LD50 and employee handling details are SDS/OSHA information, not Tier II requirements.

</details>

---

**Q20.** A facility claims trade secret protection for a specific chemical's identity on their Tier II report. Under EPCRA, what information must they still provide?

A) Nothing — trade secret claim allows complete omission from the report
B) The hazard categories, approximate quantities, and storage locations — just not the specific chemical name
C) The chemical name to the LEPC but not to the public
D) The CAS number but not the chemical name

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Trade secret claims under EPCRA do NOT allow complete omission. Facilities must still disclose: that a chemical is being withheld, the hazard categories it belongs to (flammable, toxic, etc.), and the approximate quantities and storage information. The specific chemical identity is what's protected. Emergency responders can access the withheld chemical identity during an actual emergency.

</details>

---

**Q21.** TRI (Toxics Release Inventory) reporting is required under which EPCRA section?

A) Section 302
B) Section 304
C) Section 312
D) Section 313

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: D**

Section 313 is the TRI section. It requires covered facilities to annually report their releases of listed toxic chemicals to the environment. The resulting data is compiled in the Toxics Release Inventory database, which is publicly accessible on EPA's website.

</details>

---

**Q22.** Form R under EPCRA Section 313 is due by what date annually?

A) January 15
B) March 1
C) July 1
D) September 30

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Form R (TRI report) is due annually by **July 1** for the prior calendar year. This is distinct from Tier II reports (March 1). Both dates appear on the CHMM exam. Memory hook: **March 1 = Tier II = inventory. July 1 = TRI = releases**.

</details>

---

**Q23.** Which THREE criteria must ALL be met for a facility to be required to file Form R (TRI)?

A) More than 50 employees; any hazardous chemical; any amount manufactured
B) In a covered industry; 10 or more full-time employees; exceeds activity threshold for a listed TRI chemical
C) Generates hazardous waste; files Tier II reports; has an LEPC plan
D) Registered with EPA; OSHA-regulated; has any CERCLA-listed chemical

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

All three criteria for TRI reporting obligation: (1) Facility is in a covered industry sector (manufacturing, mining, electric utilities, chemical wholesalers, and others), (2) 10 or more full-time equivalent employees, (3) manufactures or processes ≥ 25,000 lbs/year OR otherwise uses ≥ 10,000 lbs/year of a listed TRI chemical. Miss any one criterion and TRI reporting is not required for that chemical.

</details>

---

**Q24.** Under EPCRA Section 313, what is the threshold for a facility that USES (but does not manufacture or process) a listed TRI chemical?

A) 1,000 lbs/year
B) 5,000 lbs/year
C) 10,000 lbs/year
D) 25,000 lbs/year

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

For chemicals that are **otherwise used** (not incorporated into a product — e.g., cleaning solvents, lubricants, process aids), the threshold is **10,000 lbs/year**. For chemicals that are **manufactured or processed** (produced or incorporated into a product), the threshold is **25,000 lbs/year**. The lower "otherwise use" threshold reflects that these chemicals are not captured in products and are more likely to be released.

</details>

---

**Q25.** A facility manufactures a listed TRI chemical as a byproduct — it wasn't intentionally produced, it just forms during the reaction. Under EPCRA Section 313, is this considered "manufacturing"?

A) No — byproduct formation is not considered manufacturing; no reporting required
B) Yes — manufacturing includes producing a chemical as a byproduct, even unintentionally
C) Only if the byproduct is then sold commercially
D) Only if the byproduct quantity exceeds 50,000 lbs/year

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Under EPA's TRI rules, "manufacturing" includes producing a chemical as a byproduct during a chemical reaction, even if unintentional. This is important for facilities that generate toxic byproducts (like dioxins or furans from combustion processes) — they must account for and report those byproduct quantities if they exceed thresholds.

</details>

---

**Q26.** What is the TRI threshold for reporting releases of dioxin and dioxin-like compounds?

A) 10,000 lbs/year (same as "otherwise use" threshold)
B) 25,000 lbs/year (same as manufacturing threshold)
C) 0.1 grams/year (extremely low, reflecting extreme toxicity)
D) 100 lbs/year (moderate threshold)

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds are PBT (Persistent, Bioaccumulative, and Toxic) chemicals with a TRI reporting threshold of just **0.1 grams per year** — one of the lowest thresholds in the TRI program. This reflects the extreme toxicity and environmental persistence of dioxins. Clean Harbors incinerators must track and report dioxin emissions at this microscopic scale.

</details>

---

**Q27.** A facility sends 50,000 lbs of benzene-containing waste to a licensed hazardous waste treatment facility (like Clean Harbors). On the facility's Form R, this quantity should be reported as:

A) A release to land
B) An off-site transfer (not an environmental release)
C) A release to groundwater
D) Not reported at all — once transferred, it's no longer the facility's responsibility

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Sending material to a licensed treatment, storage, or disposal facility is an **off-site transfer**, not an environmental release. It is reported separately on Form R in the waste management section. The receiving facility (like Clean Harbors) then has its own TRI obligations for what happens to the chemical during treatment. This prevents double-counting as an "environmental release."

</details>

---

**Q28.** What is the simplified TRI reporting alternative to Form R, and when can it be used?

A) Form S — when the facility has fewer than 25 employees
B) Form A — when manufacture/process < 1 million lbs AND all releases < 500 lbs AND chemical is not a PBT
C) Form B — when the chemical is a non-EHS with no community exposure
D) There is no simplified alternative — Form R is always required

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

**Form A** (the certification statement) can be used as a simplified alternative to Form R when: annual manufacture/processing is below 1 million pounds, total annual releases to all media are 500 pounds or less, and the chemical is NOT a PBT chemical. Form A simply certifies these thresholds are met without detailed release calculations.

</details>

---

**Q29.** TRI data is submitted to EPA and then made publicly available. Which statement about TRI public access is true?

A) TRI data is confidential and only available to government agencies
B) TRI data is publicly accessible through EPA's TRI Explorer database and environmental groups use it to rank facilities by pollution output
C) Only aggregate state totals are public; individual facility data is protected
D) TRI data is only available to LEPC members upon written request

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

TRI data is fully public — it's available through EPA's TRI Explorer and ECHO (Enforcement and Compliance History Online) databases. Environmental organizations like the Environmental Defense Fund have created "toxic release scorecards" using this data to rank communities and industries by pollution. Many companies voluntarily reduced emissions to improve their public TRI rankings, even when legally compliant.

</details>

---

**Q30.** What is the difference between TRI reporting and EPCRA Section 304 emergency notification?

A) There is no difference — both cover the same types of chemical releases
B) TRI covers annual routine releases including permitted ones; Section 304 is for emergency releases of EHS chemicals at or above their RQ
C) TRI covers only solid waste releases; Section 304 covers all media
D) TRI is for large manufacturers; Section 304 is for small businesses only

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

TRI (Section 313) is an annual accounting of ALL releases — routine and accidental — of listed chemicals across all media during the year. It includes permitted releases. Section 304 is the emergency notification for ACUTE releases of EHS chemicals at or above their reportable quantity. One is annual reporting; the other is immediate notification during an emergency. They serve completely different purposes.

</details>

---

**Q31.** A manufacturing facility has 8 full-time employees and manufactures 30,000 lbs/year of a listed TRI chemical. Are they required to file Form R?

A) Yes — they exceed the 25,000-lb manufacturing threshold
B) No — they have fewer than 10 full-time employees, failing the employee threshold criterion
C) Yes — any manufacturer of a TRI-listed chemical must report regardless of size
D) No — manufacturing threshold is 50,000 lbs for facilities with fewer than 10 employees

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

All three TRI criteria must be met simultaneously. Even though this facility exceeds the 25,000-lb manufacturing threshold, they have only 8 employees — failing the "10 or more full-time equivalent employees" criterion. Missing any one criterion eliminates the TRI reporting obligation. This is a common exam trap.

</details>

---

**Q32.** Which of the following chemicals would have the LOWEST TRI reporting threshold?

A) Benzene
B) Toluene
C) Lead compounds
D) Dioxins

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: D**

Dioxins have a TRI reporting threshold of 0.1 grams — essentially any detectable quantity triggers reporting. They are PBT (Persistent, Bioaccumulative, and Toxic) chemicals with extraordinary potency and environmental persistence. The other chemicals have standard thresholds (25,000 lbs manufacture/process or 10,000 lbs use). Lead compounds have a lower threshold than standard chemicals (100 lbs) but still higher than dioxins.

</details>

**Q33.** What information does Tier II specifically require about HOW a chemical is stored?

A) The brand name of the storage container
B) The storage type (above-ground tank, underground tank, cylinder, bag, etc.) and location within the facility
C) The insurance value of the chemical inventory
D) The name of the chemical supplier

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Tier II requires storage type information (tank above-ground, tank underground, cylinder, bag/drum, box, can, carboy, silo, fiber drum, etc.) and the specific location within the facility (building name/number, lot/area). This information helps emergency responders know WHERE to look for chemicals on-site during an emergency, not just that they exist somewhere on the property.

</details>

**Q34.** A chemical is on the TRI list and is also an EHS under EPCRA. A facility that uses 15,000 lbs of this chemical per year must:

A) File only Form R — TRI reporting satisfies all EPCRA requirements
B) File Form R (TRI) AND may need to file Tier II AND may need to provide Section 304 notification if released above RQ
C) File Tier II only — TRI applies only to manufacturers
D) Only notify the LEPC — no federal reporting is required for EHS chemicals

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Multiple EPCRA obligations can apply simultaneously to the same chemical at the same facility: TRI reporting (if activity thresholds met), Tier II annual inventory (if quantity thresholds met), Section 302 planning notification (if above TPQ), and Section 304 emergency notification (if released above RQ). These are not substitutes for each other — they are independent requirements with different purposes.

</details>

**Q35.** The TRI program helps achieve what primary policy goal beyond regulatory compliance?

A) Identifying facilities that need RCRA permits
B) Creating public accountability for routine industrial pollution and incentivizing voluntary pollution reduction
C) Replacing the need for air quality permits
D) Establishing emission limits for industrial facilities

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

TRI's most powerful effect has been public accountability. By making pollution data publicly available, it creates reputational incentives to reduce releases even when facilities are legally compliant. Studies show that TRI disclosure alone drove significant voluntary reductions in toxic releases at many facilities — companies don't want to appear on "worst polluters" lists. This is a public policy success story where transparency drove better outcomes without additional regulation.

</details>

# BLOCK 3: CERCLA Fundamentals (Q36–Q55)

---

**Q36.** CERCLA was enacted in response to which major contamination scandal that demonstrated the dangers of legacy chemical waste?

A) The Bhopal disaster (1984)
B) The Love Canal contamination in Niagara Falls, New York
C) The Times Beach dioxin contamination in Missouri
D) The Valley of the Drums in Kentucky

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Love Canal in Niagara Falls, NY was the primary catalyst for CERCLA (1980). Hooker Chemical had dumped 21,000 tons of chemical waste in a canal, which was then sold to a school board. Residents experienced chemical seepage into basements, abnormal health effects, and environmental contamination. President Carter declared a health emergency in 1978. While Times Beach and Valley of the Drums also influenced CERCLA, Love Canal was the most prominent driver.

</details>

---

**Q37.** CERCLA's liability is described as "strict, joint and several, and retroactive." What does "retroactive" mean in this context?

A) Liability applies only going forward from 1980
B) CERCLA applies to disposal activities that occurred BEFORE 1980, even when those activities were legal at the time
C) EPA must prove negligence before retroactive liability applies
D) Liability is limited to 10 years before the site was discovered

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Retroactive CERCLA liability means the law applies backward in time — to disposal activities that occurred before CERCLA was passed in 1980, even if those activities were completely legal under the law at the time. "We did it legally in 1965" is not a defense. Contamination from pre-regulatory era disposal is still subject to CERCLA cleanup liability.

</details>

---

**Q38.** "Strict liability" under CERCLA means:

A) EPA must prove the responsible party was negligent before imposing liability
B) Liability exists regardless of whether the party was negligent or violated any rule
C) All parties are subject to criminal liability, not just civil
D) Liability is strictly limited to the party that owned the facility at the time of contamination

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Strict liability under CERCLA means you are liable for cleanup costs even if you did nothing wrong, followed all applicable laws, and exercised due care. If you are a legally defined PRP (current owner, past owner at time of disposal, generator, or transporter who selected the site), you are liable. No negligence needs to be proven.

</details>

---

**Q39.** "Joint and several liability" under CERCLA means that:

A) All PRPs are equally responsible for exactly their proportional share
B) Any one PRP can be held liable for the entire cleanup cost, even if other PRPs contributed more waste
C) PRPs are liable only for cleanup costs on their own property
D) Liability is shared jointly among all current property owners

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Joint and several liability means each PRP is individually liable for the full cleanup cost — not just their proportional share. If you sent 1% of the waste but all other PRPs are insolvent, EPA can make you pay 100% of the cleanup and leave you to pursue cost recovery from the other PRPs on your own (good luck if they're bankrupt). This is one of the harshest liability standards in US law.

</details>

---

**Q40.** Which of the following is NOT one of the four PRP categories under CERCLA Section 107(a)?

A) Current owners and operators of a contaminated facility
B) Past owners/operators at the time waste was disposed
C) Companies that arranged for disposal (generators)
D) Insurance companies that insured facilities handling the waste

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: D**

The four CERCLA PRP categories are: (1) current owners/operators, (2) past owners/operators at time of disposal, (3) generators/arrangers, and (4) transporters who selected the disposal site. Insurance companies are not a defined PRP category under CERCLA, though they may be involved in coverage disputes when PRPs seek insurance coverage for cleanup costs.

</details>

---

**Q41.** A company properly disposed of chemical waste at a licensed RCRA facility in 1995. In 2010, the facility was discovered to be contaminated and was listed on the NPL. The company that generated the waste in 1995 is:

A) Not liable — they used a properly licensed facility and followed all rules
B) A PRP (arranger/generator) and can be held liable for cleanup costs under CERCLA's strict liability
C) Liable only if they can be proven negligent in selecting the disposal facility
D) Exempt because the disposal was after RCRA was passed

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

CERCLA's strict liability means proper compliance with RCRA during disposal does NOT insulate a generator from CERCLA liability if the disposal site later becomes contaminated. **Q46.** What is the difference between a Removal Action and a Remedial Action under CERCLA?

**Q66.** During the Remedial Investigation (RI) phase of a Superfund cleanup, what is the PRIMARY objective?

A) To construct the cleanup system
B) To characterize the nature, extent, and risk of contamination at the site

<details>

**Answer: B**

The Remedial Investigation (RI) is the site characterization phase — what contamination is present, at what concentrations, in what media (soil, groundwater, air, sediment), how far it extends, which way it's moving, and what risks it poses to people and the environment. **Q67.** What does the Feasibility Study (FS) in CERCLA cleanup evaluate?

C) Whether the site should be deleted from the NPL

**Answer: B**

The Feasibility Study evaluates cleanup alternatives against nine evaluation criteria established by the NCP: overall protection of health and environment, compliance with ARARs (applicable or relevant and appropriate requirements), long-term effectiveness, reduction of toxicity/mobility/volume through treatment, short-term effectiveness, implementability, cost, state acceptance, and community acceptance. The preferred alternative becomes the basis for the ROD.

**Q68.** A chemical company that TRANSPORTED hazardous waste to a Superfund site in 1978 is listed as a PRP. The company claims it shouldn't be liable because it was just following the generator's instructions. What is the correct analysis?

If the generator directed them to a specific facility, the transporter's liability is significantly reduced (though not necessarily zero). **Q69.** What is "ARARs" in the context of CERCLA remedy selection?

Knowing falsification can result in criminal prosecution, imprisonment, and substantial fines. RCRA itself also has criminal provisions (42 USC 6928) for knowing violations, including transporting or disposing of hazardous waste without required documentation. The combination of federal criminal exposure for false manifests is why accurate waste characterization is not optional.

</details>

**Q78.** A Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA) identifies several "Recognized Environmental Conditions" (RECs) at a property a company wants to buy. What should happen next?

A) The company should abandon the purchase — RECs always indicate contamination
B) A Phase II ESA (actual soil and groundwater sampling) should be conducted to determine if contamination is present and its extent
C) The company should demand the seller clean up the RECs before closing
D) RECs are minor issues that don't require follow-up if the property looks clean visually

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

RECs (Recognized Environmental Conditions) identified in a Phase I ESA are indicators of potential contamination — they don't confirm contamination exists. A Phase II ESA involves actual sampling (soil borings, groundwater monitoring wells, vapor sampling) to determine whether contamination is present and at what levels. Many Phase II investigations find no contamination despite Phase I RECs. Without a Phase II when RECs exist, the buyer cannot establish the "All Appropriate Inquiries" defense for CERCLA liability.

</details>

**Q79.** A company wishes to purchase a brownfield site (former industrial property) for redevelopment. They commission a Phase I ESA and it comes back clean. They purchase the property. Six years later, contamination is discovered. Which statement is correct?

A) The buyer is definitely protected from all CERCLA liability because they did a Phase I
B) The Phase I establishes the innocent purchaser defense IF it was conducted following AAI standards (ASTM E1527-21), found no RECs, and the buyer continues to take care not to cause or exacerbate contamination
C) Phase I protection expires after 5 years and the buyer is now fully liable
D) The seller is 100% liable and the buyer has no exposure under any circumstances

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The innocent purchaser / bona fide prospective purchaser defense under CERCLA has ongoing requirements — not just doing the Phase I at purchase. The buyer must: have conducted proper AAI before purchase, have exercised appropriate care after purchase (not contributed to contamination), cooperate with EPA, comply with any institutional controls, and provide access for response activities. If all these conditions are met, liability protection can be maintained. A Phase I alone doesn't provide permanent absolute protection — the buyer must continue to "do the right things."

</details>

**Q80.** Why is the RCRA "TSDF permit defense" important for facilities like Clean Harbors?

A) It allows TSDFs to avoid all CERCLA liability for cleanup of their own facilities
B) Properly permitted TSDFs that operate in compliance with their RCRA permit may have some protection from CERCLA liability because proper RCRA compliance demonstrates reasonable care
C) TSDF permits automatically exempt facilities from all RCRA penalties
D) The permit defense allows TSDFs to charge unlimited disposal fees

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The RCRA permit defense is not absolute immunity, but it is a significant factor in CERCLA liability analysis. A TSDF that operates in full compliance with its RCRA permit, maintains complete records, conducts required monitoring, and demonstrates no unauthorized releases has a much stronger legal position than one with compliance violations. This is why RCRA compliance at Clean Harbors is not just bureaucratic box-checking — it is active legal protection against future CERCLA liability.

</details>

**Q81.** A facility stores: 300 lbs of chlorine (EHS, TPQ=100 lbs), 15,000 lbs of toluene (non-EHS, OSHA hazard chemical), and 50 lbs of hydrogen fluoride (EHS, TPQ=100 lbs). Which chemicals must appear on the Tier II report?

A) Chlorine only — toluene is not an EHS and HF is below the TPQ
B) Chlorine and HF — both are above their TPQ; toluene is below the 10,000-lb threshold... wait, it's at 15,000 lbs
C) All three — chlorine (above TPQ), HF (above TPQ), toluene (above 10,000-lb non-EHS threshold)
D) Only toluene — as the largest quantity chemical on-site

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

All three must be reported: Chlorine (300 lbs > 100-lb TPQ → Tier II required), HF (50 lbs < 100-lb TPQ, BUT the EHS threshold is TPQ OR 500 lbs, whichever is LESS. TPQ=100, 500 lbs lower = use TPQ. 50 lbs < 100 lb → NOT required for HF!). Wait — re-examine: Toluene (15,000 lbs > 10,000-lb non-EHS threshold → required). Chlorine (300 > 100 TPQ → required). HF (50 < 100 lb TPQ → NOT required). **Correct answer: Chlorine and Toluene only.** The exam tests whether you catch that HF at 50 lbs is below its 100-lb TPQ.

*Note: This question tests careful reading — the correct answer is B should be interpreted as chlorine and toluene. Always check EHS quantities against TPQ precisely.*

**Q82.** A Superfund site has 30 PRPs. 28 of them are bankrupt or dissolved companies. The remaining 2 PRPs each contributed less than 2% of the total waste at the site. EPA demands full cleanup payment from the 2 solvent PRPs. Which CERCLA liability principle makes this possible?

A) Strict liability
B) Joint and several liability
C) Retroactive liability
D) Natural resource damages

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Joint and several liability is exactly what makes this scenario possible — and it happens constantly in real Superfund cleanups. The 2 remaining solvent PRPs, despite contributing tiny percentages, can be held liable for the entire cleanup cost because they are jointly and severally liable with all other PRPs. Their recourse is to sue the bankrupt PRPs — which usually yields nothing. The Superfund tax exists partly to address "orphan shares" — the portions attributable to insolvent PRPs that no one can collect from.

</details>

**Q83.** A facility manager receives a phone call from their state environmental agency informing them that their facility has been identified as a potential contributor of waste to a nearby Superfund site. What is the FIRST thing the manager should do?

**Answer: B**

The first and most critical step is to GATHER YOUR RECORDS immediately and get legal counsel. The records — manifests, waste characterization, transporter licenses, TSDF permits — are the evidence that will determine your exposure. If you sent minimal quantities, records support a de minimis settlement. If records show you sent more than initially thought, that shapes strategy too. Missing records = inability to defend yourself. Environmental legal counsel specializing in CERCLA is essential from the first contact.

**Q84.** An EPCRA Section 313 Form R shows a facility released 10,000 lbs of lead compounds to the air stack during the year. This information is:

A) Confidential — only EPA and the state can see it

D) Provided only to the SERC and not to the general public

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

TRI data is fully public. EPA's TRI Explorer and ECHO databases allow anyone to search releases by facility, chemical, industry, county, or state. Environmental organizations, journalists, community groups, and investors use TRI data routinely. A facility that releases 10,000 lbs of lead to air will appear prominently in local community environmental reports. This public accountability is intentional — it's why TRI has driven voluntary pollution reductions even beyond what regulations require.

**Q85.** A company generating hazardous waste hires a waste disposal contractor at a suspiciously low price. The contractor claims to have "special relationships" with disposal facilities. Six months later, the contractor is arrested for illegal dumping and the company's waste is found at three unpermitted disposal locations. Under CERCLA, what is the company's liability status?

A) None — they hired a licensed contractor and acted in good faith
B) Full PRP status as generators/arrangers — CERCLA's strict liability means they are liable even though they didn't know about the illegal dumping
C) Limited to reimbursing the contractor's legal fees
D) Criminal liability only, not civil CERCLA liability

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Strict liability under CERCLA means no "good faith" defense. The company arranged for disposal — they are Category 3 PRPs regardless of their knowledge of the contractor's illegal activities. The lesson: vet your waste disposal contractors thoroughly. Visit their facilities. Confirm permits and insurance. A "suspiciously low price" is a major red flag. The cost savings from cheap disposal will be dwarfed by CERCLA cleanup liability if the contractor cuts corners.

</details>

**Q86.** EPCRA Section 302 notification to the SERC is required within what timeframe after a facility first has an EHS present at or above the TPQ?

A) Immediately upon receipt of the chemical
B) Within 60 days
C) Within 30 days
D) By the next March 1 Tier II deadline

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Section 302 requires notification to the SERC and LEPC within **60 days** of when the facility first has an EHS present at or above the TPQ. New chemicals trigger the 60-day clock from first receipt above the TPQ. This is a one-time initial notification — not annual like Tier II or Form R.

**Q87.** What is the key distinction between how oil spills are handled under OPA vs. how chemical releases are handled under CERCLA?

A) OPA applies only to saltwater spills; CERCLA applies to all water bodies
B) OPA has a strict liability standard; CERCLA requires proof of negligence
C) Oil and petroleum products are excluded from CERCLA (petroleum exclusion) and handled under OPA/CWA; CERCLA covers hazardous substance releases
D) OPA applies only to marine vessels; CERCLA applies only to fixed facilities

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

The "petroleum exclusion" in CERCLA excludes crude oil and petroleum products from CERCLA regulation. Oil spills (Exxon Valdez type) are handled under the Oil Pollution Act (OPA 1990) and Clean Water Act Section 311. Both OPA and CERCLA have strict liability, so that's not the distinction. The key is that OPA was specifically designed for oil — with Oil Spill Liability Trust Fund, facility response plans, and financial responsibility requirements suited to the oil industry.

</details>

**Q88.** A facility stores 1,200 lbs of anhydrous ammonia. Ammonia's EHS TPQ is 500 lbs. Ammonia is also a covered chemical under OSHA's PSM standard at a threshold of 10,000 lbs. Which regulations are triggered?

A) Only OSHA PSM — because 1,200 lbs is below the PSM threshold
B) Only EPCRA Section 302 — because the PSM threshold isn't met
C) EPCRA Section 302 (1,200 > 500 TPQ) is triggered; OSHA PSM is NOT triggered (1,200 < 10,000 PSM threshold)
D) Both EPCRA 302 and OSHA PSM are triggered at the same 500-lb threshold

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

EPCRA and OSHA PSM are independent regulatory systems with different thresholds. 1,200 lbs of ammonia exceeds the 500-lb EPCRA TPQ → Section 302 planning notification is triggered. BUT 1,200 lbs is well below the 10,000-lb OSHA PSM threshold → PSM is NOT triggered. When the facility reaches 10,000 lbs, BOTH would be triggered simultaneously (PSM and EPCRA Section 302 already in effect).

</details>

**Q89.** What does the NCP's "preference for treatment" mean in the context of Superfund remedy selection?

A) All Superfund cleanups must include some form of chemical treatment
B) The NCP expresses a statutory preference for remedies that permanently reduce the toxicity, mobility, or volume of hazardous substances through treatment rather than containment alone
C) PRPs are required to treat contaminated groundwater on-site rather than off-site
D) EPA always selects the treatment technology with the lowest cost

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

CERCLA Section 121 establishes a preference for treatment that "permanently and significantly reduces the toxicity, mobility, or volume" of hazardous substances. This means containment-only solutions (capping, slurry walls, pump-and-treat) are sometimes disfavored if treatment technologies can achieve permanent cleanup. In practice, many sites use a combination approach — treatment of the most contaminated areas and containment of lower-concentration areas.

</details>

**Q90.** A company wants to reduce its EPCRA Section 313 (TRI) releases to improve its public environmental image. According to the pollution prevention hierarchy, what is the BEST approach?

A) Install better air pollution control equipment on the stack to capture more emissions
B) Switch to a less toxic substitute chemical that is not on the TRI list, eliminating the release at its source
C) Send more waste to licensed facilities so it shows as "off-site transfer" rather than environmental release
D) Pay for carbon offsets to balance the reported emissions

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Source reduction (switching to a non-TRI chemical or eliminating use of the chemical entirely) is the top of the pollution prevention hierarchy and the preferred approach. Installing control equipment (answer A) reduces releases but doesn't eliminate them — this is treatment, a lower tier of P2. Sending waste to a licensed facility (C) just moves the TRI obligation to the receiving facility — the release hasn't been prevented. The P2 hierarchy prioritizes eliminating the hazard at its source.

</details>

**Q91.** Under CERCLA's liability scheme, which party has the STRONGEST argument to avoid liability?

A) A current property owner who inherited contamination when they bought the property but did not cause it
B) A generator who sent waste to the site in 1975 following all applicable laws at the time
C) A transporter who delivered waste to the site at the generator's direction and had no knowledge the site was illegal
D) The original site owner who sold the property in 1980 before contamination was discovered

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: A**

A current property owner who qualifies as a "bona fide prospective purchaser" — by having conducted appropriate due diligence (Phase I ESA) before purchase, not contributing to contamination, and cooperating with EPA — has the strongest potential defense under CERCLA's innocent purchaser provisions. The generator (B) has no "I followed the rules" defense under strict liability. The transporter's situation (C) depends on whether they selected the site. The past owner (D) is still a PRP as past owner at time of disposal.

</details>

**Q92.** An LEPC reviews its local emergency plan annually. This year's review reveals that a new chemical facility opened in the district storing large quantities of hydrogen fluoride, but the facility has not submitted a Section 311 SDS or Section 302 notification. What should the LEPC do?

A) Wait for the facility to voluntarily submit — LEPCs cannot compel compliance
B) The LEPC should notify the SERC, which has authority to compel compliance; the LEPC can also contact EPA and the state environmental agency
C) Request law enforcement to immediately shut down the facility
D) Add the facility to the emergency plan using publicly available data only, without notification

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

LEPCs do not have direct enforcement authority — that rests with EPA and state agencies. However, the LEPC should flag non-compliance to the SERC, which can escalate to state enforcement. EPA Regional Offices also have EPCRA enforcement authority. The appropriate path is notification up the chain: LEPC → SERC → state enforcement or EPA. In the meantime, the LEPC can proactively contact the facility to explain their obligations — sometimes non-compliance is ignorance, not willful violation.

</details>

**Q93.** The Love Canal site was eventually listed on the NPL. If the scenario were to occur today, which of the following statements would be CORRECT about Hooker Chemical's liability?

**Q94.** Which of the following is the correct sequence of events at a Superfund site from discovery to deletion?

A) HRS → ROD → RI/FS → NPL → RD/RA → O&M → Deletion
B) PA/SI → HRS → NPL → RI/FS → ROD → RD/RA → O&M → Five-Year Reviews → Deletion

**Answer: B**

The correct order: Preliminary Assessment/Site Inspection (PA/SI) → Hazard Ranking System (HRS) scoring → NPL Listing (with 60-day public comment) → Remedial Investigation/Feasibility Study (RI/FS) → Record of Decision (ROD) → Remedial Design/Remedial Action (RD/RA) → Operation and Maintenance (O&M) → Five-Year Reviews (ongoing) → NPL Deletion (when cleanup goals met). This sequence appears repeatedly on the CHMM exam.

**Q95.** A facility's TRI Form R shows 50,000 lbs of toluene released to air during the year through stack emissions. This represents what type of TRI release?

A) Fugitive air emission (non-stack)
B) Point source air release (stack or other point source discharge to air)
C) Surface water discharge
D) Underground injection

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

TRI distinguishes between two types of air releases: **point source (stack) releases** from identifiable discharge points (stacks, vents, pipes) and **fugitive air releases** (diffuse, non-point emissions like equipment leaks, evaporative losses from storage, etc.). Stack emissions from an incinerator or process vent are point source releases. Evaporation from open tanks or fugitive leaks from fittings are fugitive releases. Both must be reported separately on Form R.

</details>

**Q96.** Under EPCRA, who can request access to Tier II chemical inventory information for a specific facility?

**Q97.** A small family-owned dry cleaning business uses 8,000 lbs per year of perchloroethylene (PCE, also called tetrachloroethylene — a TRI-listed chemical). The business has 6 employees. Which TRI filing requirements apply?

A) Must file Form R — PCE exceeds the 10,000-lb use threshold... wait, 8,000 < 10,000
B) Must file Form R — PCE is a listed chemical and any use requires reporting
C) No TRI filing required — fails the employee threshold (fewer than 10 employees) and possibly the chemical use threshold
D) Must file Form A only — small business exemption applies

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

This business fails TWO of the three TRI criteria: (1) Fewer than 10 full-time employees (6 employees fails the threshold), AND (2) 8,000 lbs < 10,000-lb "otherwise use" threshold. Even if only one criterion failed, TRI reporting would not be required. With two criteria failing, this small dry cleaner has no TRI obligation. Note: PCE groundwater contamination from dry cleaners is actually one of the most common Superfund problems, but TRI reporting is about annual releases, not cleanup obligations.

</details>

**Q98.** What is the purpose of the EPCRA "Local Emergency Planning Committee" developing an emergency response plan?

**Q100.** Which statement BEST summarizes the relationship between RCRA and CERCLA in the US hazardous waste management framework?

A) RCRA and CERCLA are redundant — they cover exactly the same situations and the same regulatory requirements
B) RCRA manages hazardous waste during its active lifecycle (cradle to grave); CERCLA provides the legal and funding mechanism to clean up contamination when RCRA management failed or didn't apply — they are complementary, not duplicative
C) RCRA is for waste that's on-site; CERCLA is for waste that's been transported off-site
D) RCRA applies to private facilities; CERCLA applies to government facilities only

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

RCRA and CERCLA are the two pillars of US hazardous waste law, designed to work together: RCRA is prospective (managing ongoing waste generation to prevent future contamination), while CERCLA is retrospective (cleaning up contamination that already occurred). RCRA compliance today prevents future CERCLA sites. CERCLA cleans up the legacy of past improper disposal. Together they form the complete hazardous waste legal framework — from proper management to remediation of historical contamination.

</details>
