**Q1.** The Occupational Safety and Health Act was signed into law on December 29, 1970, and became effective on which date — now designated "Workers Memorial Day"?

> A) January 1, 1971
> B) April 28, 1971
> C) July 4, 1971
> D) December 29, 1971

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The OSH Act became effective **April 28, 1971.** This date is now recognized as Workers Memorial Day in the United States, honoring workers killed on the job. The exam sometimes asks about this date and its significance.

</details>

---

**Q2.** Which of the following entities is NOT covered under OSHA jurisdiction?

A) A privately owned chemical manufacturing plant
B) A self-employed plumber with no employees
C) A federal government agency workplace
D) Employees of a private hospital

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

OSHA does not cover self-employed individuals with no employees — there is no "worker" to protect. Federal agencies are covered under Executive Order 12196 (must comply with OSHA standards). Private sector workers including hospital employees are covered. Family farms with only immediate family members are also exempt.

</details>

**Q3.** A state has an OSHA-approved State Plan. Which statement correctly describes the relationship between the State Plan and federal OSHA?

A) State Plans must be identical to federal OSHA standards
B) State Plans must be "at least as effective" as federal OSHA but may be more stringent
C) State Plans replace federal OSHA entirely and federal standards no longer apply
D) State Plans only cover public sector employees; private sector follows federal OSHA

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

OSHA-approved State Plans must be "at least as effective" as the federal OSHA program — but they can be MORE stringent. California's Cal/OSHA, for example, has requirements that exceed federal OSHA in many areas. In a State Plan state, workers follow the state plan, not federal OSHA directly.

</details>

**Q4.** The General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1)) can be cited by OSHA when:

A) Any workplace injury occurs, regardless of whether a standard was violated
B) A recognized hazard causing or likely to cause death or serious harm exists, no specific standard covers it, and feasible abatement is possible
C) An employer has more than 100 employees in a high-hazard industry
D) An employee files a formal complaint about workplace conditions

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The General Duty Clause requires four elements: (1) a hazard exists, (2) the hazard is recognized, (3) the hazard causes or is likely to cause death or serious physical harm, and (4) there is a feasible means to eliminate or control the hazard. It's the catch-all for hazards not covered by a specific standard.

</details>

**Q5.** NIOSH differs from OSHA in that NIOSH:

A) Enforces workplace safety regulations and issues citations
B) Conducts research and makes recommendations but does not have enforcement authority
C) Is the appeals body for contested OSHA citations
D) Regulates only chemical manufacturers, not general industry

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

NIOSH (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health) is a research agency within CDC/HHS. It conducts research, publishes Recommended Exposure Limits (RELs), and makes recommendations to OSHA. It does NOT write or enforce regulations. OSHA enforces. OSHRC hears appeals. NIOSH researches.

</details>

**Q6.** A new chemical is introduced into a manufacturing process. No specific OSHA standard exists for it, but scientific literature documents significant respiratory hazard at occupational exposure levels. Workers begin developing respiratory symptoms. Which OSHA provision can be used to cite the employer?

A) The employer cannot be cited because there is no specific standard
B) Section 5(a)(1) — the General Duty Clause
C) 29 CFR 1910.1000 — the Air Contaminants standard
D) 29 CFR 1910.134 — Respiratory Protection (automatic application)

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The General Duty Clause applies when there is a recognized hazard (recognized by scientific literature and the industry) causing harm, no specific standard covers it, and abatement is feasible. The absence of a specific standard does NOT mean the employer has no obligation to protect workers. The GDC is how OSHA addresses emerging hazards.

</details>

**Q7.** Section 11(c) of the OSH Act protects employees who:

A) Refuse to work due to any safety concern, however minor
B) File OSHA complaints, participate in OSHA inspections, or exercise other rights under the OSH Act
C) Are injured on the job and file workers' compensation claims
D) Belong to a union and report safety concerns to their union representative

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Section 11(c) is OSHA's anti-retaliation (whistleblower) provision. Employers cannot discharge or discriminate against employees for filing OSHA complaints, participating in inspections, exercising any rights under the OSH Act, or refusing to work in conditions of imminent danger (under specific circumstances). Workers' compensation is state law, not directly Section 11(c).

</details>

**Q8.** The Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission (OSHRC) serves which function?

A) Issues citations and penalties for OSHA violations
B) Conducts research on occupational health risks
C) Serves as the independent adjudicatory body that hears appeals of contested OSHA citations
D) Writes new OSHA standards and regulations

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

OSHRC is the independent quasi-judicial body that hears and rules on cases when employers contest OSHA citations. It is separate from both OSHA (enforcement) and NIOSH (research). If an employer files a Notice of Contest within 15 working days of receiving a citation, the case goes to OSHRC.

</details>

**Q9.** An employer receives an OSHA citation on March 15. What is the deadline to contest the citation?

A) 30 calendar days from March 15
B) 15 working days from receipt of the citation
C) 60 days from March 15
D) 15 calendar days from March 15

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Employers have **15 WORKING DAYS** (not calendar days) from receipt of the citation to file a Notice of Contest. Missing this deadline makes the citation final and non-appealable. Note: "working days" excludes weekends and federal holidays.

</details>

---

**Q10.** Which citation type carries the highest possible OSHA penalty?

A) Serious violation
B) Other-than-serious violation
C) Willful violation
D) Repeated violation

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Willful violations carry the highest penalties — up to $165,514 per violation (2024 figures). A willful violation is one where the employer intentionally and knowingly committed the violation, or was aware that a hazardous condition existed and made no reasonable effort to eliminate it. Repeated violations can match the willful maximum. Serious violations max at approximately $16,550.

</details>

---

**Q11.** An employer's failure to correct a previously cited violation beyond the abatement date is classified as which type of OSHA violation?

A) Willful
B) Repeated
C) Failure to Abate
D) Other-than-serious

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Failure to Abate means the employer did not correct a previously cited violation within the time period specified in the citation. Penalties can be up to $16,550 per day beyond the abatement date — this can accumulate rapidly. A Repeated violation is when the same or similar violation is found at a subsequent inspection within 5 years.

</details>

---

**Q12.** OSHA inspections are prioritized. Which type of inspection receives the HIGHEST priority?

A) Planned/programmed inspection
B) Complaint investigation
C) Imminent danger situation
D) Follow-up to a previous citation

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

OSHA's inspection priority order: (1) Imminent danger — highest priority, (2) Fatality/catastrophe investigation, (3) Complaint investigation, (4) Referral, (5) Planned/programmed, (6) Follow-up. Imminent danger gets immediate attention because death or serious injury is expected to occur before normal enforcement can address the hazard.

</details>

**Q13.** Under what specific conditions can a worker legally refuse to perform work under the OSH Act?

A) Any time the worker feels uncomfortable with the task assigned
B) When the worker believes the condition poses imminent danger of death or serious injury, a reasonable person would agree, the employer was notified and didn't fix it, and there wasn't time for OSHA to respond
C) When the worker's union representative has advised refusal
D) Only after a formal OSHA complaint has been filed

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The right to refuse unsafe work under OSHA has four specific conditions: (1) genuine belief of imminent danger of death or serious injury, (2) a reasonable person in the same situation would agree, (3) the hazard was reported to the employer who failed to correct it, (4) insufficient time to get OSHA to investigate. All four must be present. Mere discomfort or preference is not sufficient.

</details>

**Q14.** The OSHA 300A Annual Summary must be posted in the workplace from:

A) January 1 to March 31
B) February 1 to April 30
C) March 1 to June 30
D) November 1 to December 31

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The OSHA 300A Annual Summary must be posted from **February 1 to April 30** each year. It must be posted in a conspicuous location where employee notices are customarily placed. The 300A is signed and certified by a company executive (owner, officer, or highest-ranking company official). Workers have the right to review the 300 Log at any time.

</details>

**Q15.** A worker is cut on the job. The company nurse cleans the wound, applies a bandage, and gives the worker two Advil tablets (over-the-counter). The worker returns to full duty the same day. Is this incident OSHA recordable?

A) Yes — any injury requiring medical attention is recordable
B) No — treatment consisted only of first aid (cleaning, bandaging, OTC medication at nonprescription dose) and the worker returned to full duty
C) Yes — any cut that requires cleaning is recordable
D) No — only injuries causing lost time are recordable

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

This incident is **not recordable**. Cleaning a wound, applying a bandage, and administering over-the-counter medication at nonprescription strength are all first aid treatments — not medical treatment. The OSHA recordkeeping standard lists specific first aid treatments that do NOT make an incident recordable. If the nurse had prescribed a stronger medication or if stitches were needed, it would be recordable.

</details>

# BLOCK 2: HAZWOPER (Q16–Q40)

---

**Q16.** HAZWOPER is codified in which OSHA regulation?

A) 29 CFR 1910.119
B) 29 CFR 1910.134
C) 29 CFR 1910.120
D) 29 CFR 1910.146

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

HAZWOPER (Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response) is **29 CFR 1910.120** for general industry. The construction industry equivalent is **29 CFR 1926.65**. 1910.119 is PSM, 1910.134 is respiratory protection, and 1910.146 is permit-required confined spaces.

</details>

---

**Q17.** A worker at a Superfund cleanup site notices a drum leaking an unknown liquid and immediately runs to a safe distance and calls 911. He does not attempt to identify the substance or stop the leak. This behavior is consistent with which HAZWOPER training level?

A) First Responder Operations (FRO)
B) Hazardous Materials Technician (HMT)
C) First Responder Awareness (FRA)
D) On-Scene Incident Commander (IC)

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

First Responder Awareness (FRA) trains workers to recognize and report hazardous substance releases — nothing more. "Recognize, retreat, report" is the FRA motto. FRO workers would take defensive action (contain from perimeter). HMT workers would attempt to stop the release. The worker correctly did not attempt to intervene — appropriate FRA response.

</details>

---

**Q18.** What is the minimum HAZWOPER training requirement for a First Responder Operations (FRO) level worker?

A) Competency-based — no specific hour requirement
B) 8 hours minimum
C) 24 hours minimum
D) 40 hours minimum

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

FRO training requires a minimum of **8 hours**. FRA is competency-based with no specific hour minimum. HMT and HMS both require 24 hours of training at the prior level. The 40-hour HAZWOPER training is associated with uncontrolled hazardous waste site cleanup workers — a separate requirement under 1910.120(e).

</details>

---

**Q19.** Workers assigned to a hazardous waste site cleanup who have not yet completed full HAZWOPER training may still participate in site activities if they are:

A) Supervised by a trained coworker at all times
B) Under the direct supervision of a trained, experienced supervisor (on-the-job training while receiving required training)
C) Wearing Level A PPE at all times
D) Restricted to the cold zone only for the duration of their employment

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

HAZWOPER 1910.120(e)(3) allows workers to participate in site activities before completing all required training if they are under the direct supervision of a trained, experienced supervisor. This is the on-the-job training provision. It does not mean unsupervised participation — direct supervision is the key requirement.

</details>

---

**Q20.** How often must HAZWOPER-trained workers at the FRO level and above receive refresher training?

A) Every 2 years
B) Every 5 years
C) Annually (every 12 months) — 8 hours minimum
D) Only when new chemicals are introduced to the site

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

HAZWOPER requires **annual 8-hour refresher training** for all workers trained at FRO level and above. This maintains competency, introduces regulatory updates, and reinforces safety practices. The annual requirement ensures skills don't atrophy between training cycles.

</details>

---

**Q21.** Under HAZWOPER, when must a Site Safety and Health Plan (SSHP) be developed?

A) Only when chemical concentrations exceed the PEL
B) Before beginning any hazardous waste cleanup operations at an uncontrolled site
C) Within 30 days after site operations begin
D) Only when workers will be using Level A PPE

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The SSHP must be developed **before operations begin** at an uncontrolled hazardous substance site. It is a pre-operational planning document, not a reactive document. No HAZWOPER workers should enter a contaminated site without a completed and available SSHP.

</details>

---

**Q22.** Which of the following is NOT a required element of a HAZWOPER Site Safety and Health Plan?

A) Emergency response procedures
B) PPE requirements by zone and task
C) List of chemical suppliers for the site
D) Air monitoring plan

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

A list of chemical suppliers is not a required SSHP element. Required elements include: site description, hazard evaluation, employee training requirements, PPE requirements, site control measures, medical surveillance program, air monitoring plan, decontamination procedures, emergency response procedures, confined space procedures (if applicable), and spill containment.

</details>

---

**Q23.** A hazardous materials technician approaches a leaking chemical storage tank with the intent to close a valve and stop the leak. This type of response is best described as:

A) Defensive response
B) Offensive response
C) Awareness-level response
D) Support-level response

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Going to the source of a release to stop it (closing a valve, plugging a leak, shutting off a process) is **offensive response** — the hallmark of the Hazardous Materials Technician (HMT) level. Defensive response (FRO) means containing from a distance without approaching the source. Awareness (FRA) means recognizing and reporting only.

</details>

**Q24.** Under HAZWOPER medical surveillance requirements, who must receive medical examinations?

A) Only workers who have experienced a chemical exposure incident
B) All site workers regardless of exposure potential
C) Workers exposed at or above the action level for 30+ days/year, workers wearing respirators 30+ days/year, emergency responders to significant releases, and HAZMAT team members
D) Only Site Safety Officers and Incident Commanders

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

HAZWOPER medical surveillance applies to: workers exposed at or above the action level (or PEL if no action level) for 30 or more days per year; workers required to wear respirators for 30 or more days per year; workers involved in emergency response to hazardous substance releases involving significant exposures; and members of HAZMAT teams. Medical surveillance must be provided at no cost to the employee.

</details>

**Q25.** A physician conducting HAZWOPER medical surveillance determines that a worker has a cardiac condition that limits their ability to wear a SCBA. Which information can the physician share with the employer?

A) The specific cardiac diagnosis and treatment plan
B) Whether the worker is fit, not fit, or fit with restrictions — but NOT the specific diagnosis
C) The complete medical record including all conditions found during examination
D) Only that the worker is fit or not fit, with no additional information

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

HAZWOPER medical surveillance maintains medical confidentiality. The physician provides a written opinion that includes: (1) whether the worker has any medical conditions that place them at increased risk, (2) any recommended limitations on work or PPE use, (3) a statement that the worker has been informed of results. The specific diagnosis is NOT shared with the employer — medical privacy is protected.

</details>

---

**Q26.** The Hot Zone in a HAZWOPER operation is also called:

A) The Support Zone
B) The Contamination Reduction Zone
C) The Exclusion Zone
D) The Command Zone

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Hot Zone = **Exclusion Zone** (area of actual contamination, highest hazard, maximum PPE required).
Warm Zone = Contamination Reduction Zone (CRZ) — decontamination happens here.
Cold Zone = Support Zone — command post, staging, clean area.

</details>

---

**Q27.** Personnel moving from the hot zone to the cold zone must first pass through:

A) The cold zone command post for clearance
B) The decontamination station in the warm zone
C) A medical monitoring station in the cold zone
D) The exclusion zone perimeter check point only

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Workers ALWAYS move from hot → warm → cold, with decontamination occurring in the **warm zone (Contamination Reduction Zone)** before entering the cold zone. Bypassing decon and taking contamination into the cold zone contaminates the command post, medical station, and equipment storage — this cascading contamination is exactly what work zones are designed to prevent.

</details>

**Q28.** An emergency responder is working at a scene involving an unknown chemical spill. The concentration is unknown and skin absorption is suspected. Which PPE level is initially MOST appropriate until the hazard is characterized?

A) Level D — work uniform
B) Level C — APR with splash protection
C) Level B — non-encapsulating suit with SCBA
D) Level A — fully encapsulating suit with SCBA inside

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: D**

When the chemical is unknown AND skin absorption is suspected as a potential hazard, **Level A** is indicated — it provides maximum protection against skin contact with unknown vapors and liquids. The encapsulating vapor-tight suit protects against everything until the hazard is characterized. Once the hazard is known and skin absorption is found not to be a significant concern, the PPE level can be downgraded through the proper process (documented air monitoring, hazard assessment).

</details>

**Q29.** The primary difference between Level A and Level B PPE is:

A) Level A uses a SCBA; Level B uses an APR
B) Level A uses an encapsulating vapor-tight suit; Level B uses a non-encapsulating suit — both use SCBA
C) Level A provides more respiratory protection; Level B provides more skin protection
D) Level B is used outdoors; Level A is for indoor use only

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Both Level A and Level B require positive-pressure SCBA for respiratory protection. The key difference is the **suit**: Level A uses a fully encapsulating, vapor-tight chemical protective suit (SCBA worn inside the suit), providing maximum skin/vapor protection. Level B uses a non-encapsulating suit (SCBA worn outside), providing splash protection but not vapor-tight skin protection.

</details>

**Q30.** Which condition MUST be met to use Level C PPE (air-purifying respirator)?

A) The contaminant concentration must be at or above the IDLH
B) Oxygen must be within the normal range (19.5-23.5%), the contaminant must be identified and filterable, and the concentration must be known and below IDLH
C) The skin hazard must be classified as severe
D) A HAZMAT team must be present at the scene

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Level C (APR) requires ALL of these conditions simultaneously: (1) oxygen is within normal range — APRs cannot supply oxygen, (2) the contaminant is identified — you need to know what cartridge to use, (3) the contaminant can be filtered by available cartridges, (4) the concentration is below IDLH — above IDLH requires atmosphere-supplying respirators (SCBA). Any of these conditions failing disqualifies Level C.

</details>

**Q31.** What is the correct action if air monitoring at a site shows that benzene concentrations have risen above the IDLH during a work operation?

A) Continue work but switch from half-face to full-face APR
B) Immediately evacuate the hot zone — IDLH conditions require SCBA and all personnel without SCBA must exit immediately
C) Notify the site safety officer and continue until their inspection is complete
D) Increase the frequency of air monitoring and note the readings in the log

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

IDLH conditions require positive-pressure SCBA — period. Personnel wearing APRs (Level C) in an area that has crossed into IDLH territory must evacuate immediately. APRs do not provide adequate protection at IDLH concentrations. Entry into IDLH conditions without SCBA is a life-threatening situation. Evacuation is the only appropriate response for personnel without SCBA.

</details>

**Q32.** HAZWOPER's emergency response provisions apply to which types of incidents?

A) Only incidents at designated RCRA hazardous waste facilities
B) Emergency response to releases of hazardous substances regardless of where the release occurs — including industrial facilities, transportation accidents, and other locations

HAZWOPER's emergency response section (1910.120(q)) applies to ANY emergency response involving hazardous substances — at industrial plants, on highways, at rail yards, at municipal facilities. **Q33.** What role does the Site Safety Officer (SSO) play under HAZWOPER?

B) The SSO is responsible for developing and implementing the Site Safety and Health Plan, conducting pre-entry briefings, monitoring safety conditions, and stopping work when unsafe conditions are identified

The Site Safety Officer (SSO) is a company-designated person responsible for overseeing the SSHP, conducting safety briefings, monitoring site conditions, and stopping operations if an unsafe condition develops. **Q34.** Under HAZWOPER, what is the "buddy system" and why is it required?

A) Two workers share one SCBA to conserve air
B) Workers in the hot zone must work in pairs so that if one worker is incapacitated, the other can provide immediate assistance or initiate rescue
C) The buddy system requires one worker to monitor atmospheric conditions while the other works
D) Two workers alternate between the hot zone on 15-minute shifts

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

HAZWOPER requires a buddy system — no worker enters the hot zone alone. If a worker is overcome by chemical exposure, heat stress, or a slip/fall inside an SCBA, their buddy can provide immediate assistance or initiate rescue. The buddy system is the first layer of emergency response for workers in the hot zone. This pairs with the backup personnel requirement (trained personnel with appropriate PPE standing by at the hot/warm zone boundary ready to rescue).

</details>

**Q35.** The HAZWOPER standard requires that an emergency response plan include which element specifically addressing personnel who will be responsible for rescue?

A) A list of all chemical substances that might be present
B) Identification of the local hospital and its address
C) Identification of emergency response personnel and their training, and how rescue will be performed
D) Contact information for EPA and OSHA regional offices

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

The emergency response plan under HAZWOPER must include information on who will perform rescue, their training level, what equipment is available for rescue (including PPE at the rescue team's level), and the procedures for rescue operations. This ensures that rescue capability exists before it's needed — not assembled from scratch during an incident.

</details>

**Q36.** HAZWOPER requires backup personnel to be stationed at a hazardous waste site operation. What is their primary purpose?

A) To monitor air quality for the workers in the hot zone
B) Trained personnel with appropriate PPE who can immediately respond if hot zone workers need assistance or rescue
C) Administrative staff who document safety observations
D) Medical personnel who treat injuries after workers exit the hot zone

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Backup personnel (sometimes called "standby rescuers") must be stationed at the hot zone boundary with appropriate PPE and ready to immediately enter the hot zone if workers inside need rescue. They must be at the same PPE level as the workers they might rescue — you cannot send Level D workers to rescue Level A workers. The backup requirement is part of why HAZWOPER operations require significant personnel resources.

</details>

**Q37.** Under HAZWOPER, what is the purpose of the pre-entry safety briefing?

A) A legal formality required before any hazardous waste work begins
B) A mandatory communication that ensures all workers understand the specific hazards, PPE requirements, communication signals, action levels, and emergency procedures for that day's tasks before entering the hot zone
C) A briefing for management and clients about project progress
D) Training required only for workers new to the site

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The pre-entry (tailgate) safety briefing is a required operational communication before each work period or shift. It covers: the specific tasks for that day, chemical hazards relevant to those tasks, PPE requirements, air monitoring action levels and what they mean (rotate out, evacuate), emergency signals and procedures, and decontamination procedures. It is operational safety communication, not a training class.

</details>

**Q38.** A hazardous waste site worker's HAZWOPER initial training was 40 hours (for uncontrolled hazardous waste site workers). She also received 3 days of supervised field experience. What annual training does she need to maintain her HAZWOPER qualification?

Regardless of the initial training level (24-hour or 40-hour), HAZWOPER requires **8 hours of annual refresher training** to maintain qualification. The initial 40-hour training gets you started; the annual 8-hour refresher keeps skills current and covers regulatory updates. Failing to complete the annual refresher lapses the worker's HAZWOPER qualification.

</details>

**Q39.** Which of the following operations at a Treatment, Storage, and Disposal Facility (TSDF) is covered under HAZWOPER?

**Q40.** Under HAZWOPER, decontamination procedures must be established and communicated to workers before any site entry. Which of the following is NOT a purpose of decontamination?

# BLOCK 3: Confined Spaces (Q41–Q55)

**Q41.** Which three characteristics define a confined space under OSHA 29 CFR 1910.146?

A) Enclosed, requires a key to access, and is underground
B) Large enough to enter, has limited entry/exit, and is not designed for continuous occupancy
C) Hazardous atmosphere, limited lighting, and restricted communication
D) Below-grade, requires a ladder to access, and has a single entrance

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

A confined space has ALL three: (1) large enough for an employee to bodily enter and perform work, (2) has limited or restricted means of entry or exit, and (3) is not designed for continuous employee occupancy. These three together make a "confined space" — even before it's permit-required, these three must be present.

</details>

**Q42.** What makes a confined space "permit-required" (PRCS)?

A) It is underground and requires a ladder to enter
B) It is in a HAZWOPER-regulated facility

<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

**Q43.** What is the OSHA-defined oxygen-deficient atmosphere?

A) Below 18% oxygen
B) Below 19.5% oxygen
C) Below 20.9% oxygen (normal atmospheric level)
D) Below 16% oxygen

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

OSHA defines oxygen-deficient as below **19.5%** oxygen. Normal atmospheric oxygen is 20.9%. Between 19.5% and 20.9%, the atmosphere is technically "normal" by OSHA definition. Below 19.5%: hazardous, requires respiratory protection. Below 16%: cognitive impairment begins. Below 6%: death can occur within minutes. The 19.5% threshold is the regulatory trigger.

</details>

**Q44.** What is the OSHA-defined oxygen-enriched atmosphere?

A) Above 20.9%
B) Above 22%
C) Above 23.5%
D) Above 25%

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Oxygen-enriched is defined as **above 23.5%**. In an oxygen-enriched atmosphere, materials that don't normally burn may ignite, and fires burn much more intensely. Oxygen-enriched atmospheres are as dangerous as oxygen-deficient ones — both trigger the PRCS classification.

</details>

**Q45.** Why must atmospheric testing be performed in the order of: oxygen first, then LEL, then toxics?

B) Oxygen is tested first because oxygen-deficient atmospheres can cause inaccurate LEL/combustible gas readings on many instruments, and knowing O₂ first ensures subsequent readings are reliable

**Answer: B**

Many catalytic bead combustible gas sensors require oxygen to generate an accurate reading — in oxygen-deficient atmospheres, they may read falsely low (showing 0% LEL when the space actually contains explosive gases). Testing O₂ first catches this situation. If O₂ is deficient, the worker knows the LEL reading may be unreliable. Additionally, if O₂ is below 19.5%, entry is already prohibited without respiratory protection — no need to risk entering to test for other things.

**Q56.** Which PPE level uses a fully encapsulating vapor-tight suit with the SCBA worn inside the suit?

A) Level B
B) Level C
C) Level D
D) Level A

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: D**

Level A is the fully encapsulating, vapor-tight suit with SCBA worn inside the suit — providing maximum protection against gases, vapors, and liquids. The suit itself forms the outer vapor barrier. Level B puts the SCBA outside the suit. Levels C and D don't use SCBA at all. Memory: A = Astronaut suit (totally sealed, SCBA inside).

</details>

---

**Q57.** Which control in the hierarchy of controls provides the MOST effective protection?

A) PPE
B) Administrative controls
C) Elimination
D) Engineering controls

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

Elimination — removing the hazard from the workplace entirely — is at the top of the hierarchy and is the most effective control because once the hazard is gone, it cannot cause harm under any circumstances. Engineering controls (third tier) are highly effective but don't eliminate the hazard — they contain or redirect it. PPE (fifth tier) is the least effective because it depends entirely on the worker wearing it correctly every time.

</details>

---

**Q58.** A ventilation system installed over a solvent cleaning tank that captures and exhausts solvent vapors before they reach the worker's breathing zone is an example of which control type?

A) Substitution
B) Engineering control
C) Administrative control
D) PPE

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Local exhaust ventilation (LEV) is an **engineering control** — it physically modifies the work environment to capture contaminants at the source before they reach the worker. Engineering controls work regardless of worker behavior or compliance. This distinguishes them from administrative controls (require worker/supervisor action) and PPE (requires worker compliance).

</details>

**Q59.** The LOTO standard requires that each employee working on a machine apply their own personal lock. A supervisor wants to apply a single company lock on behalf of five workers doing maintenance on the same machine. Is this acceptable?

A) Yes — one authorized lock is sufficient if a supervisor applies it
B) No — each authorized employee must apply their own individual lock; no single person can apply a lock on behalf of others
C) Yes — if all five workers sign a LOTO log
D) Yes — if workers are at the same company and trained together

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Individual locks are non-negotiable. Each worker who is exposed to hazardous energy must apply their own personal lock to each energy isolating device. The reason: only the individual worker knows when they are clear of the machine. A supervisor who removes their own lock doesn't know whether all workers are out — only each worker knows that about themselves. Group LOTO hasp devices allow multiple personal locks on a single energy isolating device.

</details>

**Q60.** The LOTO standard allows tagout instead of lockout when:

A) The employer prefers tags because they are faster to apply
B) The energy isolating device is physically incapable of being locked out
C) The worker has received tagout-specific training
D) Only a single worker is doing the maintenance

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Tagout is only acceptable when the energy isolating device cannot physically be locked (e.g., some older equipment designs without lockable switches). Even then, OSHA requires additional protection measures to provide the equivalent level of safety as lockout. Tagout alone is LESS safe than lockout — tags can be accidentally removed, ignored, or misunderstood. Employers who can add lockout capability to existing equipment are required to do so.

**Q61.** After applying lockout devices and releasing stored energy, a maintenance worker must perform which critical final verification step?

A) Call the supervisor for a sign-off
B) Wait 5 minutes for any residual energy to dissipate
C) Attempt to start the machine using its normal controls to verify that it cannot be energized (verify zero energy state)
D) Place a yellow safety cone in front of the machine

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

The "try to start" verification — attempting to operate the machine with its normal controls after lockout is applied — is the critical final step that confirms the lockout was applied to the correct energy source and that stored energy has been fully released. Many LOTO fatalities have occurred because workers locked out the wrong breaker or didn't fully release hydraulic pressure. The try-to-start step catches these errors before they cause injury.

</details>

**Q62.** Before being required to wear a respirator at work, an employee must first receive:

**Q63.** A half-face Air-Purifying Respirator (APR) has an Assigned Protection Factor (APF) of 10. If the PEL for a contaminant is 2 ppm, what is the Maximum Use Concentration (MUC) for a half-face APR?

C) 20 ppm
D) 100 ppm

MUC = APF × PEL = 10 × 2 ppm = **20 ppm**. If the measured contaminant concentration is at or below 20 ppm, a half-face APR is adequate. If the concentration exceeds 20 ppm, a respirator with a higher APF (full-face APR = APF 50, PAPR = APF 1,000, SCBA = APF 10,000) would be required.

**Q85.** Under OSHA's Management of Change (MOC) element of PSM, which of the following triggers the MOC procedure?

**Answer: B**

**Q86.** An industrial hygienist is called to investigate a worker complaint about a chemical smell in an office area above a chemical storage room. Which is the FIRST step in the industrial hygiene approach?

A) Sample the air immediately
B) Review the SDS for chemicals stored below and evaluate whether the reported odor is consistent with the stored chemicals — anticipating and recognizing the potential hazard before sampling
C) Issue respirators to all office workers immediately
D) Contact OSHA to report the potential exposure

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The industrial hygiene process starts with ANTICIPATE and RECOGNIZE — before sampling begins, the hygienist should review what chemicals are stored below, what their vapor pressures are, what concentrations are possible, and what the odor thresholds are. This information guides the sampling strategy: what instruments to use, what chemicals to test for, where to sample. Randomly sampling without knowing what to look for is inefficient and may miss the actual contaminant.

</details>

**Q87.** Three workers die in a grain storage silo over a 20-minute period. The first enters and collapses. The second enters to help and collapses. The third enters without PPE to help both. All three die. Which OSHA standards were most directly violated in this sequence of events?

A) HAZWOPER only — it was an emergency response situation
B) PSM — grain storage is a covered process
C) Permit-Required Confined Spaces (1910.146) — the space should have been atmospheric-tested, permitted, and no one should have entered without SCBA as a non-entry rescue was required; the attendant (if present) should not have allowed additional entry
D) Bloodborne Pathogens — body fluid contact during rescue

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: C**

This is a textbook confined space fatality cascade — and one of the most common patterns. The grain silo is a permit-required confined space with atmospheric hazard (O₂ deficiency from grain decomposition). No atmospheric testing was conducted. No permit was issued. No attendant was stationed outside. No retrieval system was in place. Each subsequent person who entered to "rescue" the previous victim became a victim themselves because the atmospheric hazard was unknown and no respiratory protection was used. The Confined Space standard (1910.146) was designed specifically to prevent this exact scenario.

</details>

**Q88.** A maintenance worker is replacing a pump on a chemical process line. The line contains a toxic liquid under 150 psi pressure. Before starting work, the worker closes the upstream isolation valve but does not open a drain valve to verify the line is depressurized and does not lock out the valve. When he opens the pump flange, toxic liquid sprays under pressure, causing severe chemical burns. Which control failure is the PRIMARY cause?

A) Failure to wear Level A PPE during pump maintenance
B) Failure to properly implement LOTO procedures — specifically failing to verify zero energy state (residual hydraulic pressure was not released/verified)
C) Failure to consult the SDS before beginning work
D) Failure to notify OSHA before beginning confined space entry

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The primary failure was LOTO — specifically: (1) no lock applied to the isolation valve (so the valve could be inadvertently opened), and (2) stored energy not verified as released (the line pressure was not verified at zero by opening a drain/vent valve). Simply closing a valve without locking it out is not LOTO compliance. The LOTO standard requires both isolation AND verification of zero energy state. The toxic spray was the consequence of stored hydraulic energy (pressurized liquid) not being properly released.

</details>

**Q89.** An employer conducts initial air monitoring at a new job site and finds that chemical concentrations are consistently below detection limits for all contaminants. Workers still complain of headaches and fatigue. What additional monitoring approach would most directly evaluate whether the workers' symptoms have a work-related cause?

A) Repeat air sampling at a different time of day
B) Conduct biological monitoring to assess body burden from all routes of exposure, not just inhalation
C) Issue Level A PPE until the cause is identified
D) Contact OSHA and request an inspection

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Biological monitoring evaluates the total body burden from all exposure routes — inhalation, dermal absorption, and ingestion. Air sampling only captures inhalation exposure. If workers are absorbing chemicals through the skin or inadvertently ingesting them (hand-to-mouth), air sampling would be below detection limits while biological markers could show significant internal exposure. Biological monitoring completes the picture that air sampling alone cannot provide.

**Q90.** A chemical plant PSM audit reveals that the Process Hazard Analysis (PHA) for the chlorine process was last conducted 7 years ago and has never been updated to reflect equipment changes made 3 years ago. What violations exist?

A) No violation — PHAs are required only once at startup
B) Two violations: (1) PHA must be revalidated at least every 5 years (the 7-year gap violates this), and (2) Management of Change was likely violated when equipment changed without updating the PHA
C) Only one violation — the 5-year PHA revalidation requirement was missed
D) Violation of EPCRA only — PSM does not require regular PHA updates

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

Two independent PSM violations exist: (1) PSM requires PHA revalidation at least **every 5 years** — at 7 years, this is overdue. (2) When equipment changed 3 years ago, Management of Change (MOC) required that the change be reviewed, that existing PHAs be updated to reflect the change, and that affected workers be informed. Failing to update the PHA for known changes is an MOC violation. Both violations put workers at risk from unanalyzed hazards.

</details>

**Q91.** A worker is exposed to methyl isocyanate (the Bhopal chemical) at a facility in the US where it is used in pesticide manufacturing. The worker's employer says "there's no specific OSHA standard for MIC exposure, so we're not required to monitor or protect workers." Is this correct?

A) Yes — without a specific standard, no OSHA requirements apply
B) No — the General Duty Clause requires protection from recognized hazards that could cause serious harm, regardless of whether a specific standard exists; MIC is one of the most toxic industrial chemicals known
C) Correct — only EPCRA Section 302 applies (MIC has a 500-lb TPQ)
D) Yes — but only EPA has jurisdiction since MIC causes environmental harm

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

The General Duty Clause (Section 5(a)(1)) fills exactly this gap. MIC's extreme toxicity is documented in scientific literature and was globally recognized after Bhopal. The hazard is "recognized," it clearly can cause death or serious injury, and engineering controls (enclosed systems, atmospheric monitoring, emergency shutdown) are feasible. The employer cannot use the absence of a specific standard as an excuse to do nothing. The GDC was designed precisely for situations like this.

</details>

**Q96.** A HAZWOPER-trained incident commander is directing emergency response to a chlorine gas release at an industrial facility. The IC orders evacuation of a residential area within 1,000 feet. **Q97.** Under PSM, when must a Pre-Startup Safety Review (PSSR) be conducted?

**Q98.** A worker is exposed to a chemical 10 hours per day instead of the standard 8-hour shift. The standard TLV-TWA is 50 ppm (8-hour). Using the brief and Scala model, which of the following correctly describes the adjusted exposure limit?

A) The adjusted limit is 50 ppm — TLV-TWA doesn't change for extended shifts
B) The adjusted limit must be LOWER than 50 ppm because the worker receives more cumulative dose and has less recovery time between shifts
C) The adjusted limit is 50 ppm × 10/8 = 62.5 ppm (proportionally higher for longer shift)
D) No adjustment is needed as long as the 10-hour TWA is below 50 ppm

<details>
<summary>▶ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer: B**

For extended work shifts, TLV-TWAs must be adjusted DOWNWARD — not upward. The Brief and Scala pharmacokinetic model generates correction factors that reduce the allowable exposure concentration for extended shifts. A worker on a 10-hour shift receives more chemical per day than an 8-hour worker at the same concentration AND has less time for biological recovery before the next shift. Simply comparing the 10-hour TWA to the 8-hour TLV is inadequate — the limit itself must be reduced.

</details>
