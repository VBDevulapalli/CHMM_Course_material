# PART 1: THE ORIGIN STORY — WHY OSHA EXISTS

---

## The Triangle Shirtwaist Fire: The Death That Changed Everything

On March 25, 1911, a fire broke out on the upper floors of the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory in New York City. The building employed hundreds of young women — many of them immigrants — who worked 12-hour shifts sewing garments.

When the fire started, workers discovered:

- The stairwell doors were locked from the outside to prevent theft and unauthorized breaks
- There was no fire escape that could handle the load
- The fire department's ladders only reached the sixth floor — the fire was on the 8th, 9th, and 10th floors

**146 workers died in less than 15 minutes.** Many jumped from windows rather than burn. Bodies were stacked on the sidewalk. The owners were acquitted of manslaughter.

Congress did almost nothing at the federal level for nearly 60 more years. An estimated 14,000 American workers died on the job every year by the late 1960s. Hundreds of thousands more were seriously injured.

---

## The OSH Act of 1970: Finally, a Federal Law

Between 1911 and 1970, workplace safety was largely left to states — and most states did nothing meaningful.

On December 29, 1970, President Nixon signed the **Occupational Safety and Health Act** into law. It became effective **April 28, 1971.**

April 28 is now designated **Workers Memorial Day** — the day OSHA took effect.

The Act created three agencies:

- **OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration):** writes and enforces rules, in the Department of Labor
- **NIOSH (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health):** does research, sets RELs, in CDC/HHS
- **OSHRC (Occupational Safety and Health Review Commission):** independent body that hears appeals of OSHA citations

### The Most Important Distinction

OSHA **enforces.** NIOSH **researches.**

If an OSHA inspector cites you, that's OSHA's job. If NIOSH says a PEL should be lower based on new science, that's their recommendation — OSHA still has to go through rulemaking to make it a legal requirement.

These two agencies are constantly compared on the CHMM exam.

---

## Who Does OSHA Cover?

OSHA covers:

- All private sector employers and employees in the 50 states
- Federal agencies under Executive Order 12196 (must follow OSHA standards)
- Some public sector employers in states with OSHA-approved State Plans

OSHA does NOT cover:

- Self-employed individuals with no employees (no one to protect but yourself)
- Immediate family members of farm employers (family-run agricultural operations)
- Workplaces regulated by another federal agency's safety regulations (mining = MSHA, nuclear = NRC)
- State and local government employees UNLESS the state has an OSHA-approved State Plan

---

## State Plans

29 states and territories have OSHA-approved State Plans.

These states administer their own safety programs, but they must be **at least as effective** as federal OSHA. Some states (California's Cal/OSHA, for example) are significantly more stringent than federal OSHA.

If you work in a state with a State Plan, you follow the state plan — not federal OSHA directly.

**The exam tests this:** State Plan states must be **AT LEAST as effective** — not identical — to federal OSHA.

---

## The General Duty Clause: OSHA's Catch-All

Section 5(a)(1) of the OSH Act is the General Duty Clause. It states:

> Each employer shall furnish to each of his employees employment and a place of employment which are free from recognized hazards that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm to his employees.

This is OSHA's **catch-all provision.** If no specific OSHA standard covers a particular hazard, OSHA can still cite an employer under the General Duty Clause if:

1. A hazard exists in the workplace
2. The hazard is recognized (by the employer, the industry, or common sense)
3. The hazard causes or is likely to cause serious harm or death
4. There is a feasible means to abate the hazard

### Scenario: The General Duty Clause in Action

A production facility uses a chemical that causes severe respiratory effects, but is so new that OSHA hasn't yet published a specific standard for it. Workers are developing occupational asthma.

OSHA arrives for an inspection, finds no specific standard was violated, but cites the employer under the General Duty Clause — the employer knew (or should have known) about the chemical's hazard, the hazard was recognized in scientific literature, it was causing serious harm, and engineering controls were feasible.

**There's no specific OSHA standard for it does NOT mean you're free to expose workers to it. The General Duty Clause is always there.**

# PART 2: HAZWOPER — THE MOST IMPORTANT OSHA STANDARD FOR HAZMAT WORKERS

---

## What is HAZWOPER?

HAZWOPER (Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response)

**29 CFR 1910.120** for general industry and **29 CFR 1926.65** for construction

HAZWOPER is the OSHA standard that governs workers involved in:

1. Cleanup operations at sites designated by the EPA (Superfund/CERCLA sites or state equivalents)
2. Corrective actions involving cleanup at RCRA-regulated facilities
3. Voluntary cleanup operations at uncontrolled hazardous substance sites (recognized by a government body)
4. Operations at Treatment, Storage, and Disposal Facilities (TSDFs) handling hazardous waste
5. Emergency response to incidents involving hazardous substances (anywhere they occur)

**Who is a HAZWOPER worker?**

If you work at a hazardous waste facility, respond to chemical spills, or clean up contaminated sites — HAZWOPER applies to you. The standard covers workers at every level — from the person doing manual labor to the site safety officer.

---

## The Five Training Levels Under HAZWOPER

HAZWOPER establishes five distinct training levels based on the scope of a worker's role in hazardous material operations. Each level has minimum hour requirements and specific competency topics.

---

### Level 1: First Responder Awareness (FRA)

**Minimum training:** No specific hour requirement — competency-based

**Who they are:** People who are likely to witness or discover a hazardous substance release AND are trained to initiate an emergency response by notifying authorities. They do NOT take action themselves.

**What they can do:**

- Recognize that a hazardous substance has been released
- Understand the risks involved
- Understand the role of an awareness-level responder (call for help, do not intervene)
- Initiate the emergency notification process

### Scenario

A delivery driver pulls up to a loading dock and notices white smoke coming from a chemical storage area and a strong acrid smell. He doesn't know what chemical it is. He has FRA training.

He drives away from the area immediately, calls 911, stays upwind, and gives a description of what he saw.

He does NOT go investigate. He does NOT try to find and close the valve. He does NOT enter the building.

That is exactly right. **Awareness level means recognize, retreat, report. Nothing else.**

---

### Level 2: First Responder Operations (FRO)

**Minimum training:** 8 hours

**Who they are:** Workers who respond to releases of hazardous substances for the purpose of protecting nearby people, property, or the environment from the effects of a release. They respond in a **defensive fashion** — they don't actually try to stop the release at its source.

**What they can do:**

- Understand hazards and risks associated with working in PPE
- Select and use PPE (minimum Level B or C)
- Implement basic decontamination procedures
- Understand basic chemical terminology
- Implement defensive containment measures (damming a spill, plugging a drain)

### Defensive vs. Offensive Response

FRO workers **defend the perimeter.** They put up barriers, divert contaminated runoff, evacuate people.

They do NOT go to the source of the leak to try to shut it off. That's the difference between defensive (FRO) and offensive (hazmat team operations at the next level).

---

### Level 3: Hazardous Materials Technician (HMT)

**Minimum training:** 24 hours of FRO level training + specific HMT competencies

**Who they are:** Personnel who respond to hazardous substance releases with the intent to **stop or control the release at or near the source.** This is offensive, hands-on intervention.

**What they can do:**

- Implement the emergency response plan
- Select, don, and use appropriate PPE (up to Level A)
- Perform advanced control, containment, and/or confinement operations within the capabilities of available resources
- Implement decontamination procedures
- Understand chemical, toxicological, and radiological terminology and behavior

### Scenario: The Chlorine Cylinder Valve

A chlorine cylinder at a water treatment plant develops a small leak from the valve packing.

HMT-trained technicians suit up in appropriate PPE (possibly Level B or A depending on concentration readings), approach the cylinder, and use a **chlorine emergency kit (C-kit for 150-lb cylinders)** to stop the leak by applying a cap or plug at the source.

This is **offensive action** at the point of the release — appropriate for HMT level, NOT for FRO or FRA levels.

---

### Level 4: Hazardous Materials Specialist (HMS)

**Minimum training:** 24 hours of HMT level + HMS competencies

**Who they are:** Personnel who provide support to hazardous materials technicians. They are more specialized and have deeper knowledge of specific chemical families, response techniques, and regulatory requirements.

**What they can do:**

- Everything HMT can do, with greater technical depth
- Act as the site liaison with federal, state, and local government representatives
- Understand in-depth chemistry and toxicology
- Implement decontamination procedures for specialized situations

---

### Level 5: On-Scene Incident Commander (IC)

**Minimum training:** 24 hours of FRO level + IC-specific competencies

**Who they are:** Personnel who assume control of the incident scene beyond the first-responder awareness level. They coordinate the overall emergency response.

**What they do:**

- Implement the employer's Incident Command System (ICS)
- Know and implement the local emergency response plan
- Know state and federal emergency response plans
- Understand hazards and risks of operating in PPE
- Implement the local emergency notification procedure

---

## HAZWOPER Level Quick Reference Table

| Level | Role | Hours | Action |
|-------|------|-------|--------|
| FRA | Witness, notifies | Competency-based | CALL — do not act |
| FRO | Defensive response | 8 hrs min | Contain from perimeter |
| HMT | Offensive (stops release) | 24 hrs min | Go to the source |
| HMS | Specialist support | 24 hrs min | Technical support + liaison |
| IC | Incident Commander | 24 hrs min | Command the scene |

---

## HAZWOPER Refresher Training

All HAZWOPER-trained workers (FRO and above) must receive **8 hours of annual refresher training** to maintain their certification.

The refresher covers new information, regulations, and competency maintenance.

**Exam Fact:** The refresher requirement is 8 hours annually for FRO through IC levels. There is no specific refresher requirement stated for FRA — it's competency-based.

---

## HAZWOPER Site Safety Plan (SSP)

Before any hazardous waste cleanup operation begins at an uncontrolled hazardous substance site, a **Site Safety and Health Plan (SSHP or SSP)** must be developed.

### Required elements of the SSP:

1. **Site description** (location, size, history of chemical use, known contaminants)
2. **Risk analysis** (hazard assessment based on available chemical information)
3. **Employee training** (training levels required for site workers)
4. **PPE requirements** (PPE selection for each zone and task)
5. **Site control measures** (work zones: hot, warm, cold; access/egress procedures)
6. **Medical surveillance program** (who needs monitoring, what's being monitored)
7. **Air monitoring plan** (what instruments, what triggers — action level vs. IDLH)
8. **Decontamination procedures** (step-by-step decon for each PPE level)
9. **Emergency response procedures** (what happens if someone is injured or a release occurs)
10. **Confined space entry procedures** (if confined space entry will occur)
11. **Spill containment program**
12. **Safe work practices** for hazardous tasks

### Scenario: The Pre-Entry Meeting

Workers are about to enter a contaminated warehouse site where illegal chemical dumping occurred. Before anyone puts on PPE, the Site Safety Officer (SSO) holds a mandatory tailgate/pre-entry safety briefing. She reviews the SSP:

> "Today's task is drum characterization in Zone 1. Based on air monitoring this morning, we're at Level C PPE — half-face APR with organic vapor cartridges. The action level for benzene is 1 ppm — if your monitor reads 1 ppm, we rotate out of the hot zone immediately. Decon station is set up at the hot/warm zone boundary. If anyone feels dizzy, shortness of breath, or skin irritation, that's a self-rescue signal — you come out immediately and don't wait for someone else to tell you."

Everything she said is required by HAZWOPER. The pre-entry briefing is not optional.

---

## HAZWOPER Medical Surveillance

Workers covered by HAZWOPER must be provided medical surveillance **at no cost to the employee.**

### Who gets medical surveillance:

- Workers who are or may be exposed to hazardous substances or health hazards at or above the action level (or if no action level, above the PEL) for **30 or more days per year**
- Workers who wear a respirator for **30 or more days per year**
- Workers who are exposed to unexpected or emergency releases of hazardous substances
- Members of HAZMAT teams

### What medical surveillance includes:

- Pre-employment baseline physical examination
- Annual examination during employment
- Examination upon termination or reassignment away from hazmat work
- Examination after any emergency incident involving significant exposure

### The physician's role:

- Must provide a **written opinion** on the worker's fitness to use respirators and wear PPE
- **Cannot share** the worker's specific medical diagnosis with the employer — confidentiality
- Must inform the employer of any medical condition that limits the worker's ability to do hazmat work

**Critical Point:** The employer gets the physician's **CONCLUSION** (fit for duty / not fit / fit with restrictions) — but NOT the worker's specific medical diagnosis. Medical confidentiality is preserved under HAZWOPER.

---

## Work Zones: Hot, Warm, and Cold

Every HAZWOPER site must be divided into control zones based on contamination levels and hazard proximity:

```
                      COLD ZONE
                 (Support / Command)
                   Command post,
                   staging, media

                 WARM ZONE
         (Contamination Reduction)
         Decontamination station,
              PPE on/off

              HOT ZONE
         (Exclusion Zone)
     The actual contamination
        / release
      Highest PPE level
       required here

        HOT LINE             CONTAMINATION          COMMAND
      (Hot/Warm boundary)    CONTROL LINE           POST
                          (Warm/Cold boundary)
```

### Hot Zone (Exclusion Zone)

- Area of actual or potential contamination
- Highest concentration of hazardous materials
- Requires maximum PPE level for the situation
- Entry controlled — only authorized personnel with appropriate PPE
- Also called **Exclusion Zone**

### Warm Zone (Contamination Reduction Zone)

- Buffer zone between hot and cold zones
- Location of the **decontamination line**
- PPE required, but potentially one level lower than hot zone
- Also called **Contamination Reduction Zone (CRZ)**

### Cold Zone (Support Zone)

- Clean area — no contamination expected
- Command post, staging, medical station, media
- Standard safety gear only (at minimum)
- Also called **Support Zone**

### The Critical Rule

Personnel and equipment ALWAYS move from **hot → warm → cold,** NEVER from cold → hot without full PPE and authorization.

Decontamination ALWAYS happens in the warm zone before entering the cold zone.

**If you skip decon and bring contamination into the cold zone, you've just contaminated your own command post.**

# PART 3: PERMIT-REQUIRED CONFINED SPACES (29 CFR 1910.146)
## The Standard That Exists Because Rescuers Kept Dying

---

## What is a Confined Space?

A **Confined Space** has ALL THREE of these characteristics:

1. Large enough for an employee to enter and perform work
2. Limited or restricted means of entry or exit
3. Not designed for continuous human occupancy

**Examples:** tanks, vessels, silos, storage bins, hoppers, vaults, pits, manholes, tunnels, sewers, underground utility vaults

---

## Permit-Required vs. Non-Permit Required

A confined space becomes **Permit-Required (PRCS)** if it has any of these:

### A. Contains or has a potential to contain a SERIOUS atmospheric hazard

- Toxic atmosphere
- Flammable atmosphere (≥10% of LEL)
- Oxygen-deficient atmosphere (<19.5% O₂)
- Oxygen-enriched atmosphere (>23.5% O₂)

### B. Contains a MATERIAL that could engulf an entrant

- Grain, sand, liquid — anything that can trap and bury a person

### C. Has an INTERNAL CONFIGURATION that could trap or asphyxiate

- Inwardly converging walls (funnel shape — person slides in and gets trapped)
- Floor that slopes downward to smaller cross-section

### D. Contains ANY OTHER recognized serious safety or health hazard

- Exposed live electrical components
- Mechanical hazards (rotating equipment)
- Extreme heat
- Anything that could cause serious harm or death

---

## The Most Common Killer in Confined Spaces: Oxygen-Deficient Atmosphere

Normal air = 20.9% oxygen.

If oxygen falls below 19.5%, the space is considered hazardous.

Below 16%: cognitive impairment begins.

Below 6%: death can occur within minutes.

**The insidious part:** in an oxygen-deficient atmosphere, the air can LOOK, SMELL, and FEEL completely normal. There is no sensory warning. A worker can walk into a space, take two breaths of oxygen-depleted air, and lose consciousness before they can turn around.

**This is why you ALWAYS test before entry. Always.**

---

## Atmospheric Testing Order: Matters!

When testing a confined space atmosphere, you must test in this specific order:

### 1. OXYGEN (O₂) — first

- **Normal:** 19.5% - 23.5%
- **<19.5%:** Oxygen-deficient — PRCS, may not enter without respiratory protection
- **>23.5%:** Oxygen-enriched — Severe fire risk, PRCS

### 2. FLAMMABILITY — second (LEL/LFL)

- **<10% LEL:** Generally acceptable (many programs use 10% as safe)
- **≥10% LEL:** Too dangerous to enter

**NOTE:** If O₂ is deficient, LEL readings may be INACCURATE on some instruments — This is why O₂ is tested FIRST.

### 3. TOXIC CONTAMINANTS — third

- Test for expected contaminants based on space history
- Compare to PEL, TLV, IDLH
- If IDLH: Entry only with positive pressure SCBA

---

## Scenario: The Order of Testing and Why It Matters

A maintenance technician uses a multi-gas meter to test a below-ground pump vault before entry. The readings come back:

- **O₂ = 18.2%**
- **LEL = 0%**
- **H₂S = 2 ppm**

He sees the LEL is 0% and thinks "no flammable gas — must be safe." He enters without respiratory protection.

Within 30 seconds he's on his knees.

### What happened?

Oxygen at 18.2% is BELOW the 19.5% minimum — the space is oxygen-deficient. At 18.2% O₂, cognitive function begins to degrade.

The LEL reading of 0% is actually consistent with oxygen depletion — an oxygen-depleted atmosphere suppresses many LEL readings; some instruments require sufficient oxygen to generate an accurate combustible gas reading.

He should have seen **18.2% O₂** and STOPPED. Never proceed past a deficient oxygen reading without respiratory protection.

**The order of testing exists for exactly this reason.**

---

## The Three Roles in Confined Space Entry

### 1. Authorized Entrant

The person who actually enters the confined space.

**Responsibilities:**

- Know the hazards of the space
- Use appropriate PPE
- Maintain communication with the attendant
- Alert the attendant to warning signs or symptoms
- **Self-rescue:** exit the space immediately if:
  - Attendant orders evacuation
  - Warning alarm sounds
  - Any symptoms of exposure occur
  - Prohibited condition is detected

### 2. Attendant

The person who stays OUTSIDE the space and monitors the entrants at all times.

**Responsibilities:**

- Maintain accurate count of entrants in the space
- **Remain at the entry point** — they cannot enter the space for any reason
- Maintain communication with entrants
- Monitor for prohibited conditions (atmospheric changes, equipment failure)
- Call for rescue if needed
- Order evacuation if conditions deteriorate
- **PERFORM NON-ENTRY RESCUE** using retrieval system

**The Attendant's Most Critical Rule:**

The attendant MUST NOT enter the permit space to attempt rescue — even if they hear someone calling for help. The attendant calls for rescue services. The attendant does NOT become a victim by entering without appropriate respiratory protection and backup.

**More confined space fatalities occur to would-be rescuers than to original victims.**

### 3. Entry Supervisor

The person responsible for authorizing and overseeing the entry.

**Responsibilities:**

- Know hazards of the permit space
- Verify that all required preparations and permits are in order
- Authorize entry
- Remove unauthorized persons from the permit space area
- Cancel or suspend the permit if unsafe conditions develop
- Determine that rescue services are available and means of communication are functioning

---

## Retrieval Systems: Non-Entry Rescue

HAZWOPER and 1910.146 both emphasize **non-entry rescue** as the preferred rescue method. This means:

- Every entrant in a permit space that requires personal protective equipment must have a **retrieval system** (wristlets connected to a retrieval line, and a retrieval system / mechanical winch capable of retrieving the entrant without additional entry)
- The attendant must be capable of operating the retrieval system from outside the space
- Vertical retrieval (from straight vertical entries): retrieval line must enable straight retrieval

---

## The Gut Check Scenario

A worker inside a tank stops responding over the radio. You are the attendant standing outside. Every instinct says "go in and get him."

Here is what you must do instead:

1. Try to contact the entrant immediately (call his name, use radio)
2. **Activate the retrieval system** (pull the retrieval line)
3. **Call for emergency rescue services** (911 or site emergency team)
4. **Alert the Entry Supervisor**
5. Keep the entry point clear for rescue team
6. **Do NOT enter without SCBA and backup**

You entering without proper protection doesn't save him — it adds another victim.

**This is the hardest rule in safety to follow** because every human instinct says "help your coworker." But rescuer fatalities in confined spaces are heartbreakingly common.

**The rule exists because it had to be written after too many rescuers died.**
