## PART 1 — WHY OSHA EXISTS

### The Triangle Shirtwaist Fire — Why Laws Get Written

On March 25, 1911, fire broke out at the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory in New York City. The stairwell doors were locked from the outside by management. Fire department ladders only reached the 6th floor. The fire was on the 8th, 9th, and 10th floors.

**146 workers died in less than 15 minutes.** Most were young immigrant women. Many jumped from windows rather than burn alive. The building owners were acquitted of manslaughter.

Congress did almost nothing for 60 more years. By the late 1960s, an estimated **14,000 American workers died on the job every year** — roughly 38 people every single day.

> **Real Scenario — The Factory Before OSHA:**
> A shoe factory in 1968 has no ventilation for solvent fumes. Workers developing liver damage are told "that's just how this job is." No air monitoring, no exposure limits, no required medical exams. Three workers die of liver failure over five years. Their families receive nothing. The factory continues operating unchanged. This was legal and routine before 1971. This is why OSHA exists.

---

### The OSH Act of 1970

On December 29, 1970, President Nixon signed the Occupational Safety and Health Act. It became effective **April 28, 1971 — now designated Workers Memorial Day.**

The Act created three separate agencies with deliberately separate roles:

| Agency | Role | Can It Issue Citations? |
|---|---|---|
| **OSHA** | Writes and enforces standards | YES — the only one |
| **NIOSH** | Conducts research, sets RELs, recommends standards | NO |
| **OSHRC** | Independent body that hears appeals of OSHA citations | NO — it rules on appeals |

> **Exam Critical — The Most Common Trick Question:**
> "NIOSH determines that a chemical causes cancer and recommends a new exposure limit. Who enforces this recommendation?" Answer: Nobody yet. NIOSH has no enforcement power. They recommend. OSHA must then go through rulemaking to adopt it as a standard. Until OSHA acts, the recommendation has no legal force.

---

### Who OSHA Covers and Who It Doesn't

**OSHA Covers:**
- All private sector employers and their employees
- Federal government agencies (under Executive Order 12196)
- Public sector in states with approved State Plans

**OSHA Does NOT Cover:**
- Self-employed individuals with no employees — no workers to protect
- Immediate family members on a farm with no other employees
- Workplaces regulated by a separate federal agency with their own safety rules:
  - Mining → MSHA (Mine Safety and Health Administration)
  - Nuclear → NRC (Nuclear Regulatory Commission)
  - Aviation → FAA

> **Scenario — Is This Worker Covered?**
> A solo plumber owns her own business, has no employees, and gets injured while working in a client's home. Is OSHA involved? No. She is self-employed with no employees — OSHA does not cover her. If she had even one employee, OSHA would cover that employee (but still not her as the employer-only situation).

---

### State Plans

29 states and territories run OSHA-approved State Plans. Key rules:

- State Plans must be **"at least as effective"** as federal OSHA
- States **may be MORE stringent** than federal OSHA — and many are
- In a State Plan state, workers follow the **state plan**, not federal OSHA directly
- California (Cal/OSHA), Michigan, Washington State are examples with stricter standards than federal

> **Scenario:**
> A new federal OSHA standard requires safety guards on grinding wheels. California's Cal/OSHA already had a stricter standard with additional requirements. Do California employers follow the federal standard or Cal/OSHA? They follow Cal/OSHA — it is more stringent and that is permitted. Federal OSHA does not displace a stricter state rule.

---

### The General Duty Clause — Section 5(a)(1)

**The exact language:**
> "Each employer shall furnish to each of his employees employment and a place of employment which are free from recognized hazards that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm."

This is OSHA's **catch-all provision**. When no specific standard covers a hazard, this clause applies.

**All four elements must exist to cite it:**

| # | Element | Key Word |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | A hazard exists in the workplace | Exists |
| 2 | The hazard is recognized — by the employer, the industry, or common sense | Recognized |
| 3 | The hazard causes or is likely to cause **serious harm or death** | Serious |
| 4 | There is a **feasible** means to abate the hazard | Feasible |

> **Scenario A — Classic GDC Application:**
> A warehouse uses a new industrial degreaser with no OSHA-specific standard. The SDS from the manufacturer warns of severe neurotoxicity. Workers begin reporting dizziness and memory problems. OSHA can cite under the GDC: hazard exists (confirmed), it is recognized (manufacturer's own SDS documents it), it is causing harm (documented neurological symptoms), and ventilation is a feasible fix. "There's no standard for it" is NOT a defense.

> **Scenario B — When GDC Does NOT Apply:**
> An employer eliminates all four elements but a freak accident still occurs — a ceiling tile falls and injures a worker. OSHA cannot use the GDC because the hazard was not "recognized" in advance and there was no feasible pre-existing abatement. The GDC requires a recognized, controllable hazard — not a completely unpredictable freak event.

## PART 2 — HAZWOPER: 29 CFR 1910.120

### What Is HAZWOPER and Who It Covers

**HAZWOPER = Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response**

Applies to:
1. Cleanup at CERCLA/Superfund sites
2. Corrective actions at RCRA-regulated facilities
3. Voluntary cleanup at uncontrolled hazardous substance sites
4. Operations at Treatment, Storage, and Disposal (TSD) Facilities
5. **Emergency response to hazardous substance releases anywhere**

Regulation: **29 CFR 1910.120** (general industry) and **29 CFR 1926.65** (construction)

---

### The Five HAZWOPER Training Levels

#### Level 1 — First Responder Awareness (FRA)

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Training Hours | Competency-based — no specific hour requirement |
| Role | Witness and notify — nothing more |
| Action | Recognize, Retreat, Report |
| PPE | None required — they leave the area |

> **Scenario:**
> A delivery driver pulls into a chemical plant and sees smoke billowing from a storage shed with a skull-and-crossbones sign. He drives away from the smoke, goes upwind, calls 911, and tells the dispatcher what he saw and where. He does not investigate, does not open the shed door, does not try to close a valve. That is perfect First Responder Awareness behavior. His job ends when he makes the call.

---

#### Level 2 — First Responder Operations (FRO)

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Training Hours | **8 hours minimum** |
| Role | Defensive response — protect people and property from a safe distance |
| Action | Contain spills from perimeter, dam runoff, evacuate, set up barriers |
| PPE | Appropriate for defensive distance |

The critical word is **defensive**. FRO does not go to the source. They work the perimeter.

> **Scenario:**
> A tanker truck overturns on a highway and begins leaking liquid. FRO-trained firefighters arrive and set up a perimeter, divert traffic, tell bystanders to evacuate upwind, and use sand berms to prevent the liquid from reaching a storm drain. They do NOT approach the tanker to identify the material or plug the leak. That action requires HMT level. FRO contains the consequences from the outside.

---

#### Level 3 — Hazardous Materials Technician (HMT)

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Training Hours | **24 hours at FRO level** |
| Role | Offensive response — stops or controls the release at the source |
| Action | Close valves, plug leaks, use emergency response kits at the leak point |
| PPE | Up to Level A |

The critical word is **offensive**. HMT goes to the problem and fixes it.

> **Scenario:**
> A chlorine cylinder at a water treatment plant develops a pinhole leak from a corroded valve fitting. HMT technicians don Level B PPE (SCBA + splash suit), approach the cylinder, and apply a C-kit — a chlorine emergency repair kit — directly to the leaking valve to stop the release at the source. This is classic HMT work: approach the hazard, intervene at the source, stop the release. An FRO would only be working the perimeter, not touching the cylinder.

---

#### Level 4 — Hazardous Materials Specialist (HMS)

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Training Hours | 24 hours at HMT level |
| Role | Technical support for HMTs; government agency liaison |
| Action | Deeper chemistry/toxicology knowledge, interprets sampling data, advises on decisions |
| Special Role | Acts as on-scene liaison with EPA, state agencies, local government |

> **Scenario:**
> A railroad car is leaking an unknown liquid near a river. The HMT team is on-scene. The HMS specialist runs chemical identification tests, reviews the shipping papers and ERG, consults with the railroad's emergency response team by phone, and communicates hazard data and protection zone recommendations to the Incident Commander. She is not suiting up to plug the leak — the HMTs do that. She is the technical brain behind the operation.

---

#### Level 5 — On-Scene Incident Commander (IC)

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Training Hours | 24 hours at FRO level |
| Role | Commands the entire incident scene |
| Action | Implements Incident Command System (ICS), coordinates all responders, makes strategic decisions |

> **Scenario:**
> A chemical plant fire involves multiple hazardous materials. The IC establishes command post in the Cold Zone, activates the Incident Command System, assigns sector commanders for operations, logistics, and safety, coordinates with fire, police, and EMS, and makes the decision to shift from defensive to offensive operations when conditions allow. He does not go to the hot zone. He manages the response from a position where he can see the big picture.

---

### HAZWOPER Level Summary Table

| Level | Role Summary | Hours | Key Action Word |
|---|---|---|---|
| FRA | Witness | Competency-based | **Report** |
| FRO | Defensive perimeter | 8 minimum | **Contain** |
| HMT | Offensive — fix the problem | 24 minimum | **Stop** |
| HMS | Technical expert + liaison | 24 minimum | **Advise** |
| IC | Commands the scene | 24 minimum | **Command** |

**Annual refresher: 8 hours for ALL levels FRO through IC**

---

### HAZWOPER Site Safety Plan

Required before operations begin at any uncontrolled hazardous substance site. Non-optional.

Required elements include:
- Site description and chemical use history
- Risk and hazard assessment
- Employee training requirements
- PPE requirements by zone and task
- Site control — work zone layout
- Medical surveillance program
- Air monitoring plan with action levels
- Decontamination procedures
- Emergency response procedures
- Confined space procedures if applicable
- Spill containment program

> **Scenario — Why the Site Safety Plan Exists:**
> A Superfund cleanup crew arrives at an old industrial site. Without a Site Safety Plan, workers might pick up the first shovel and start digging — not knowing there are buried solvent drums, that benzene concentrations in soil vapor can exceed 500 ppm at this site, that there is a drainage channel 20 feet from the excavation that leads directly to a wetland, and that the local volunteer fire department is the backup emergency response (which means a 25-minute response time). The Site Safety Plan forces all of this to be documented, understood, and planned for before anyone puts a shovel in the ground.

---

### HAZWOPER Medical Surveillance

Provided **at no cost to the employee** for all of the following:

- Workers exposed at or above the action level for **30 or more days per year**
- Workers wearing respirators for **30 or more days per year**
- Workers exposed to emergency releases of hazardous substances
- All HAZMAT team members

**Exam sequence:** Baseline exam → Annual exam → Post-emergency exam → Exit exam at end of employment

**Critical rule — Medical Confidentiality:**
The physician shares with the employer: fit for duty / not fit for duty / fit with restrictions.
The employer does **NOT** receive the diagnosis, medical history, or lab results. The doctor communicates findings — not details.

> **Scenario:**
> A HAZMAT technician's annual medical exam reveals early-stage kidney disease. The physician determines she is still fit for duty but recommends avoiding certain nephrotoxic chemicals. The doctor tells the employer: "Fit for duty with restriction — avoid direct exposure to halogenated solvents." The employer never learns she has kidney disease. Medical details stay with the doctor. The employer gets only what they need to protect her.

---

### HAZWOPER Work Zones

| Zone | Also Called | What Happens Here | Who Can Enter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hot Zone | Exclusion Zone | Active contamination present | Trained workers in max PPE only |
| Warm Zone | Contamination Reduction Zone (CRZ) | Decontamination station | Decon team in appropriate PPE |
| Cold Zone | Support Zone | Command post, staging, clean equipment | Support personnel, standard safety gear |

**Golden Rule: Always move Hot → Warm → Cold. Decontamination always happens in the Warm Zone. Never skip decon.**

> **Scenario — What Happens When You Skip Decon:**
> An HMT technician exits the Hot Zone and walks straight through to the command post in the Cold Zone because "it was just a quick job." His suit has a thin film of chlorinated solvent on the arms. He sets his suit down on a table in the command post. Three other workers touch the table and later report skin irritation. The contaminant is now in the clean zone. The command post must now be treated as contaminated. This is how one shortcut turns a contained incident into a wide contamination event.

## PART 3 — PERMIT-REQUIRED CONFINED SPACES (29 CFR 1910.146)

### What Is a Confined Space?

All three characteristics must be present — ALL three, no exceptions:

1. **Large enough** for an employee to bodily enter and perform work
2. **Limited or restricted** means of entry or exit
3. **Not designed** for continuous human occupancy

Examples: tanks, vessels, silos, manholes, tunnels, sewers, utility vaults, pits, reactor vessels, grain bins

---

### What Makes a Space Permit-Required?

A confined space becomes **Permit-Required** if it has ANY of these four hazard categories:

**A. Serious Atmospheric Hazard**

| Condition | Threshold | Hazard |
|---|---|---|
| Oxygen deficiency | Below 19.5% O₂ | Unconsciousness, death |
| Oxygen enrichment | Above 23.5% O₂ | Extreme fire/explosion risk |
| Flammable atmosphere | At or above 10% LEL | Explosion risk |
| Toxic atmosphere | At or above IDLH | Acute poisoning |

**B. Engulfment Hazard** — Material that can trap and bury a worker: grain, sand, liquid

**C. Internal Configuration Hazard** — Inwardly converging walls or floor that slopes to a smaller cross-section, potentially trapping a worker

**D. Any Other Recognized Serious Hazard** — Exposed energized electrical, rotating machinery, extreme heat

> **Scenario — Normal Air That Kills:**
> A maintenance tech needs to check a pump in an underground vault. He opens the hatch and peers inside. The air looks clear. No smell. No visible fumes. He descends the ladder. By the third rung his vision tunnels. He grabs the ladder and struggles up — barely making it out. The vault had accumulated carbon dioxide from nearby decomposing organic material in the soil. CO₂ is colorless, odorless, heavier than air, and had displaced oxygen to approximately 16%. You cannot smell oxygen deficiency. The only protection is testing before entry.

---

### Atmospheric Testing — The Mandatory Order

Test in this sequence every time, no exceptions:

**1. OXYGEN FIRST**
- Normal: 19.5% to 23.5%
- Below 19.5%: deficient — entry requires respiratory protection or continuous forced-air ventilation
- Above 23.5%: enriched — severe fire and explosion risk, it is a PRCS

**2. FLAMMABILITY (LEL) SECOND**
- Below 10% LEL: generally acceptable for entry
- At or above 10% LEL: do not enter

**3. TOXICS THIRD**
- Compare to PEL, TLV, and IDLH for each substance present

**Why oxygen must be tested first:**
Most catalytic bead LEL sensors require oxygen to work. In an oxygen-deficient atmosphere, the sensor may show 0% LEL — meaning "no flammable gas" — when in reality it simply does not have enough oxygen to generate an accurate reading. An 0% LEL reading in an oxygen-deficient space is meaningless. Test O₂ first so you know whether to trust the LEL reading.

> **Scenario — The Fatal Order:**
> A technician tests a utility vault before entry. The meter reads: LEL = 0%, H₂S = 2 ppm, O₂ = 18.2%. He sees the 0% LEL and 2 ppm H₂S (which is below the PEL of 20 ppm) and decides it is safe. He descends. Within 30 seconds he is on his knees. He barely self-rescues. The O₂ reading of 18.2% was below 19.5%. That number alone meant: do not enter without respiratory protection. The LEL reading of zero was consistent with O₂ depletion making the sensor unreliable. He read the instrument left-to-right and focused on the numbers he expected to be the problem. He should have stopped at the O₂ number.

---

### The Three Confined Space Roles

#### Authorized Entrant
- The person who enters the space
- Must self-rescue immediately if: instrument alarms, physical symptoms develop, attendant orders evacuation, or conditions deteriorate

#### Attendant
- Stays **OUTSIDE** the space at all times — no exceptions, ever
- Maintains accurate count of all entrants in the space
- Maintains continuous communication with entrants
- Monitors conditions inside and outside the space
- Calls for emergency rescue if needed
- Activates the retrieval system for non-entry rescue
- **Must NOT enter the space under any circumstances — even to rescue a collapsed worker**

#### Entry Supervisor
- Signs and authorizes the permit after verifying all preparations are complete
- Can cancel or suspend the permit if conditions change
- Ensures trained rescue services are available before entry begins

> **Scenario — The Hardest Rule in Safety:**
> A worker collapses inside a manhole. His coworker, the attendant, looks down and sees him unresponsive. Every human instinct says: climb down and help him. The rule says: do not enter. Activate the retrieval system. Call trained rescue with SCBA.
>
> Why? Statistics show that more confined space fatalities occur to would-be rescuers than to the original victims. An attendant who enters without SCBA to "save" a victim is likely to become a victim themselves within seconds. The confined space does not care about courage or intent — it just depletes whatever oxygen the rescuer brings down with them. More people die. The attendant's job is to get trained help — not to become another casualty.

---

### Entry Permit Required Contents

- Space ID and location
- Purpose of entry and tasks to be performed
- Date and authorized duration (permits expire, typically end of shift)
- Names of authorized entrants, current attendant, entry supervisor
- Hazards of the space
- Isolation and hazard control measures taken
- Acceptable entry conditions: O₂ range, LEL limit, toxic substance limits
- Initial and periodic atmospheric test results with authorized tester signature
- Communication procedures
- Required PPE and rescue equipment
- Emergency rescue procedures
- Any additional information needed for safe entry

**Retention: Cancelled or completed permits must be kept for at least 1 year.**

## PART 4 — LOCKOUT/TAGOUT (29 CFR 1910.147)

### What LOTO Controls and Why It Exists

LOTO controls hazardous energy during **servicing and maintenance** of machinery and equipment.

Types of hazardous energy covered:

| Energy Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Electrical | Motors, circuits, control systems |
| Mechanical | Stored energy in compressed springs, gravity-loaded equipment |
| Hydraulic | Pressurized cylinders, hydraulic lines |
| Pneumatic | Pressurized air lines, accumulators |
| Thermal | Steam lines, hot fluid systems |
| Chemical | Pressurized process piping |

**The core principle:** If you are about to reach into any machine that could start, press, crush, cut, or injure you in any way — every energy source must be isolated and all stored energy released before you reach in. Every time. No exceptions for "quick fixes."

> **Scenario — The Conveyor:**
> A production worker needs to clear a paper jam in a high-speed conveyor. He thinks "I'll just pull it out fast." The belt catches his glove. His hand is drawn in. The amputation takes 0.3 seconds. The machine does not hesitate. The machine does not care. It simply applies kinetic energy to whatever is in its path. LOTO exists because this scenario has repeated itself thousands of times. The machine must be powered down, locked out, stored energy released, and then the jam cleared.

---

### LOTO Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Energy Isolating Device (EID) | A mechanical device that physically prevents energy transmission — circuit breaker, disconnect switch, line valve |
| Lockout | Placing a personal padlock on an EID in the open/off position so it physically cannot be operated |
| Tagout | A warning tag placed on an EID when locking is physically impossible — LESS safe than lockout |
| Zero Energy State | All energy sources isolated AND all stored energy released (bled, discharged, blocked, drained) |

**Why lockout is always preferred over tagout:** Tags can be accidentally removed, intentionally ignored, or misread. A padlock cannot be accidentally bypassed. Tagout is only permitted when the EID physically cannot accept a lock.

---

### The Six LOTO Steps

| Step | Action | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Notify** all affected employees | They need to know the machine is out of service |
| 2 | **Identify** ALL energy sources | Missing even one source can be fatal |
| 3 | **Shut down** using normal stopping procedures | Prevents a sudden stop that could damage equipment |
| 4 | **Isolate** all energy sources | Open disconnects, close line valves, block pneumatic lines |
| 5 | **Apply** personal locks — each worker puts THEIR OWN lock on EACH EID | One lock per person per energy point |
| 6 | **Verify zero energy state** — release stored energy, then **TRY TO START** the machine | Catches mistakes before injury |

> **The Step People Skip and Die From:**
> Step 6 — verifying zero energy state by actually trying to start the machine. Workers lock the breaker they think controls the machine and skip the verify step. But if they locked the wrong breaker, or if there is a secondary electrical feed they missed, the machine can still energize when they reach in. Trying to start the machine with normal controls after lockout catches this error with no one inside it yet. The verify step has saved countless lives. The workers who skip it don't get a second chance to learn the lesson.

---

### Group LOTO

When multiple workers perform maintenance on the same machine:

- Each worker places their **own personal padlock** on each energy isolating device
- The machine cannot be energized until **every worker removes their own lock**
- No supervisor, manager, or safety officer can remove another worker's lock

> **Scenario — Why Personal Locks Are Non-Negotiable:**
> Worker A finishes his part of the job and tells the foreman "I'm done, you can pull my lock." Worker B is still inside the machine cavity. The foreman pulls Worker A's lock, restores power to test the system, and Worker B is crushed. With mandatory personal locks, this is impossible — Worker B's lock is still on the disconnect. The machine stays locked until Worker B personally removes their lock, which means Worker B has personally confirmed they are clear. No third party can make that decision for them.

## PART 5 — PPE LEVELS A THROUGH D

### Level A — Maximum Protection (The Spacesuit)

**Fully encapsulating vapor-tight chemical suit with SCBA worn INSIDE the suit.**

| When to Use | Examples |
|---|---|
| Unknown chemical with potential skin absorption | Unidentified drum with vapor release |
| IDLH atmosphere with known skin absorption hazard | High-concentration organophosphate spill |
| Vapor exposure that can penetrate skin | Certain nerve agents, hydrogen cyanide |

Components:
- Positive-pressure SCBA — inside the suit
- Fully encapsulating, vapor-tight chemical protective suit
- Chemical-resistant inner and outer gloves
- Chemical-resistant inner and outer boots
- Two-way radio inside the suit

**Memory cue: A = Astronaut. Totally sealed from the environment. SCBA is inside. Looks like a space suit.**

> **Scenario:**
> First responders arrive at a scene where an unknown white powder is vaporizing from a crashed vehicle. No identification is possible. The atmosphere is unknown, the chemical is unknown, and skin absorption potential is unknown. The precautionary principle requires Level A. You assume worst case until you know otherwise. Once the substance is identified and confirmed to not absorb through skin at these concentrations, you may be able to downgrade.

---

### Level B — Maximum Respiratory, Splash Skin Protection

**Non-encapsulating suit with SCBA worn OUTSIDE the suit.**

| When to Use | Key Condition |
|---|---|
| IDLH atmosphere present BUT skin absorption is NOT significant | Respiratory hazard with low skin penetration potential |
| Initial site entry when atmosphere unknown but skin hazard unlikely | During initial air monitoring before substance identification |

Components:
- Positive-pressure SCBA — worn outside the suit
- Non-encapsulating chemical protective suit (splash-resistant, NOT vapor-tight)
- Chemical-resistant inner and outer gloves
- Chemical-resistant boots

**Key distinction between A and B:**
Both A and B use SCBA. The difference is the suit. Level A = vapor-tight, SCBA inside. Level B = splash-resistant (not vapor-tight), SCBA outside.

> **Scenario:**
> HAZMAT team responds to a hydrochloric acid spill — a large HCl release from a failed transfer line. HCl causes severe respiratory and mucous membrane damage at high concentrations but does not significantly absorb through intact skin. The atmosphere is IDLH. The team uses Level B — SCBA for the serious respiratory hazard, splash suit for incidental liquid contact. Level A is not needed because skin absorption is not the primary concern with HCl.

---

### Level C — APR Plus Splash Protection

**Air-purifying respirator (no SCBA) plus chemical splash protection.**

ALL of these conditions must be simultaneously true for Level C:

| Condition | Required Status |
|---|---|
| Oxygen level | Within 19.5–23.5% — APRs cannot supply oxygen |
| Chemical identity | Known — there must be a cartridge that filters it |
| Concentration | Known and below the IDLH |
| Skin absorption | Not a significant hazard |

Components:
- Full-face or half-face APR, or PAPR
- Chemical-resistant coveralls (Tyvek or heavier)
- Chemical-resistant gloves and boots
- Hard hat

> **Scenario — Making the Level C Decision:**
> Air monitoring at a remediation site shows benzene at 4 ppm. PEL = 1 ppm. IDLH = 500 ppm. O₂ = 20.8% (normal). Benzene is identified, O₂ is normal, the concentration is above the PEL but far below the IDLH, organic vapor cartridges filter benzene effectively, and benzene's skin absorption at 4 ppm ambient is not the primary concern. Level C is appropriate: full-face APR with OV cartridges, Tyvek coveralls, chemical-resistant gloves and boots.
>
> Change the scenario: benzene concentration rises to 450 ppm, approaching the IDLH of 500 ppm. Level C becomes inadequate. Switch to Level B with SCBA.

---

### Level D — Work Uniform Only

**No respiratory protection. Standard work clothing.**

| When to Use | Condition Required |
|---|---|
| No respiratory hazard of any kind | Confirmed by air monitoring |
| No splash risk | Confirmed by task analysis |

Components: Steel-toed boots, safety glasses, hard hat, standard work clothing

If there is ANY respiratory concern — even mild — the minimum is Level C. Level D has zero respiratory protection.

> **Scenario:**
> A sampling technician collects soil samples from a former agricultural site. Air monitoring shows no volatile organic compounds above background. The task involves no chemical exposure, no aerosol generation, no splash risk. Level D is appropriate — safety boots, safety glasses, nitrile gloves for hygiene purposes, hard hat because heavy equipment is operating nearby.

---

### PPE Level Decision Guide

| Question | Yes → |
|---|---|
| Is atmosphere unknown or potentially IDLH with skin absorption? | **Level A** |
| IDLH atmosphere present but skin absorption not significant? | **Level B** |
| Atmosphere known, O₂ normal, below IDLH, filterable, low skin absorption? | **Level C** |
| No respiratory or splash hazard of any kind? | **Level D** |

**The Biggest Exam Trap:** Higher letter = LESS protection. Level A is maximum. Level D is minimum. This is counterintuitive. Remember: **A = Armor (maximum). D = Daily wear (minimum).**

## PART 6 — INDUSTRIAL HYGIENE

### The Four-Step Definition — Commit This to Memory

**Anticipate → Recognize → Evaluate → Control**

This appears on nearly every CHMM exam in one form or another.

| Step | What You Do | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Anticipate** | Identify hazards BEFORE work begins | Review SDS before using a new solvent; consider what could go wrong before the job starts |
| **Recognize** | Identify existing hazards through surveys, monitoring, worker interviews | Notice workers complaining of headaches near a specific process line |
| **Evaluate** | Quantify the hazard — air sampling, bio monitoring, noise dosimetry | Personal air sample shows benzene at 3 ppm vs. PEL of 1 ppm |
| **Control** | Implement the Hierarchy of Controls | Install LEV; if not feasible, provide respirators |

> **Scenario — IH in Practice:**
> A new painting operation is added to a factory. Before it starts, the industrial hygienist reviews the SDS for the paints (Anticipate). Once running, she interviews painters who report mild dizziness (Recognize). She places personal air samplers on three painters for a full shift (Evaluate). Results show toluene at 120 ppm vs. the PEL of 200 ppm — below PEL but above the action level. She recommends increased local exhaust ventilation, not just more respirators (Control — engineering before PPE).

---

### The Hierarchy of Controls — Best to Worst

| Rank | Control Type | Reliability | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Elimination** | Highest — hazard gone | Stop using the toxic chemical entirely |
| 2 | **Substitution** | High — hazard reduced | Replace toxic solvent with water-based cleaner |
| 3 | **Engineering Controls** | High — protects all workers | Local exhaust ventilation, machine guards, enclosures |
| 4 | **Administrative Controls** | Moderate — depends on humans | Job rotation, SOPs, training, scheduling |
| 5 | **PPE** | Lowest — last resort | Respirators, gloves, hearing protection |

**Why engineering controls beat administrative controls:**
Engineering controls work whether or not the worker remembers to do anything. A ventilation hood captures vapors even if the worker never received training. Administrative controls require humans to follow procedures every time correctly. People forget. People take shortcuts. People have bad days. The system should not depend on perfect human performance.

**Why PPE is last:**
PPE does not reduce the hazard. It only protects the person wearing it — and only if it is worn correctly, every time, properly maintained, and properly fitted. If a worker forgets to wear gloves for one task, the hazard is unmitigated. Engineering controls protect everyone in the area regardless of what any individual does.

> **Exam Critical:** The exam will present a scenario and ask which control is most preferred. If an engineering control is feasible, it beats administrative controls and PPE. Elimination beats everything. The answer is never "require PPE" if there is an engineering control available.

> **Scenario — Choosing the Right Control:**
> A metal grinding operation produces airborne dust containing hexavalent chromium, a carcinogen. What is the most preferred control? Options are: (A) require respirators, (B) limit time workers spend at the grinder, (C) install a local exhaust ventilation hood at the grinder, (D) eliminate grinding and use pre-formed parts. Answer: D (Elimination). If elimination is not feasible, then C (Engineering). B is Administrative. A is PPE — last resort.

### Air Sampling Methods

| Method | What It Captures | Best Used For |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal air sampling** | Air in worker's breathing zone (within 12 inches of nose/mouth) | Calculating actual worker exposure — the gold standard |
| **Area sampling** | Air at a fixed location | Mapping contamination sources, not worker exposure |
| **Grab sampling** | Instantaneous or short sample | Identifying which chemicals are present |
| **Integrated sampling** | Air pulled through sorbent media over full shift | Calculating time-weighted average (TWA) exposure |

> **Scenario — Area vs. Personal Sampling:**
> An area sampler at the center of a workshop shows 0.3 ppm benzene — below the PEL of 1 ppm. The industrial hygienist decides personal sampling is still needed. Results show a worker who leans over a specific tank for 2 hours of her shift has personal exposure of 2.1 ppm — above the PEL. The area sample looked fine because it measured the air in the room, not the air she actually breathes. Personal sampling is always more representative of actual exposure.

---

### Biological Monitoring

Measures what is actually **in the worker's body** — not just what is in the air. Catches exposure from all routes combined: inhalation + dermal absorption + incidental ingestion.

| Substance | Biological Test |
|---|---|
| Lead | Blood lead level (BLL) |
| Arsenic, mercury, cadmium | Urine levels |
| Organophosphate/carbamate pesticides | Blood cholinesterase activity |

**Cholinesterase explanation:** Organophosphate and carbamate pesticides work by blocking cholinesterase — the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine at nerve synapses. When cholinesterase is depressed, acetylcholine builds up, causing nerve overstimulation (the SLUDGE/DUMBELS toxidrome). Blood tests that show depressed cholinesterase activity indicate pesticide absorption even before symptoms appear.

## PART 7 — OSHA RECORDKEEPING AND REPORTING

### The Three OSHA Forms

| Form | Name | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **300** | Log of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses | Running record of every recordable case throughout the year |
| **300A** | Summary of Work-Related Injuries and Illnesses | Annual totals — must be posted **February 1 to April 30** |
| **301** | Injury and Illness Incident Report | Detailed information for each individual recordable case |

---

### What Makes a Case Recordable?

A work-related injury or illness is **recordable** if it results in any of:

- Death
- Days away from work
- Restricted work or job transfer
- Medical treatment **beyond first aid**
- Loss of consciousness
- Diagnosis of a significant injury/illness by a healthcare professional

**Not recordable:** First aid only — cleaning a wound, applying a bandage, OTC medication at nonprescription dose, nonprescription eye drops, splinting a suspected fracture before diagnosis.

> **Scenario — Recordable or Not?**
> A worker cuts his hand on a metal edge. He goes to the plant nurse who cleans the wound, applies antibiotic ointment, and puts on a bandage. He returns to full duty. **Not recordable** — treatment was first aid only.
>
> Same cut. The nurse can't close the wound edges with a bandage. She sends him to urgent care where a physician closes the wound with two stitches. **Recordable** — sutures (stitches) are medical treatment beyond first aid.

### Mandatory Reporting to OSHA — The Times You Must Never Miss

| Event | Report Within | How |
|---|---|---|
| Work-related fatality | **8 hours** | Phone or online |
| In-patient hospitalization | **24 hours** | Phone or online |
| Amputation | **24 hours** | Phone or online |
| Loss of an eye | **24 hours** | Phone or online |

**Memory trick: 8 for the dead. 24 for the rest.**

Small employers (10 or fewer employees) and employers in low-hazard industries are exempt from 300 Log requirements — but **everyone** must report fatalities and severe injuries regardless of size.

### OSHA Citation Types and Penalties (2024 Figures)

| Type | Definition | Max Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Other-than-Serious | Not likely to cause death or serious harm | $16,550 per violation |
| Serious | Substantial probability of death or serious harm | $16,550 per violation |
| Willful | Intentionally and knowingly committed | $165,514 per violation |
| Repeated | Same violation within 5 years of prior citation | $165,514 per violation |
| Failure to Abate | Not corrected by abatement deadline | $16,550 per day beyond deadline |

**Contest period: 15 WORKING DAYS (not calendar days) from receipt of citation.**
File a Notice of Contest with OSHRC. Missing this deadline makes the citation final and non-appealable — no court, no second chance.

> **Scenario — Willful vs. Serious:**
> An employer knows workers are exposed to lead above the PEL and doesn't implement required controls because "the controls cost too much this quarter." OSHA inspects and cites the employer. Because the employer knowingly allowed the hazardous condition, this is a **Willful** violation — up to $165,514. Compare: an employer who didn't know about the overexposure due to a lapsed air monitoring program gets cited for **Serious** — up to $16,550. The employer's knowledge and intent determines the classification.

## PART 8 — MAJOR OSHA STANDARDS

### Respiratory Protection — 29 CFR 1910.134

Required elements of a written Respiratory Protection Program:
- Respirator selection procedures
- Medical evaluation before first use
- Fit testing procedures and schedule
- Proper use, maintenance, and storage
- Training on limitations, use, and emergency procedures

**Medical evaluation required before any employee wears a respirator.** Respirators increase breathing resistance and cardiovascular workload.

**Fit testing:**
- Before first use, annually thereafter
- Also required when model or size changes, or significant weight change occurs

| Test Type | Method | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Qualitative (QLFT) | Worker detects test agent (saccharin, BitrexTM, isoamyl acetate) | Pass/Fail |
| Quantitative (QNFT) | Instruments measure actual leakage — generates a Fit Factor | Numerical score |

**Assigned Protection Factors (APF):**

| Respirator Type | APF |
|---|---|
| Half-face APR | 10 |
| Full-face APR | 50 |
| PAPR (full-face) | 1,000 |
| SCBA positive-pressure | 10,000 |

**Maximum Use Concentration (MUC) = APF × PEL**

If the actual concentration exceeds the MUC, you must upgrade to a more protective respirator.

> **Scenario — Using APF:**
> A worker will enter a space with toluene at 450 ppm. Toluene PEL = 200 ppm. Half-face APR APF = 10. MUC = 10 × 200 = 2,000 ppm. The actual concentration of 450 ppm is below the MUC of 2,000 ppm. A half-face APR with organic vapor cartridges is adequate.
>
> If toluene were at 2,500 ppm (exceeding the MUC), upgrade to full-face APR (APF 50, MUC = 10,000 ppm) or SCBA.

---

### HazCom — 29 CFR 1910.1200 (The Right to Know Standard)

Requires employees to be informed about hazardous chemicals in their workplace.

**Core components:**
- Written HazCom Program
- Safety Data Sheets (SDS) for all hazardous chemicals — must be accessible to workers at all times
- Labels on all containers with GHS-compliant information
- Training for all affected employees

**GHS SDS — 16 Standardized Sections:**

| Sections | Content |
|---|---|
| 1–3 | Identification, Hazards, Composition |
| 4–6 | First Aid, Fire Fighting, Accidental Release |
| 7–10 | Handling/Storage, Exposure Controls/PPE, Physical Properties, Reactivity |
| 11–16 | Toxicology, Ecological, Disposal, Transport, Regulatory, Other |

> **Scenario:**
> A new employee starts work in a chemical warehouse. Before she handles any chemical, her employer must provide: a written HazCom program, access to SDS for every chemical she may encounter, training on how to read labels and SDS, and information on the specific chemicals present. Not after orientation week — before she handles anything.

---

### Process Safety Management — 29 CFR 1910.119

Applies to facilities with **listed highly hazardous chemicals at or above threshold quantities (TQ).**

**Sample PSM chemicals:**

| Chemical | Threshold Quantity |
|---|---|
| Chlorine | 1,500 lbs |
| Anhydrous Ammonia | 10,000 lbs |
| Hydrogen Fluoride | 1,000 lbs |
| Methyl Isocyanate (Bhopal chemical) | 250 lbs |

**The 14 PSM Elements:**

| # | Element | Key Timing |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Employee Participation | Ongoing |
| 2 | Process Safety Information | Before PHA |
| 3 | Process Hazard Analysis | Every 5 years (revalidated) |
| 4 | Operating Procedures | Ongoing |
| 5 | Training | Initial + refresher |
| 6 | Contractors | Before work begins |
| 7 | Pre-Startup Safety Review | Before new/modified process starts |
| 8 | Mechanical Integrity | Ongoing |
| 9 | Hot Work Permit | Before each hot work task |
| 10 | Management of Change | Before any change to covered process |
| 11 | Incident Investigation | Within 48 hours of incident |
| 12 | Emergency Planning and Response | Ongoing |
| 13 | Compliance Audits | Every 3 years |
| 14 | Trade Secrets | Ongoing |

> **Scenario — Management of Change:**
> A plant engineer wants to replace a pump seal material with a different polymer to save money. The pump handles chlorine gas at high pressure. Under PSM, this change requires a formal Management of Change review before implementation. Why? A different polymer may have different chemical compatibility, different failure modes, or different pressure ratings. The MOC process documents the change, evaluates the hazards, updates the process safety information, revises operating procedures, and retrains affected workers. Skipping MOC for "minor" changes is how catastrophic releases start.

## PART 9 — WORKER RIGHTS UNDER THE OSH ACT

### Section 11(c) — Anti-Retaliation (Whistleblower Protection)

Employers CANNOT discharge or discriminate against employees for:
- Filing an OSHA complaint
- Participating in an OSHA inspection
- Exercising any right under the OSH Act
- Refusing to work in conditions of imminent danger (under specific conditions)

**Filing deadline: 30 days** from the date of the alleged retaliation.

> **Scenario:**
> A warehouse worker reports unsafe stacking of heavy materials to OSHA. Two weeks later, her hours are cut and she is reassigned to the least desirable shift. This is textbook Section 11(c) retaliation. She has 30 days from the date of the hour cut to file a Section 11(c) complaint with OSHA. If the employer cannot prove a legitimate non-retaliatory reason for the hour cut, they face back pay, reinstatement, and potential civil penalties.

---

### Right to Refuse Dangerous Work

A worker may refuse to perform a task if ALL of these are true:

1. The worker has a reasonable, good-faith belief that a real danger of death or serious injury exists
2. A reasonable person would agree the danger is real
3. There is not enough time to eliminate the danger through normal enforcement channels
4. The employer was asked to fix the hazard and refused

The worker must remain at the worksite and be willing to perform other tasks. This is not a general "I don't feel like it" right. It is a narrow, specific protection for imminent danger situations.

## DAY 3 MASTER SUMMARY

### The 10 Big Ideas

1. **OSHA exists because workers died.** Every rule traces back to a real incident. The Triangle Shirtwaist fire, Bhopal, construction deaths — these are not history lessons. They are why the rules are the way they are.

2. **HAZWOPER training level must match scope of action.** FRA = call for help only. FRO = defensive perimeter. HMT = offensive at the source. HMS = technical expert. IC = commands the whole scene. Doing more than your level allows is dangerous and a violation.

3. **Confined space deaths are predictable and preventable.** Always test: O₂ → LEL → Toxics in that order. Never skip the permit. The attendant never enters under any circumstances. Non-entry rescue is always the first attempt.

4. **PPE is always last in the hierarchy of controls.** Engineering controls protect everyone regardless of what they remember to do. PPE only protects the wearer and only when worn correctly. If an engineering control is feasible, it is always preferred.

5. **PPE levels go backwards by letter.** Level A = maximum protection (vapor-tight, SCBA inside). Level D = work uniform, no respiratory protection. The letter gets higher as the protection gets lower.

6. **LOTO Step 6 — the verify step — is the one people skip and die from.** After locking out, try to start the machine. This catches mistakes before injury.

7. **Report fatalities within 8 hours. Everything else within 24 hours.** Hospitalization, amputation, loss of eye. 8 for the dead. 24 for the rest.

8. **Contest OSHA citations within 15 working days.** Not calendar days. Working days. File with OSHRC. Miss the deadline and the citation is final — no appeal, no exception.

9. **NIOSH does research. OSHA enforces. OSHRC hears appeals.** They are three separate agencies with no overlap in authority. NIOSH cannot cite anyone. OSHA cannot run research programs. OSHRC cannot issue standards.

10. **Industrial hygiene in four words: Anticipate, Recognize, Evaluate, Control.** In that order, every time.
