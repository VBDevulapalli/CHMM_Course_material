## PART 1 — WHY OSHA EXISTS

### The Triangle Shirtwaist Fire

On March 25, 1911, a fire broke out at the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory in New York City. Workers discovered the stairwell doors were locked from the outside. The fire department ladders only reached the sixth floor. The fire was on the 8th, 9th, and 10th floors.

**146 workers died in less than 15 minutes.** Many jumped from windows rather than burn. The owners were acquitted.

Congress did almost nothing for nearly 60 more years. An estimated 14,000 American workers died on the job every year by the late 1960s.

### The OSH Act of 1970

On December 29, 1970, President Nixon signed the Occupational Safety and Health Act. It became effective **April 28, 1971 — now designated Workers Memorial Day.**

The Act created three agencies:

- **OSHA** — writes and enforces rules, Department of Labor
- **NIOSH** — does research, sets RELs, part of CDC/HHS
- **OSHRC** — independent body that hears appeals of OSHA citations

> **Exam Critical:** OSHA enforces. NIOSH researches. NIOSH cannot cite anyone. OSHA cannot do research. Know the difference.

### Who OSHA Covers

**OSHA covers:** All private sector employers, federal agencies, some public sector in state plan states

**OSHA does NOT cover:**
- Self-employed individuals with no employees
- Immediate family members of farm employers
- Workplaces regulated by another federal agency — mining covered by MSHA, nuclear by NRC

### State Plans

29 states have OSHA-approved State Plans. These must be **at least as effective** as federal OSHA — but may be MORE stringent. In a State Plan state, workers follow the state plan, not federal OSHA directly.

### The General Duty Clause — Section 5(a)(1)

"Each employer shall furnish to each of his employees employment and a place of employment which are free from recognized hazards that are causing or are likely to cause death or serious physical harm."

OSHA's catch-all provision. Four elements required to cite it:

1. A hazard exists in the workplace
2. The hazard is recognized by the employer, industry, or common sense
3. The hazard causes or is likely to cause serious harm or death
4. There is a feasible means to abate the hazard

**Scenario — General Duty in Action:**
A facility uses a new chemical with no specific OSHA standard. Scientific literature documents severe respiratory hazard. Workers develop occupational asthma. OSHA cites under the General Duty Clause. "There is no specific standard for it" is not a defense. The GDC is always there.

## PART 2 — HAZWOPER: THE MOST IMPORTANT OSHA STANDARD FOR HAZMAT WORKERS

### What is HAZWOPER?

HAZWOPER stands for Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response.

Regulation: **29 CFR 1910.120** for general industry and **29 CFR 1926.65** for construction.

HAZWOPER covers workers involved in:

1. Cleanup operations at CERCLA/Superfund sites
2. Corrective actions at RCRA-regulated facilities
3. Voluntary cleanup at uncontrolled hazardous substance sites
4. Operations at Treatment, Storage, and Disposal Facilities
5. Emergency response to hazardous substance releases anywhere

---

### The Five Training Levels

**First Responder Awareness (FRA)**

- Minimum hours: Competency-based, no specific hour requirement
- Role: Witnesses or discovers a release and initiates the response by notifying authorities. Does NOT take action.
- The rule: Recognize, Retreat, Report. Nothing else.

> **Scenario:** A delivery driver sees white smoke from a chemical storage area. He drives away from the area immediately, calls 911, stays upwind, and describes what he saw. He does not investigate or try to close a valve. That is exactly right for FRA level. Awareness level means recognize, retreat, report — nothing more.

---

**First Responder Operations (FRO)**

- Minimum hours: **8 hours**
- Role: Responds to releases to protect nearby people and property. DEFENSIVE action only. Does not go to the source.
- Actions: Contain spills from perimeter, dam runoff, evacuate people, set up barriers

---

**Hazardous Materials Technician (HMT)**

- Minimum hours: **24 hours at the FRO level**
- Role: Responds with intent to stop or control the release at the source. This is offensive, hands-on intervention.
- Actions: Close valves, plug leaks, use emergency response kits at the point of release. Can use up to Level A PPE.

> **Scenario:** A chlorine cylinder develops a valve leak at a water treatment plant. HMT technicians suit up in appropriate PPE, approach the cylinder, and use a C-kit to stop the leak at the source. This is offensive action — appropriate for HMT level, not for FRO or FRA.

---

**Hazardous Materials Specialist (HMS)**

- Minimum hours: 24 hours at the HMT level
- Role: Provides technical support to HMTs. Acts as site liaison with federal, state, and local government agencies. Has deeper chemistry and toxicology knowledge.

---

**On-Scene Incident Commander (IC)**

- Minimum hours: 24 hours at the FRO level
- Role: Assumes control of the incident scene. Implements the Incident Command System (ICS). Coordinates the entire emergency response.

---

### HAZWOPER Level Summary Table

| Level | Role | Minimum Hours | Key Action |
|-------|------|---------------|------------|
| FRA | Witness | Competency-based | Call for help only |
| FRO | Defensive response | 8 hours | Contain from perimeter |
| HMT | Offensive | 24 hours | Stop release at source |
| HMS | Specialist support | 24 hours | Technical support and liaison |
| IC | Incident Commander | 24 hours | Command the scene |

**Annual refresher: 8 hours for ALL levels FRO through IC**

---

### HAZWOPER Site Safety Plan

Must be developed before operations begin at any uncontrolled hazardous substance site. Required elements:

- Site description and history of chemical use
- Risk analysis and hazard assessment
- Employee training requirements
- PPE requirements by zone and task
- Site control measures including work zones
- Medical surveillance program
- Air monitoring plan with action levels
- Decontamination procedures
- Emergency response procedures
- Confined space entry procedures if applicable
- Spill containment program

> **Scenario — The Pre-Entry Briefing:**
> Before anyone puts on PPE, the Site Safety Officer holds a mandatory tailgate briefing covering: today's task, chemical hazards, PPE requirements, air monitoring action levels, what those levels mean for the team, decon station location, and self-rescue signals. Everything she covers is required by HAZWOPER. This briefing is not optional and not a formality.

---

### HAZWOPER Medical Surveillance

Provided at no cost to the employee for:

- Workers exposed at or above the action level for 30 or more days per year
- Workers wearing respirators for 30 or more days per year
- Workers exposed to emergency releases of hazardous substances
- All HAZMAT team members

Sequence: Baseline exam → Annual exam → Exit exam → Post-emergency exam when significant exposure occurs

**Critical rule:** The physician shares the conclusion — fit, not fit, or fit with restrictions — with the employer. The specific medical diagnosis is NOT shared. Medical confidentiality is preserved.

---

### Work Zones

| Zone | Also Called | What Happens Here | PPE Required |
|------|-------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Hot Zone | Exclusion Zone | Actual contamination present | Maximum level |
| Warm Zone | Contamination Reduction Zone (CRZ) | Decontamination station | Intermediate level |
| Cold Zone | Support Zone | Command post, staging, clean area | Standard safety gear |

**Movement rule: Always Hot to Warm to Cold. Decontamination always happens in the Warm Zone before entering the Cold Zone. Never skip decon.**

If you skip decon and bring contamination into the cold zone, you have contaminated your own command post, medical station, and equipment. Work zones exist to prevent exactly this.

## PART 3 — PERMIT-REQUIRED CONFINED SPACES (29 CFR 1910.146)

### What is a Confined Space?

All three characteristics must be present:

1. Large enough for an employee to enter and perform work
2. Has limited or restricted means of entry or exit
3. Not designed for continuous human occupancy

Examples: tanks, vessels, silos, manholes, tunnels, sewers, vaults, pits

### What Makes a Space Permit-Required?

A confined space becomes Permit-Required if it has any of these:

**A. Serious atmospheric hazard:**
- Toxic atmosphere
- Flammable atmosphere at or above 10 percent of LEL
- Oxygen-deficient atmosphere below 19.5 percent O₂
- Oxygen-enriched atmosphere above 23.5 percent O₂

**B. Engulfment hazard:** Material that can trap and bury a worker — grain, sand, liquid

**C. Internal configuration hazard:** Inwardly converging walls or floor sloping downward to smaller cross-section

**D. Any other recognized serious safety or health hazard** — exposed electrical, rotating equipment, extreme heat

> **The Most Common Killer — Oxygen Deficiency:**
> Normal air is 20.9 percent oxygen. Below 19.5 percent is hazardous. Below 16 percent, cognitive impairment begins. Below 6 percent, death can occur within minutes. The air can look, smell, and feel completely normal. There is no sensory warning. A worker can enter, take two breaths of oxygen-depleted air, and lose consciousness before they can turn around. This is why you always test before entry. Always.

---

### Atmospheric Testing Order

Test in this order every single time:

**1. OXYGEN first**
- Normal: 19.5 percent to 23.5 percent
- Below 19.5 percent = deficient, entry prohibited without respiratory protection
- Above 23.5 percent = enriched, severe fire risk, PRCS

**2. FLAMMABILITY second**
- Below 10 percent LEL = generally acceptable
- At or above 10 percent LEL = too dangerous to enter
- Note: O₂-deficient atmospheres can cause falsely LOW LEL readings on many instruments

**3. TOXIC CONTAMINANTS third**
- Compare readings to PEL, TLV, and IDLH for each substance

**Why oxygen must be tested first:** Many catalytic bead LEL sensors require oxygen to generate an accurate reading. In an oxygen-deficient atmosphere they may read zero even when explosive gas is present. Testing O₂ first catches the deficiency before you rely on a potentially inaccurate LEL reading.

> **Scenario — The Fatal Order:**
> A maintenance technician tests a pump vault. LEL = 0 percent, H₂S = 2 ppm, O₂ = 18.2 percent. He sees the good LEL reading and enters without respiratory protection. Within 30 seconds he is on his knees. What happened? Oxygen at 18.2 percent is below the 19.5 percent minimum. He should have seen that first number and stopped immediately. The LEL reading of zero was actually consistent with oxygen depletion on that type of instrument.

---

### The Three Confined Space Roles

**Authorized Entrant**
- The person who enters the space
- Must self-rescue immediately if conditions deteriorate, alarm sounds, or symptoms occur

**Attendant**
- Stays OUTSIDE the space at all times, no exceptions
- Maintains accurate count of all entrants
- Maintains communication with entrants
- Calls for emergency rescue if needed
- Activates retrieval system for non-entry rescue
- Must NOT enter the space under any circumstances, even to rescue a collapsed worker

**Entry Supervisor**
- Authorizes and signs the entry permit after verifying all preparations are complete
- Cancels or suspends the permit if unsafe conditions develop
- Ensures rescue services are available

> **The Hardest Rule in Safety:**
> When a worker collapses inside a confined space, every human instinct says go help. The attendant must instead activate the retrieval system and call trained rescue personnel with SCBA. Rescuers entering without SCBA add victims — they do not save victims. More confined space fatalities occur to would-be rescuers than to original victims. The rule exists because too many rescuers died before it was written.

---

### What a Confined Space Entry Permit Must Include

- Space identification and location
- Purpose of entry and work to be done
- Date and authorized duration — permits expire, typically end of shift
- Names of authorized entrants, current attendant, entry supervisor
- Hazards of the permit space
- Measures used to isolate the space and eliminate or control hazards
- Acceptable entry conditions for O₂, LEL, and specific toxic contaminants
- Initial and periodic test results signed by the authorized tester
- Communication procedures between entrants and attendant
- Required PPE and equipment
- Rescue and emergency services procedure
- Any other information needed to ensure safe entry

Cancelled or completed permits must be retained for at least one year.

## PART 4 — LOCKOUT/TAGOUT (29 CFR 1910.147)

### What LOTO Controls

Hazardous energy during servicing and maintenance of machines. Types of hazardous energy include:

- Electrical — most common
- Mechanical — stored in springs or gravity
- Hydraulic pressure
- Pneumatic pressure
- Thermal energy from steam or hot fluids
- Chemical pressure in process lines

The core principle: If you are about to reach into a machine that could start, press, crush, cut, or otherwise injure you — every energy source must be isolated and stored energy released BEFORE you reach in. Every time. No exceptions for quick fixes.

> **Scenario — The Conveyor:**
> A technician needs to clear a jam in a conveyor belt. He thinks "it is just a quick fix, I will pull the material out fast while it is running." The belt grabs his glove. His hand is drawn into the mechanism. The amputation takes 0.3 seconds. LOTO exists because this scenario repeats thousands of times per year. The conveyor must be powered down, locked out, stored energy released, and then the jam cleared.

---

### LOTO Terminology

- **Energy Isolating Device (EID):** A mechanical device that physically prevents energy from reaching the equipment — circuit breaker, disconnect switch, line valve
- **Lockout:** Placing a lock on an EID in the open/off position so it cannot be operated
- **Tagout:** A warning tag placed on an EID when locking is physically impossible — LESS safe than lockout

Tagout is only acceptable when the energy isolating device cannot physically be locked. Tags can be ignored, accidentally removed, or misread. Locks cannot be accidentally bypassed.

---

### The Six LOTO Steps

**Step 1 — Notify** all affected employees that the machine is being locked out

**Step 2 — Identify** all energy sources — electrical, hydraulic, pneumatic, spring tension, gravity

**Step 3 — Shut down** the equipment using normal stopping procedures

**Step 4 — Isolate** all energy sources — open disconnects, close valves, block pneumatic lines

**Step 5 — Apply** lockout devices — each worker places their OWN personal lock on EACH energy isolating device

**Step 6 — Verify zero energy state** — release stored energy (bleed hydraulic pressure, let rotating parts coast to complete stop), then TRY TO START the machine using normal controls to confirm it cannot be energized

The verify step is the one people skip and the one people die from. If you locked the wrong breaker or did not fully release hydraulic pressure, trying to start the machine catches that error before injury occurs.

---

### Group LOTO

When multiple workers are doing maintenance on the same machine:

- Each worker applies their own personal lock to each energy isolating device
- The machine cannot be energized until ALL workers remove their individual locks
- No supervisor, manager, or authorized person can remove another worker's personal lock

Why personal locks are required: If Worker B is still inside the machine, Worker A telling the supervisor "I am done, you can remove my lock" means Worker B is now at risk. With personal locks, Worker B removes their own lock only when they personally know they are clear.

## PART 5 — PPE LEVELS A THROUGH D

### Level A — Maximum Protection

**Fully encapsulating vapor-tight suit with SCBA worn INSIDE the suit.**

When to use:
- Unknown chemical with potential skin absorption hazard
- IDLH atmosphere with vapor or skin hazard
- High concentration of chemicals that absorb through skin

What it includes:
- Positive-pressure SCBA worn inside the suit
- Fully encapsulating, vapor-tight chemical protective suit
- Chemical-resistant inner and outer gloves
- Chemical-resistant inner and outer boots
- Two-way radio communications

Memory cue: A equals Astronaut — totally sealed from the environment, SCBA inside, looks like a space suit.

---

### Level B — Maximum Respiratory, Splash Skin Protection

**Non-encapsulating suit with SCBA worn OUTSIDE the suit.**

When to use: IDLH atmosphere exists but skin absorption is NOT a major concern

What it includes:
- Positive-pressure SCBA worn outside the suit
- Non-encapsulating chemical protective suit — splash protection but not vapor-tight
- Chemical-resistant inner and outer gloves
- Chemical-resistant boots

Key distinction: Both Level A and Level B use SCBA for respiratory protection. The difference is the suit. Level A is vapor-tight with the SCBA inside. Level B is not vapor-tight and the SCBA is on the outside.

---

### Level C — Air-Purifying Respirator Plus Splash Protection

When to use — ALL of these conditions must be simultaneously true:
- Oxygen is within the normal range of 19.5 to 23.5 percent — APRs cannot supply oxygen
- The contaminant is identified and there is a cartridge that filters it
- The concentration is known and below the IDLH
- Skin absorption is not a significant hazard

What it includes:
- Full-face or half-face Air-Purifying Respirator or PAPR
- Chemical-resistant coveralls
- Chemical-resistant gloves and boots
- Hard hat

> **Scenario — Level C Field Decision:**
> Air monitoring shows benzene at 4 ppm. Benzene PEL = 1 ppm. Benzene IDLH = 500 ppm. Oxygen = 20.8 percent, which is normal. The contaminant is identified, O₂ is normal, concentration is above the PEL but far below the IDLH, and organic vapor cartridges filter benzene. Level C is appropriate — full-face APR with OV cartridges, Tyvek coveralls, chemical-resistant gloves and boots.
> If benzene were at 450 ppm approaching the IDLH of 500 ppm, Level B with SCBA would be required.

---

### Level D — Work Uniform Only

When to use: No known respiratory or skin hazard, no splash risk

What it includes: Safety boots, safety glasses, hard hat, standard work clothing

Level D has no respirator. If any respiratory protection is needed at all, the minimum is Level C.

---

### PPE Level Decision Guide

| Question | Answer | PPE Level |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Is atmosphere unknown or is O₂ deficient? | Yes | Level B minimum |
| Is skin absorption hazardous at IDLH conditions? | Yes | Level A |
| IDLH atmosphere but skin absorption not significant? | Yes | Level B |
| Atmosphere known, O₂ normal, below IDLH, filterable by APR? | Yes | Level C |
| No atmospheric or splash hazard of any kind? | Yes | Level D |

> **The Biggest Exam Trap:** Higher letter means LESS protection. Level A is maximum. Level D is minimum. This is counterintuitive. Memorize it as A equals Armor and D equals Daily wear.

## PART 6 — INDUSTRIAL HYGIENE

### The Official Four-Step Definition

**Anticipate → Recognize → Evaluate → Control**

This sequence is the definition of industrial hygiene and appears on almost every CHMM exam.

**Anticipate:** Identify potential hazards before they cause harm. Review process chemistry and SDS before work begins. Think about what could go wrong before it does.

**Recognize:** Identify existing hazards through workplace surveys, worker interviews, monitoring data review, and symptom reporting.

**Evaluate:** Quantify the hazard through air sampling, biological monitoring, noise dosimetry, radiation surveys. Compare results to PEL, TLV, REL, and IDLH.

**Control:** Implement the Hierarchy of Controls.

---

### The Hierarchy of Controls — Best to Worst

**1. ELIMINATION — Remove the hazard entirely**

Example: Stop using a toxic chemical altogether. The hazard cannot harm anyone if it no longer exists in the workplace.

**2. SUBSTITUTION — Replace with something less hazardous**

Example: Switch from a toxic solvent-based cleaner to a less toxic water-based cleaner that performs the same function.

**3. ENGINEERING CONTROLS — Physically change the work environment**

Examples: Local exhaust ventilation that captures vapors at the source, machine guards, enclosures, wet methods to control dust, noise-dampening materials, isolation rooms.

Engineering controls protect all workers in the area regardless of whether they remember to wear PPE. This is why they rank above administrative controls and PPE.

**4. ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS — Change how work is done**

Examples: Job rotation to limit individual exposure time, training programs, standard operating procedures, scheduling high-exposure tasks during low-occupancy periods, posting warning signs.

Administrative controls depend on human behavior and management systems. They are less reliable than engineering controls because they can be forgotten or bypassed.

**5. PPE — Personal Protective Equipment — Last resort**

Examples: Respirators, gloves, goggles, hearing protection, chemical-resistant clothing.

PPE does NOT eliminate or reduce the hazard. It only protects the individual wearing it, and only if it is worn correctly every time. PPE fails when not worn, when not maintained, or when not properly fitted. This is why it sits at the bottom of the hierarchy.

> **Exam Critical:** The exam will give you a scenario and ask which control approach is most preferred. The answer is never PPE if a higher-tier control is feasible. Engineering controls are almost always preferred over administrative controls and PPE.

---

### Air Sampling Methods

**Personal air sampling:** Device worn in the breathing zone, within 12 inches of nose and mouth. The gold standard. Captures actual worker exposure including movement through all tasks and locations throughout the shift.

**Area sampling:** Stationary sample at a fixed location. Maps contamination sources but does not represent what any individual worker actually breathes.

**Grab sampling:** Single instantaneous or short-duration sample. Identifies which contaminants are present but cannot be used to calculate a time-weighted average.

**Integrated sampling:** Pump pulls air through collection media such as a sorbent tube over a full work period. The analyzed result gives the TWA exposure.

---

### Biological Monitoring

Measures what is in the worker's body rather than what is in the air. Catches total body burden from all routes of exposure — inhalation, dermal absorption, and ingestion.

- Blood lead levels for lead workers
- Urine arsenic, mercury, or cadmium for workers with those exposures
- Cholinesterase activity for organophosphate and carbamate pesticide workers

These pesticides work by inhibiting cholinesterase — the enzyme that breaks down the neurotransmitter acetylcholine. Depressed cholinesterase levels indicate pesticide absorption and potential toxicity.

## PART 7 — OSHA RECORDKEEPING AND REPORTING

### The Three OSHA Forms

**Form 300 — The Log:** Running record of every recordable injury and illness throughout the year.

**Form 300A — Annual Summary:** Totals from the 300 Log. Must be posted from **February 1 to April 30** every year in a place where employee notices are posted.

**Form 301 — Incident Report:** Individual case details for each recordable incident.

### What Makes an Injury or Illness Recordable?

A work-related case is recordable if it results in:

- Death
- Days away from work
- Restricted work or transfer to another job
- Medical treatment beyond first aid
- Loss of consciousness
- Diagnosis of a significant injury or illness by a healthcare professional

**NOT recordable:** Treatment consisting only of first aid — cleaning a wound, applying a bandage, administering over-the-counter medication at nonprescription strength, using nonprescription eye drops.

---

### Mandatory Reporting to OSHA

| Event | Report Within |
|-------|--------------|
| Work-related fatality | 8 hours |
| In-patient hospitalization | 24 hours |
| Amputation | 24 hours |
| Loss of an eye | 24 hours |

Memory: 8 for the dead, 24 for the rest.

Employers with 10 or fewer employees and employers in certain low-hazard industries are exempt from the 300 Log requirement but NOT from mandatory reporting of fatalities and severe injuries.

---

### OSHA Inspection Priority Order

1. Imminent Danger — highest priority, death or serious injury expected before normal enforcement
2. Catastrophic and Fatality Investigation
3. Complaint Investigation — from current or former employee
4. Referral — from other agencies such as EPA or NIOSH
5. Planned or Programmed — random or by industry hazard profile
6. Follow-Up — verifying prior cited violations were corrected

---

## PART 8 — MAJOR OSHA STANDARDS SUMMARY

### Respiratory Protection (29 CFR 1910.134)

Required written program elements: Respirator selection procedures, medical evaluation requirements, fit testing procedures, use and maintenance, training.

Medical evaluation: Required before any employee is required to wear a respirator. Respirators increase breathing resistance, heart rate, and heat load.

Fit testing frequency: Before first use and annually thereafter. Also required when respirator model or size changes.

- Qualitative fit test: Worker detects a test agent by taste or smell. Pass or fail.
- Quantitative fit test: Instruments measure actual leakage and generate a numerical Fit Factor.

**Assigned Protection Factors:**

| Respirator Type | APF |
|----------------|-----|
| Half-face APR | 10 |
| Full-face APR | 50 |
| PAPR full-face | 1,000 |
| SCBA positive pressure | 10,000 |

Maximum Use Concentration = APF multiplied by PEL. If concentration exceeds MUC, upgrade the respirator.

---

### Process Safety Management (29 CFR 1910.119)

Applies to facilities with listed highly hazardous chemicals at or above threshold quantities.

**Sample PSM chemicals:**

| Chemical | Threshold Quantity |
|----------|--------------------|
| Chlorine | 1,500 lbs |
| Anhydrous Ammonia | 10,000 lbs |
| Hydrogen Fluoride | 1,000 lbs |
| Methyl Isocyanate | 250 lbs |

**The 14 PSM Elements:**

1. Employee Participation
2. Process Safety Information
3. Process Hazard Analysis — revalidated every 5 years
4. Operating Procedures
5. Training
6. Contractors
7. Pre-Startup Safety Review
8. Mechanical Integrity
9. Hot Work Permit
10. Management of Change
11. Incident Investigation
12. Emergency Planning and Response
13. Compliance Audits — every 3 years
14. Trade Secrets

PSM equals OSHA focus on worker safety under 29 CFR 1910.119. EPA RMP equals community protection focus under 40 CFR Part 68. Same chemicals, similar thresholds, often triggered simultaneously.

---

### Key Substance-Specific Standards

**Benzene (29 CFR 1910.1028)**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| PEL 8-hour TWA | 1 ppm |
| Short-Term Exposure Limit | 5 ppm for 15 minutes |
| Action Level | 0.5 ppm |
| IDLH | 500 ppm |

At or above the action level: medical surveillance required, monitoring every 6 months.

---

**Lead — General Industry (29 CFR 1910.1025)**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| PEL 8-hour TWA | 50 micrograms per cubic meter |
| Action Level | 30 micrograms per cubic meter |
| Blood Lead Level — Medical Removal | 50 micrograms per deciliter |
| Blood Lead Level — Return to Work | Below 40 micrograms per deciliter |

Medical Removal Protection: When BLL reaches 50, the employer removes the worker from lead exposure AND maintains pay, seniority, and benefits during the removal period.

---

**Asbestos (29 CFR 1910.1001 for general industry, 29 CFR 1926.1101 for construction)**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| PEL 8-hour TWA | 0.1 fiber per cm³ |
| Excursion Limit 30 minutes | 1.0 fiber per cm³ |
| IDLH | 5 fibers per cm³ |

Buildings constructed before 1980: Must be treated as containing asbestos-containing material unless sampled and confirmed negative.

---

**Bloodborne Pathogens (29 CFR 1910.1030)**

Applies to workers with occupational exposure to blood or OPIM.

OPIM includes: Semen, vaginal secretions, cerebrospinal fluid, amniotic fluid, any body fluid visibly contaminated with blood, unfixed human tissues, HIV and HBV cultures.

OPIM does NOT include: Feces, nasal secretions, sweat, tears, urine — unless visibly contaminated with blood.

Requirements: Written Exposure Control Plan updated annually, engineering controls such as sharps containers, PPE, Hepatitis B vaccination offered at no cost before initial assignment, post-exposure evaluation, annual training.

- Benzene: PEL 1 ppm, AL 0.5 ppm, STEL 5 ppm, IDLH 500 ppm
- Lead: PEL 50 micrograms per m³, AL 30, BLL removal at 50 micrograms per dL
- Asbestos: PEL 0.1 f/cm³, Excursion 1.0 f/cm³, IDLH 5 f/cm³

**Respirator APF Values:**
- Half-face APR = 10
- Full-face APR = 50
- PAPR full-face = 1,000
- SCBA positive pressure = 10,000
- MUC = APF multiplied by PEL

**OSHA 300A posting period:** February 1 to April 30 every year

**Industrial Hygiene definition:** Anticipate, Recognize, Evaluate, Control

**General Duty Clause four elements:** Hazard exists, hazard is recognized, hazard causes or could cause serious harm or death, feasible abatement method exists.

## DAY 3 SUMMARY — THE BIG IDEAS

1. OSHA exists because workers died. Every rule is written in blood.

2. HAZWOPER training levels match scope of action. FRA = call only. FRO = defensive perimeter. HMT = offensive at the source. HMS = technical support. IC = commands the scene.

3. Confined space fatality patterns are predictable and preventable. Test atmosphere in order. Never skip the permit. The attendant never enters. Non-entry rescue is always the first attempt.

4. PPE goes LAST in the hierarchy of controls, not first. Engineering controls protect everyone regardless of what they remember to wear. PPE only protects the individual wearing it correctly.

5. PPE levels A through D — higher letter means less protection. Level A is maximum. Level D is work clothes.

6. LOTO has six steps and the verify step is the one people skip and die from. Try to start the machine after locking it out.

7. Report fatalities within 8 hours. Hospitalizations, amputations, and eye loss within 24 hours.

8. OSHA citations must be contested within 15 working days or they become final.
